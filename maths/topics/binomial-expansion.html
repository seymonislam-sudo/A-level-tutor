Binomial Expansion

4.1 Pascal's Triangle

```

Pascal's Triangle gives the coefficients when you expand $(1 + x)^n$. Each number is the sum of the two numbers above it. Row $n$ gives the coefficients for $(1+x)^n$.

n=01
n=111
n=2121
n=31331
n=414641
n=515101051
n=61615201561

So $(1+x)^3 = 1 + 3x + 3x^2 + x^3$ and $(1+x)^4 = 1 + 4x + 6x^2 + 4x^3 + x^4$.

Observations The row for $n$ has $n+1$ terms. The coefficients are always symmetric. The first and last coefficient are always 1. The second and second-to-last are always $n$. Pascal's Triangle becomes impractical for large $n$ — that's where the formula comes in.
Worked Example 4.1a
Use Pascal's Triangle to expand $(1 + x)^5$.
1
Row $n=5$ from Pascal's Triangle: $1, 5, 10, 10, 5, 1$.
2
Apply as coefficients, with powers of $x$ increasing from $0$ to $5$: $$(1+x)^5 = 1 + 5x + 10x^2 + 10x^3 + 5x^4 + x^5$$
```

4.2 The Binomial Formula

```

For large $n$, Pascal's Triangle is slow. The binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{r}$ (read "n choose r") gives any coefficient directly:

$$\binom{n}{r} = {}^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$ $$(a + b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r$$ $$= \binom{n}{0}a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2 + \cdots + \binom{n}{n}b^n$$ $$\text{Special case: } (1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \cdots + x^n$$

The factorial notation: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$, and $0! = 1$ by convention.

Computing $\binom{n}{r}$ Quickly Write out $r$ descending factors from $n$ in the numerator, divided by $r!$. For example: $\binom{8}{3} = \dfrac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \dfrac{336}{6} = 56$. Never expand $n!$ fully — cancel as you go.
Worked Example 4.2a
Expand $(1 + x)^6$ fully using the binomial formula.
1
Compute the coefficients: $\binom{6}{0}=1,\ \binom{6}{1}=6,\ \binom{6}{2}=15,\ \binom{6}{3}=20,\ \binom{6}{4}=15,\ \binom{6}{5}=6,\ \binom{6}{6}=1$
2
$$(1+x)^6 = 1 + 6x + 15x^2 + 20x^3 + 15x^4 + 6x^5 + x^6$$
Check: coefficients match row 6 of Pascal's Triangle. ✓
Worked Example 4.2b
Expand $(1 - 2x)^4$, simplifying all coefficients.
1
Treat as $(1 + (-2x))^4$. Use $n=4$, $b = -2x$: $$\binom{4}{0}1 + \binom{4}{1}(-2x) + \binom{4}{2}(-2x)^2 + \binom{4}{3}(-2x)^3 + \binom{4}{4}(-2x)^4$$
2
$$= 1 + 4(-2x) + 6(4x^2) + 4(-8x^3) + 1(16x^4)$$
3
$$= 1 - 8x + 24x^2 - 32x^3 + 16x^4$$
The signs alternate because $b = -2x$ is negative. Always apply the power to the whole $b$ term, including the sign and coefficient.
⚠ Most Common Mistake When $b = -2x$, then $b^2 = (-2x)^2 = 4x^2$, not $-4x^2$. Students forget to square the coefficient and/or lose the negative when squaring. Write out $(-2x)^r$ explicitly before simplifying.
```

4.3 Finding Specific Terms

```

In an exam, you'll often be asked to find one specific term of an expansion — not the whole thing. The general term is your tool.

$$\text{The } (r+1)\text{th term of } (a+b)^n \text{ is:}$$ $$T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}\,a^{n-r}\,b^r$$ $$\text{The term in } x^k \text{ corresponds to } r = k \text{ when expanding } (1 + x)^n$$
Strategy Set up the general term $\binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r$, then find $r$ by matching the power of $x$ you want. Solve for $r$, then substitute back.
Worked Example 4.3a
Find the coefficient of $x^3$ in the expansion of $(1 + 2x)^7$.
1
General term: $T_{r+1} = \binom{7}{r}(1)^{7-r}(2x)^r = \binom{7}{r}2^r x^r$
2
For the $x^3$ term, set $r = 3$: $$T_4 = \binom{7}{3} \cdot 2^3 \cdot x^3 = 35 \times 8 \times x^3 = 280x^3$$
3
The coefficient of $x^3$ is $\mathbf{280}$.
Worked Example 4.3b
Find the term independent of $x$ in the expansion of $\left(2x + \dfrac{3}{x}\right)^6$.
1
General term: $T_{r+1} = \binom{6}{r}(2x)^{6-r}\!\left(\dfrac{3}{x}\right)^r = \binom{6}{r} \cdot 2^{6-r} \cdot 3^r \cdot x^{6-r} \cdot x^{-r}$
2
Combine powers of $x$: $x^{6-r-r} = x^{6-2r}$
3
For term independent of $x$: $6 - 2r = 0 \implies r = 3$
4
$T_4 = \binom{6}{3} \cdot 2^3 \cdot 3^3 = 20 \times 8 \times 27 = \mathbf{4320}$
```

4.4 Expanding $(a + bx)^n$

```

When the first term is not $1$, factor it out first — this keeps the algebra clean and reduces errors.

$$(a + bx)^n = a^n\!\left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n$$ $$\text{then expand } \left(1 + \frac{b}{a}x\right)^n \text{ using the standard formula.}$$
Worked Example 4.4a
Expand $(2 + 3x)^4$ up to and including the term in $x^2$.
1
Factor out $2^4 = 16$: $$(2 + 3x)^4 = 2^4\!\left(1 + \tfrac{3}{2}x\right)^4 = 16\left(1 + \tfrac{3}{2}x\right)^4$$
2
Expand $\left(1 + \tfrac{3}{2}x\right)^4$ up to $x^2$: $$1 + \binom{4}{1}\!\left(\tfrac{3}{2}x\right) + \binom{4}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{3}{2}x\right)^2 + \cdots$$ $$= 1 + 4 \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}x + 6 \cdot \tfrac{9}{4}x^2 + \cdots$$ $$= 1 + 6x + \tfrac{27}{2}x^2 + \cdots$$
3
Multiply through by 16: $$(2+3x)^4 = 16 + 96x + 216x^2 + \cdots$$
Worked Example 4.4b
Find the first four terms in ascending powers of $x$ of $(3 - x)^5$.
1
$(3-x)^5 = 3^5\!\left(1 - \tfrac{x}{3}\right)^5 = 243\left(1 - \tfrac{x}{3}\right)^5$
2
Expand $\left(1 - \tfrac{x}{3}\right)^5$ with $b = -\tfrac{1}{3}x$: $$1 + 5\!\left(-\tfrac{x}{3}\right) + 10\!\left(-\tfrac{x}{3}\right)^2 + 10\!\left(-\tfrac{x}{3}\right)^3 + \cdots$$ $$= 1 - \tfrac{5x}{3} + \tfrac{10x^2}{9} - \tfrac{10x^3}{27} + \cdots$$
3
Multiply by 243: $$243 - 405x + 270x^2 - 90x^3 + \cdots$$
Check: first term $= 3^5 = 243$ ✓. Last shown term: $243 \times \frac{10}{27} = 90$ ✓.
⚠ "Up to and including" vs "first n terms" "Up to and including the term in $x^3$" means stop after the $x^3$ term — even if there are more. "First four terms" means the same thing here. Always read the question carefully. Never expand beyond what's asked for.
```

4.5 Using Expansions for Approximations

```

One of the most elegant applications of the binomial expansion: substitute a small value of $x$ to get a decimal approximation of an irrational number — without a calculator.

The Method Expand $(1 + x)^n$ or $(a + bx)^n$ to a few terms. Then choose a value of $x$ (usually a simple fraction) such that the expression evaluates to the number you want to approximate. The more terms you include, the better the approximation.
Worked Example 4.5a
Use the expansion of $(1 + x)^{1/2}$ to find an approximation to $\sqrt{1.04}$ correct to 4 decimal places. (Hint: the expansion to three terms is $1 + \tfrac{1}{2}x - \tfrac{1}{8}x^2 + \cdots$)
1
We want $\sqrt{1.04} = (1 + 0.04)^{1/2}$, so substitute $x = 0.04$.
Note: the full binomial expansion extends to negative/fractional $n$ (Year 2), but we can use the given approximation here.
2
$(1 + 0.04)^{1/2} \approx 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.04) - \tfrac{1}{8}(0.04)^2$ $$= 1 + 0.02 - \tfrac{1}{8}(0.0016) = 1 + 0.02 - 0.0002 = 1.0198$$
3
$\sqrt{1.04} \approx 1.0198$ (true value: $1.01980...$) ✓
Worked Example 4.5b
$(a)$ Find the first three terms of the expansion of $(2 + 5x)^8$ in ascending powers of $x$. $(b)$ By substituting a suitable value of $x$, use your expansion to estimate $2.5^8$.
1
Part (a): $(2+5x)^8 = 2^8\!\left(1 + \tfrac{5x}{2}\right)^8 = 256\left(1 + \tfrac{5x}{2}\right)^8$ $$\approx 256\!\left[1 + 8\cdot\tfrac{5x}{2} + 28\cdot\tfrac{25x^2}{4}\right] = 256\left[1 + 20x + 175x^2\right]$$ $$= 256 + 5120x + 44800x^2 + \cdots$$
2
Part (b): We want $2.5^8 = (2 + 5x)^8$ when $5x = 0.5$, i.e. $x = 0.1$.
3
Substitute $x = 0.1$: $$256 + 5120(0.1) + 44800(0.01) = 256 + 512 + 448 = 1216$$
Exact value: $2.5^8 = 1525.87...$. The approximation is rough here because $x = 0.1$ is not especially small and we've only used three terms. More terms would improve accuracy.
Validity A binomial approximation is only valid when $|x|$ is small — typically $|x| < 1$ for the expansion to converge. The closer $x$ is to $0$, the better the approximation. This condition becomes critical in Year 2 when you encounter infinite binomial expansions.
```

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
```
Q1
Expand $(1 + x)^7$ fully using the binomial formula.
$1 + 7x + 21x^2 + 35x^3 + 35x^4 + 21x^5 + 7x^6 + x^7$
[3 marks]
Q2
Find the coefficient of $x^4$ in the expansion of $(1 - 3x)^6$.
$T_5 = \binom{6}{4}(-3x)^4 = 15 \times 81 x^4 = 1215x^4$. Coefficient is $\mathbf{1215}$.
[3 marks]
Q3
Find the first three terms in ascending powers of $x$ of $(3 + 2x)^5$.
$(3+2x)^5 = 3^5(1+\frac{2x}{3})^5 = 243\left[1 + 5\cdot\frac{2x}{3} + 10\cdot\frac{4x^2}{9} + \cdots\right] = 243 + 810x + 1080x^2 + \cdots$
[4 marks]
Q4
Evaluate $\binom{10}{3} + \binom{10}{7}$ without a calculator. What do you notice?
$\binom{10}{3} = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8}{6} = 120$. $\binom{10}{7} = \binom{10}{3} = 120$ (by symmetry: $\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}$). Sum $= 240$. The two values are equal — binomial coefficients are symmetric.
[3 marks]
Q5
Expand $(2x - 1)^4$ fully.
$\binom{4}{0}(2x)^4 + \binom{4}{1}(2x)^3(-1) + \binom{4}{2}(2x)^2(-1)^2 + \binom{4}{3}(2x)(-1)^3 + \binom{4}{4}(-1)^4$ $= 16x^4 - 32x^3 + 24x^2 - 8x + 1$
[4 marks]
```
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
```
Q6
The coefficient of $x^2$ in the expansion of $(2 + kx)^6$ is 60. Find the possible values of $k$.
$T_3 = \binom{6}{2}(2)^4(kx)^2 = 15 \times 16 \times k^2 x^2 = 240k^2 x^2$. Set equal to $60x^2$: $240k^2 = 60 \Rightarrow k^2 = \frac{1}{4} \Rightarrow k = \pm\frac{1}{2}$.
[4 marks]
Q7
Find the term independent of $x$ in the expansion of $\left(x^2 + \dfrac{2}{x}\right)^9$.
General term: $\binom{9}{r}(x^2)^{9-r}\!\left(\frac{2}{x}\right)^r = \binom{9}{r} 2^r x^{2(9-r)-r} = \binom{9}{r}2^r x^{18-3r}$. Set $18 - 3r = 0 \Rightarrow r = 6$. Term $= \binom{9}{6}2^6 = 84 \times 64 = \mathbf{5376}$.
[5 marks]
Q8
$(a)$ Find the first four terms of the expansion of $(1 + 2x)^{10}$ in ascending powers of $x$. $(b)$ Hence find an approximation for $1.02^{10}$, giving your answer to 5 significant figures.
$(a)$: $1 + 20x + 180x^2 + 960x^3 + \cdots$ $(b)$: Set $2x = 0.02 \Rightarrow x = 0.01$. Then $1.02^{10} \approx 1 + 20(0.01) + 180(0.0001) + 960(0.000001) = 1 + 0.2 + 0.018 + 0.00096 = 1.2190$ (to 5 s.f.: $\mathbf{1.2190}$). Actual: $1.21899...$
[6 marks]
```
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
```
Q9
Show that $\displaystyle\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r} = 2^n$. Hence find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=0}^{n}\binom{n}{r}(-1)^r$ and interpret its value.
Set $x = 1$ in $(1+x)^n = \sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r}x^r$: $(1+1)^n = 2^n = \sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r}$. $\square$

Set $x = -1$: $(1-1)^n = 0 = \sum_{r=0}^n \binom{n}{r}(-1)^r$ (for $n \geq 1$). This means the alternating sum of any row of Pascal's Triangle equals zero — the sum of coefficients with even $r$ equals the sum with odd $r$. In other words, in any expansion $(1+x)^n$, exactly half the terms contribute positively and half negatively when $x = -1$.
[6 marks]
Q10
In the expansion of $(1 + x)^n(1 + x)^m = (1+x)^{n+m}$, compare the coefficient of $x^r$ on both sides to prove the Vandermonde identity: $$\sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{n}{k}\binom{m}{r-k} = \binom{n+m}{r}$$
The coefficient of $x^r$ in $(1+x)^{n+m}$ is $\binom{n+m}{r}$ (right-hand side). For the left-hand side: expand each factor separately. The coefficient of $x^k$ in $(1+x)^n$ is $\binom{n}{k}$, and the coefficient of $x^{r-k}$ in $(1+x)^m$ is $\binom{m}{r-k}$. When multiplying the two series, the coefficient of $x^r$ is the sum over all ways to pick powers summing to $r$: $$\text{coeff of } x^r \text{ in } (1+x)^n(1+x)^m = \sum_{k=0}^{r}\binom{n}{k}\binom{m}{r-k}$$ Since the two expressions are equal as polynomials, their coefficients of $x^r$ must be equal: $$\sum_{k=0}^{r}\binom{n}{k}\binom{m}{r-k} = \binom{n+m}{r} \qquad \square$$ This is a beautiful identity connecting combinatorics and algebra — it counts the number of ways to choose $r$ objects from $n+m$ by splitting into those taken from the first $n$ and those from the last $m$.
[8 marks]
```
