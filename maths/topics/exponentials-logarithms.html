6.1 Exponential Functions

```

An exponential function has the variable in the exponent: $f(x) = a^x$ where $a > 0$, $a \neq 1$. Unlike polynomials, these grow (or decay) at a rate proportional to their current value — making them the natural model for population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest, and temperature change.

$$f(x) = a^x \quad (a > 0,\ a \neq 1)$$ $$\text{Key properties: domain } (-\infty, \infty),\ \text{range } (0, \infty)$$ $$\text{Always passes through } (0, 1) \text{ since } a^0 = 1$$ $$a > 1: \text{ increasing (growth)} \qquad 0 < a < 1: \text{ decreasing (decay)}$$

The Natural Base $e$

The number $e \approx 2.718...$ is the unique base for which the exponential function is its own derivative — making $e^x$ the most important function in calculus. It arises naturally from compound interest and continuous growth.

$$e = \lim_{n \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \approx 2.71828\ldots$$ $$y = e^x: \text{ passes through }(0,1),\text{ gradient at every point equals its value}$$ $$y = e^{-x}: \text{ reflection of } e^x \text{ in the }y\text{-axis — exponential decay}$$
Graph Behaviour All exponential graphs $y = a^x$ share these features: pass through $(0,1)$; never touch the $x$-axis (the $x$-axis is a horizontal asymptote as $x \to -\infty$ for growth, $x \to +\infty$ for decay); always positive. Transformations work exactly as in Topic 1 — $y = 3e^{2x} + 1$ is a vertical stretch, horizontal compression, and upward shift of $e^x$.
Worked Example 6.1a
Sketch $y = 2e^{-x} + 3$, stating the asymptote, $y$-intercept, and whether the function is increasing or decreasing.
1
Start with $y = e^{-x}$: reflection of $e^x$ in the $y$-axis, so it is decreasing. Passes through $(0, 1)$.
2
$y = 2e^{-x}$: vertical stretch by factor 2. Passes through $(0, 2)$.
3
$y = 2e^{-x} + 3$: translate up by 3. Passes through $(0, 5)$. Asymptote shifts from $y = 0$ to $y = 3$.
4
Summary: $y$-intercept $(0, 5)$, asymptote $y = 3$, decreasing, range $(3, \infty)$.
```

6.2 Logarithms

```

The logarithm is the inverse of the exponential. If $a^x = y$, then $\log_a y = x$. You can think of $\log_a y$ as answering the question: "What power do I raise $a$ to, to get $y$?"

$$a^x = y \iff \log_a y = x$$ $$\text{The natural logarithm: } \ln x = \log_e x$$ $$e^x = y \iff \ln y = x$$ $$\log_a a = 1 \qquad \log_a 1 = 0 \qquad \log_a(a^x) = x \qquad a^{\log_a x} = x$$

The graph of $y = \log_a x$ is the reflection of $y = a^x$ in the line $y = x$. It passes through $(1, 0)$, has the $y$-axis as a vertical asymptote, and has domain $(0, \infty)$.

Worked Example 6.2a
Evaluate without a calculator: $(a)$ $\log_2 32$   $(b)$ $\log_3 \frac{1}{9}$   $(c)$ $\ln e^5$
1
$(a)$: "What power of 2 gives 32?" $2^5 = 32$, so $\log_2 32 = \mathbf{5}$.
2
$(b)$: $\frac{1}{9} = 3^{-2}$, so $\log_3 \frac{1}{9} = \mathbf{-2}$.
3
$(c)$: $\ln e^5 = \mathbf{5}$ directly — $\ln$ and $e^x$ are inverses.
⚠ Domain of Logarithms $\log_a x$ is only defined for $x > 0$. You cannot take the log of zero or a negative number. In equations, always check at the end that your solutions satisfy $x > 0$ — spurious solutions frequently arise from squaring or other manipulations.
```

6.3 Laws of Logarithms

```

These three laws are the backbone of all logarithm work. They correspond directly to the laws of indices — because logarithms are indices.

$$\log_a(xy) = \log_a x + \log_a y \quad \text{(multiplication → addition)}$$ $$\log_a\!\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_a x - \log_a y \quad \text{(division → subtraction)}$$ $$\log_a(x^n) = n\log_a x \quad \text{(power → multiply)}$$ $$\textbf{Change of base: } \log_a b = \frac{\log_c b}{\log_c a} = \frac{\ln b}{\ln a}$$
Addition Law

$\log(AB) = \log A + \log B$
Comes from $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$

Subtraction Law

$\log\!\left(\tfrac{A}{B}\right) = \log A - \log B$
Comes from $a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}$

Power Law

$\log(A^n) = n\log A$
Comes from $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$

Change of Base

$\log_a b = \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a}$
Lets you use a calculator for any base

Worked Example 6.3a — Simplifying
Write $2\log 3 + \log 4 - \log 6$ as a single logarithm.
1
Apply power law to the first term: $2\log 3 = \log 3^2 = \log 9$.
2
Apply addition law: $\log 9 + \log 4 = \log(9 \times 4) = \log 36$.
3
Apply subtraction law: $\log 36 - \log 6 = \log\!\left(\dfrac{36}{6}\right) = \log 6$.
Worked Example 6.3b — Expanding
Express $\ln\!\left(\dfrac{x^3\sqrt{y}}{z^2}\right)$ in terms of $\ln x$, $\ln y$, $\ln z$.
1
Split numerator and denominator: $\ln(x^3\sqrt{y}) - \ln(z^2)$
2
$= \ln(x^3) + \ln(y^{1/2}) - \ln(z^2)$
3
Apply power law: $= 3\ln x + \tfrac{1}{2}\ln y - 2\ln z$
⚠ Common Misconceptions $\log(x + y) \neq \log x + \log y$. The addition law only works for products inside the log, not sums. Similarly, $\dfrac{\log x}{\log y} \neq \log\!\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)$ — the division law only applies when the division is inside the log. These are the two most common errors in this topic.
```

6.4 Solving Exponential Equations

```

When the unknown is in the exponent, take logarithms of both sides to bring it down. The power law does the heavy lifting.

$$a^x = b \implies x\ln a = \ln b \implies x = \frac{\ln b}{\ln a}$$ $$\text{Or using change of base: } x = \log_a b$$
Worked Example 6.4a
Solve $5^x = 20$, giving your answer to 3 significant figures.
1
Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln(5^x) = \ln 20$
2
Power law: $x\ln 5 = \ln 20$
3
$x = \dfrac{\ln 20}{\ln 5} = \dfrac{2.9957\ldots}{1.6094\ldots} \approx \mathbf{1.86}$
Worked Example 6.4b
Solve $3^{2x} - 10 \cdot 3^x + 9 = 0$.
1
Let $u = 3^x$. Note $3^{2x} = (3^x)^2 = u^2$. The equation becomes: $$u^2 - 10u + 9 = 0$$
2
Factorise: $(u - 1)(u - 9) = 0$, so $u = 1$ or $u = 9$.
3
$3^x = 1 \implies x = 0$.   $3^x = 9 = 3^2 \implies x = 2$.
Solutions: $x = 0$ and $x = 2$.
Worked Example 6.4c — Solving with $\ln$
Solve $\ln(3x - 1) + \ln(x + 2) = \ln(2x^2 + 1)$.
1
Combine the left side using the addition law: $$\ln\!\big[(3x-1)(x+2)\big] = \ln(2x^2+1)$$
2
Since $\ln$ is one-to-one, the arguments must be equal: $$(3x-1)(x+2) = 2x^2 + 1$$
3
Expand: $3x^2 + 6x - x - 2 = 2x^2 + 1$ $$x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0 \implies x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{37}}{2}$$
4
$x \approx 0.541$ or $x \approx -5.541$. Check domain: need $3x - 1 > 0 \Rightarrow x > \frac{1}{3}$ and $x + 2 > 0 \Rightarrow x > -2$. Only $x \approx 0.541$ is valid.
Always check solutions satisfy the domain conditions for all log terms.
```

6.5 Exponential Modelling & Linearisation

```

Exponential functions model real-world phenomena where the rate of change is proportional to the current value. This covers population growth, radioactive decay, cooling, and compound interest.

$$N = N_0\,e^{kt}$$ $$k > 0: \text{ growth} \qquad k < 0: \text{ decay}$$ $$\text{Half-life: time } T \text{ such that } N = \tfrac{1}{2}N_0 \implies e^{kT} = \tfrac{1}{2} \implies T = \frac{-\ln 2}{k}$$
Worked Example 6.5a
A population of bacteria grows according to $P = 500e^{0.4t}$ where $t$ is measured in hours. $(a)$ Find the initial population. $(b)$ Find the population after 3 hours. $(c)$ Find the time when the population reaches 5000.
1
$(a)$: At $t = 0$: $P = 500e^0 = 500$.
2
$(b)$: At $t = 3$: $P = 500e^{1.2} = 500 \times 3.320 \approx 1660$ bacteria.
3
$(c)$: Set $500e^{0.4t} = 5000 \implies e^{0.4t} = 10 \implies 0.4t = \ln 10 \implies t = \dfrac{\ln 10}{0.4} \approx 5.76$ hours.

Linearisation: $y = ax^n$ and $y = ab^x$

Taking logarithms of an exponential or power model transforms it into a linear relationship — so you can plot a straight-line graph and read off the constants. This technique is used in every science subject.

$$y = ax^n \implies \log y = \log a + n\log x$$ $$\text{Plot } \log y \text{ against } \log x: \text{ gradient} = n,\ \text{intercept} = \log a$$ $$y = ab^x \implies \log y = \log a + x\log b$$ $$\text{Plot } \log y \text{ against } x: \text{ gradient} = \log b,\ \text{intercept} = \log a$$
Worked Example 6.5b
A scientist believes variables $x$ and $y$ satisfy $y = ax^n$. When $\log y$ is plotted against $\log x$, a straight line passes through $(0.5, 1.3)$ and $(2, 4.3)$. Find $a$ and $n$.
1
The gradient of the line (which equals $n$): $$n = \frac{4.3 - 1.3}{2 - 0.5} = \frac{3}{1.5} = 2$$
2
Find the intercept using point $(0.5, 1.3)$ and gradient 2: $$\log y - 1.3 = 2(\log x - 0.5) \implies \log y = 2\log x + 0.3$$ So $\log a = 0.3 \implies a = 10^{0.3} \approx 2.00$.
3
Therefore $y = 2x^2$.
Always convert back from $\log a$ to $a$ at the end — the intercept on a log graph gives $\log a$, not $a$ itself.
Which Model Applies? If you plot $\log y$ vs $\log x$ and get a straight line, the model is $y = ax^n$ (a power law). If you plot $\log y$ vs $x$ and get a straight line, the model is $y = ab^x$ (an exponential). This distinction is tested directly in exams — you'll be given the graph and asked to identify the model and find the constants.
```

```
Q1
Evaluate: $(a)\ \log_5 125$   $(b)\ \log_4 \dfrac{1}{16}$   $(c)\ \ln e^{-3}$
$(a)$: $5^3 = 125$, so $\log_5 125 = 3$.   $(b)$: $4^{-2} = \frac{1}{16}$, so $\log_4\frac{1}{16} = -2$.   $(c)$: $\ln e^{-3} = -3$.
[3 marks]
Q2
Write as a single logarithm: $3\ln 2 - \ln 4 + \ln 5$.
$3\ln 2 = \ln 8$. $\ln 8 - \ln 4 = \ln 2$. $\ln 2 + \ln 5 = \ln 10$.
[3 marks]
Q3
Solve $7^x = 50$, giving your answer to 3 significant figures.
$x\ln 7 = \ln 50 \implies x = \dfrac{\ln 50}{\ln 7} = \dfrac{3.912}{1.946} \approx \mathbf{2.01}$.
[3 marks]
Q4
Solve $\ln(2x + 3) = 2$, giving your answer exactly.
$2x + 3 = e^2 \implies x = \dfrac{e^2 - 3}{2}$.
[3 marks]
Q5
A radioactive substance decays according to $M = 200e^{-0.05t}$ grams, where $t$ is in years. Find $(a)$ the initial mass, $(b)$ the mass after 10 years, $(c)$ the half-life.
$(a)$: $M(0) = 200$ g. $(b)$: $M(10) = 200e^{-0.5} \approx 121$ g. $(c)$: $e^{-0.05T} = 0.5 \implies T = \dfrac{\ln 2}{0.05} \approx 13.9$ years.
[5 marks]
```
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
```
Q6
Solve $2^{2x+1} - 5 \cdot 2^x + 2 = 0$.
$2^{2x+1} = 2 \cdot (2^x)^2$. Let $u = 2^x$: $2u^2 - 5u + 2 = 0 \implies (2u-1)(u-2) = 0$. So $u = \frac{1}{2}$ or $u = 2$. $2^x = 2^{-1} \Rightarrow x = -1$. $2^x = 2 \Rightarrow x = 1$.
[5 marks]
Q7
Variables $x$ and $y$ satisfy $y = ab^x$. When $\log_{10} y$ is plotted against $x$, a straight line with gradient $0.3$ and $y$-intercept $1.7$ is obtained. Find $a$ and $b$.
$\log y = \log a + x\log b$. So $\log b = 0.3 \Rightarrow b = 10^{0.3} \approx 2.00$ and $\log a = 1.7 \Rightarrow a = 10^{1.7} \approx 50.1$.
[4 marks]
Q8
Solve $\log_2(x+1) - \log_2(x-1) = 3$.
$\log_2\!\left(\dfrac{x+1}{x-1}\right) = 3 \implies \dfrac{x+1}{x-1} = 8 \implies x + 1 = 8x - 8 \implies 7x = 9 \implies x = \dfrac{9}{7}$. Check: $x - 1 = \frac{2}{7} > 0$ ✓.
[5 marks]
```
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
```
Q9
$(a)$ Show that $\log_a b \cdot \log_b a = 1$ for all valid $a, b$. $(b)$ Show that $\log_a b \cdot \log_b c = \log_a c$. $(c)$ Hence evaluate $\log_2 3 \cdot \log_3 4 \cdot \log_4 8$ without a calculator.
$(a)$: By change of base, $\log_a b = \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a}$ and $\log_b a = \dfrac{\ln a}{\ln b}$. Product $= \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a} \cdot \dfrac{\ln a}{\ln b} = 1$. $\square$ $(b)$: $\log_a b \cdot \log_b c = \dfrac{\ln b}{\ln a} \cdot \dfrac{\ln c}{\ln b} = \dfrac{\ln c}{\ln a} = \log_a c$. $\square$ $(c)$: By $(b)$ applied twice: $\log_2 3 \cdot \log_3 4 \cdot \log_4 8 = \log_2 4 \cdot \log_4 8 = \log_2 8 = 3$.
[8 marks]
Q10
Prove that $e > 2.7$ by showing that $\left(1 + \dfrac{1}{n}\right)^n$ is increasing in $n$, and evaluating it at $n = 10$. Hence explain why $e$ cannot be rational by considering the series $e = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{1}{k!}$ (you may assume this series converges to $e$).
At $n = 10$: $\left(\frac{11}{10}\right)^{10} = \frac{11^{10}}{10^{10}}$. Numerically: $1.1^{10} \approx 2.5937 > 2.5$. Since the sequence is increasing (can be shown by AM-GM: $\frac{n \cdot \frac{n+1}{n} + 1}{n+1} \geq \left(\frac{n+1}{n}\right)^{n/(n+1)}$ — a STEP-level argument), and $1.1^{10} \approx 2.594$, we have $e > 2.594$. With $n = 100$, one gets $> 2.705$, approaching $e \approx 2.718$. For irrationality (sketch of argument): Suppose $e = p/q$ for integers $p, q$. Then $q! \cdot e = q! \cdot \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k!}$. The partial sum $\sum_{k=0}^{q} \frac{q!}{k!}$ is an integer. The tail $q!\sum_{k=q+1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{k!} = \frac{1}{q+1} + \frac{1}{(q+1)(q+2)} + \cdots$, which is strictly between 0 and 1 (geometric series bound). So $q!\cdot e$ is an integer plus a non-integer — contradiction. Therefore $e$ is irrational. $\square$
[10 marks]
```
