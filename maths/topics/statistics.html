16.1 Data & Sampling

Statistics begins with data. Before analysing anything, you need to understand what type of data you have and how it was collected.

Types of Data

Qualitative data is non-numerical (e.g. eye colour, favourite subject). Quantitative data is numerical, and splits into discrete (countable, e.g. number of siblings) and continuous (measurable, e.g. height, time).

Populations and Samples

A population is the entire group being studied. A sample is a subset of the population. We take samples because it is usually impractical or impossible to survey an entire population.

Key Point A census surveys every member of a population. It gives completely accurate results but is time-consuming and expensive. A sample survey is faster and cheaper but introduces sampling error.

Sampling Methods

You need to know five sampling methods for Edexcel:

Simple random sampling: Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. Use a random number generator or lottery method. Unbiased but impractical for large populations.

Systematic sampling: Select every $k$th member from a list, where $k = \frac{\text{population size}}{\text{sample size}}$. Quick and easy, but can introduce bias if the list has a periodic pattern.

Stratified sampling: Divide the population into groups (strata) and sample from each in proportion to the stratum's size. Ensures representation from all subgroups.

Opportunity (convenience) sampling: Use whoever is available. Quick but highly biased — not representative.

Quota sampling: Like stratified but the researcher chooses who to include within each stratum. Faster than stratified but introduces researcher bias.

$$\text{Stratum sample size} = \frac{\text{Stratum size}}{\text{Population size}} \times \text{Total sample size}$$
Worked Example 16.1a
A school has 800 students: 320 in Year 12 and 480 in Year 13. A stratified sample of 50 students is required. How many should come from each year group?
1
Year 12: $\dfrac{320}{800} \times 50 = 20$ students
Proportion from Year 12 is 320/800 = 0.4
2
Year 13: $\dfrac{480}{800} \times 50 = 30$ students
3
Total: $20 + 30 = 50$ ✓

16.2 Measures of Location & Central Tendency

Measures of location describe where the centre of a dataset lies.

$$\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum x}{n} \qquad \text{(mean of raw data)}$$ $$\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum fx}{\sum f} \qquad \text{(mean of frequency distribution)}$$

The median is the middle value when data is ordered. For $n$ values, the median is at position $\dfrac{n+1}{2}$.

The mode is the most frequent value. A dataset can be bimodal (two modes) or have no mode.

Grouped Data

For grouped frequency tables, you cannot find exact values — you estimate using the midpoint of each class.

Worked Example 16.2a
Find the estimated mean of the following grouped data:
Heights (cm): 150–160 (freq 4), 160–170 (freq 11), 170–180 (freq 7), 180–190 (freq 3)
1
Use midpoints: 155, 165, 175, 185
2
$\sum fx = 155(4) + 165(11) + 175(7) + 185(3) = 620 + 1815 + 1225 + 555 = 4215$
3
$\sum f = 4 + 11 + 7 + 3 = 25$
4
$\bar{x} = \dfrac{4215}{25} = 168.6$ cm

Quartiles and Percentiles

The lower quartile $Q_1$ is at position $\frac{n}{4}$, the upper quartile $Q_3$ is at position $\frac{3n}{4}$. For grouped data, use linear interpolation.

$$Q_1 \text{ position}: \frac{n}{4} \qquad Q_2 \text{ (median)}: \frac{n}{2} \qquad Q_3 \text{ position}: \frac{3n}{4}$$

16.3 Measures of Spread

Measures of spread describe how dispersed the data is around the centre.

$$\text{Range} = \text{max} - \text{min}$$ $$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$$ $$\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sum(x - \bar{x})^2}{n} = \dfrac{\sum x^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2$$ $$\sigma = \sqrt{\dfrac{\sum x^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2}$$
Key Point The formula $\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sum x^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2$ is much faster in practice than computing each $(x - \bar{x})^2$ separately. Always use this version in exams.

For Frequency Distributions

$$\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sum fx^2}{\sum f} - \bar{x}^2$$
Worked Example 16.3a
The values $3, 7, 8, 5, 9, 4, 6$ have mean $\bar{x} = 6$. Find the standard deviation.
1
$\sum x^2 = 9 + 49 + 64 + 25 + 81 + 16 + 36 = 280$
2
$\sigma^2 = \dfrac{280}{7} - 6^2 = 40 - 36 = 4$
3
$\sigma = \sqrt{4} = 2$

Outliers

An outlier is a value more than $1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ below $Q_1$ or above $Q_3$. Outliers should be noted and investigated — they may indicate genuine extreme values or data errors.

$$\text{Outlier if: } x < Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR} \quad \text{or} \quad x > Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$$

16.4 Probability

Probability measures how likely an event is to occur, on a scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

$$P(A) = \dfrac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of equally likely outcomes}}$$ $$P(A') = 1 - P(A)$$ $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

Independent and Mutually Exclusive Events

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot both occur: $P(A \cap B) = 0$, so $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$.

Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the other: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$.

Conditional Probability

$$P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

Read $P(A \mid B)$ as "the probability of $A$ given that $B$ has occurred". This is vital for tree diagrams and Venn diagrams.

Worked Example 16.4a
In a class of 30, 18 study French, 12 study Spanish, and 5 study both. A student is chosen at random. Find the probability that they study French, given that they study Spanish.
1
$P(F \cap S) = \dfrac{5}{30}$, $\quad P(S) = \dfrac{12}{30}$
2
$P(F \mid S) = \dfrac{P(F \cap S)}{P(S)} = \dfrac{5/30}{12/30} = \dfrac{5}{12}$
⚠ Common Mistake Independent does NOT mean mutually exclusive. Independent means $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$. Mutually exclusive means $P(A \cap B) = 0$. Two events with non-zero probability cannot be both independent and mutually exclusive simultaneously.

16.5 Statistical Distributions

A random variable $X$ takes numerical values determined by the outcome of a random process. We describe it using a probability distribution.

Discrete Probability Distributions

For a discrete random variable, we list all possible values and their probabilities. The probabilities must sum to 1.

$$\sum P(X = x) = 1$$ $$E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(X = x)$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = E(X^2) - [E(X)]^2 = \sum x^2 P(X=x) - \mu^2$$
Worked Example 16.5a
$X$ has the distribution: $P(X=1) = 0.2$, $P(X=2) = 0.5$, $P(X=3) = 0.3$. Find $E(X)$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.
1
$E(X) = 1(0.2) + 2(0.5) + 3(0.3) = 0.2 + 1.0 + 0.9 = 2.1$
2
$E(X^2) = 1^2(0.2) + 2^2(0.5) + 3^2(0.3) = 0.2 + 2.0 + 2.7 = 4.9$
3
$\text{Var}(X) = 4.9 - (2.1)^2 = 4.9 - 4.41 = 0.49$

16.6 Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, each with the same probability of success.

We write $X \sim B(n, p)$ where $n$ is the number of trials and $p$ is the probability of success on each trial.

Four Conditions for Binomial (1) Fixed number of trials $n$. (2) Each trial is independent. (3) Only two outcomes: success or failure. (4) Constant probability of success $p$.
$$P(X = r) = \binom{n}{r} p^r (1-p)^{n-r}$$ $$E(X) = np$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$$

Where $\dbinom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$ is the binomial coefficient (number of ways to choose $r$ from $n$).

Worked Example 16.6a
$X \sim B(8, 0.3)$. Find $P(X = 3)$.
1
$P(X = 3) = \dbinom{8}{3}(0.3)^3(0.7)^5$
2
$\dbinom{8}{3} = \dfrac{8!}{3! \cdot 5!} = 56$
3
$P(X=3) = 56 \times 0.027 \times 0.16807 = 56 \times 0.004538 \approx 0.2541$
Worked Example 16.6b
$X \sim B(10, 0.4)$. Find $P(X \geq 3)$.
1
Use the complement: $P(X \geq 3) = 1 - P(X \leq 2)$
2
$P(X=0) = (0.6)^{10} \approx 0.00605$
3
$P(X=1) = 10(0.4)(0.6)^9 \approx 0.04031$
4
$P(X=2) = 45(0.4)^2(0.6)^8 \approx 0.12093$
5
$P(X \geq 3) = 1 - (0.00605 + 0.04031 + 0.12093) = 1 - 0.16729 \approx 0.8327$

16.7 Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing gives us a rigorous framework for deciding whether sample data provides enough evidence to reject a claim about a population parameter.

The Framework

Step 1 — Set up hypotheses: The null hypothesis $H_0$ is the default claim (usually "no change" or a specific value of $p$). The alternative hypothesis $H_1$ is what you are trying to show.

Step 2 — Choose significance level $\alpha$: Typically 5% ($\alpha = 0.05$) or 1%.

Step 3 — Find the critical region: The set of values that would lead you to reject $H_0$.

Step 4 — Calculate the test statistic and compare to the critical region.

Step 5 — Conclude in context.

One-tailed vs Two-tailed Tests A one-tailed test tests if $p > p_0$ or $p < p_0$ (you specify a direction). A two-tailed test tests if $p \neq p_0$ (either direction). For a two-tailed test at 5% significance, use 2.5% in each tail.
Worked Example 16.7a
A coin is tossed 20 times and lands heads 15 times. Test at the 5% significance level whether the coin is biased towards heads.
1
$H_0: p = 0.5$ (fair coin), $H_1: p > 0.5$ (biased towards heads). One-tailed test.
2
Under $H_0$: $X \sim B(20, 0.5)$. Find $P(X \geq 15)$.
3
$P(X \geq 15) = P(X=15) + \cdots + P(X=20) \approx 0.0207$
Use tables or calculator. This is the p-value.
4
$0.0207 < 0.05$, so we reject $H_0$.
5
Conclusion: There is sufficient evidence at the 5% significance level to conclude that the coin is biased towards heads.
⚠ Common Mistake Never say "we accept $H_0$". If the test is not significant, we say "there is insufficient evidence to reject $H_0$". We never prove the null hypothesis — we simply fail to disprove it.

16.8 Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is the most important continuous distribution. It is symmetric and bell-shaped, described entirely by its mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$.

We write $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. The standard normal has $\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$: $Z \sim N(0, 1)$.

$$X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2) \implies Z = \dfrac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1)$$ $$P(X < a) = P\!\left(Z < \dfrac{a - \mu}{\sigma}\right)$$
The 68–95–99.7 Rule For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$: approximately 68% of values lie within $1\sigma$ of the mean, 95% within $2\sigma$, and 99.7% within $3\sigma$. This is useful for quick sanity checks.
Worked Example 16.8a
$X \sim N(50, 16)$. Find $P(X > 54)$.
1
$\sigma = \sqrt{16} = 4$. Standardise: $Z = \dfrac{54 - 50}{4} = 1$
2
$P(X > 54) = P(Z > 1) = 1 - \Phi(1)$
3
From tables: $\Phi(1) = 0.8413$, so $P(X > 54) = 1 - 0.8413 = 0.1587$

Normal Approximation to Binomial

When $n$ is large and $p$ is not too extreme, $B(n,p)$ can be approximated by $N(np, np(1-p))$. Apply a continuity correction: replace $P(X = k)$ with $P(k - 0.5 < Y < k + 0.5)$.

$$B(n, p) \approx N\!\left(np,\, np(1-p)\right) \quad \text{when } n \text{ large, } p \text{ not near 0 or 1}$$

Hypothesis Testing with the Normal Distribution

For large samples, we can test a population mean $\mu$ using the sample mean $\bar{X}$. Under $H_0$:

$$\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu_0,\, \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \implies Z = \dfrac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1)$$

16.9 Correlation & Regression

Correlation and regression describe relationships between two variables in bivariate data.

Product Moment Correlation Coefficient (PMCC)

The PMCC $r$ measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables $x$ and $y$.

$$-1 \leq r \leq 1$$ $$r = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}}$$

where $S_{xy} = \sum xy - \dfrac{\sum x \sum y}{n}$, $\quad S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \dfrac{(\sum x)^2}{n}$, $\quad S_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \dfrac{(\sum y)^2}{n}$.

Interpreting r $r = 1$: perfect positive linear correlation. $r = -1$: perfect negative linear correlation. $r = 0$: no linear correlation. Values near $\pm 1$ indicate strong correlation; near 0 indicates weak.

Linear Regression (Least Squares)

The regression line of $y$ on $x$ is $y = a + bx$, where:

$$b = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$$

The regression line always passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. Use it to predict $y$ for given $x$ values within the data range (interpolation) — but be cautious about extrapolating beyond the data.

⚠ Correlation ≠ Causation A strong correlation between two variables does not mean one causes the other. There may be a lurking variable (confounding factor) causing both, or the correlation may be coincidental. Always comment on this in exam questions.
Worked Example 16.9a
For a dataset with $n = 6$: $\sum x = 30$, $\sum y = 42$, $\sum xy = 228$, $\sum x^2 = 170$, $\sum y^2 = 316$. Find the regression line of $y$ on $x$.
1
$\bar{x} = 30/6 = 5$, $\quad \bar{y} = 42/6 = 7$
2
$S_{xy} = 228 - \dfrac{30 \times 42}{6} = 228 - 210 = 18$
3
$S_{xx} = 170 - \dfrac{30^2}{6} = 170 - 150 = 20$
4
$b = \dfrac{18}{20} = 0.9$, $\quad a = 7 - 0.9(5) = 7 - 4.5 = 2.5$
5
Regression line: $y = 2.5 + 0.9x$

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
A company has 200 employees: 80 in sales, 60 in operations, and 60 in finance. A stratified sample of 40 is required. How many should come from each department?
Sales: $\frac{80}{200} \times 40 = 16$. Operations: $\frac{60}{200} \times 40 = 12$. Finance: $\frac{60}{200} \times 40 = 12$. Total: $16 + 12 + 12 = 40$ ✓
[3 marks]
Q2
Find the mean and standard deviation of: $2, 5, 5, 7, 9, 10, 10, 12$.
$\bar{x} = 60/8 = 7.5$. $\sum x^2 = 4+25+25+49+81+100+100+144 = 528$. $\sigma^2 = 528/8 - 7.5^2 = 66 - 56.25 = 9.75$. $\sigma = \sqrt{9.75} \approx 3.12$.
[4 marks]
Q3
$X \sim B(12, 0.25)$. Find (a) $P(X = 4)$, (b) $P(X \leq 2)$, (c) $E(X)$ and $\text{Var}(X)$.
(a) $\binom{12}{4}(0.25)^4(0.75)^8 = 495 \times 0.00391 \times 0.1001 \approx 0.1936$. (b) $P(X=0) + P(X=1) + P(X=2) = 0.0317 + 0.1267 + 0.2323 = 0.3907$. (c) $E(X) = 12 \times 0.25 = 3$; $\text{Var}(X) = 12 \times 0.25 \times 0.75 = 2.25$.
[6 marks]
Q4
$X \sim N(100, 25)$. Find (a) $P(X < 103)$, (b) $P(X > 97)$, (c) $P(96 < X < 108)$.
$\sigma = 5$. (a) $P(Z < 0.6) = 0.7257$. (b) $P(Z > -0.6) = P(Z < 0.6) = 0.7257$. (c) $P(-0.8 < Z < 1.6) = \Phi(1.6) - \Phi(-0.8) = 0.9452 - 0.2119 = 0.7333$.
[6 marks]
Q5
Events $A$ and $B$ are such that $P(A) = 0.4$, $P(B) = 0.5$, $P(A \cup B) = 0.7$. Find $P(A \mid B)$ and determine whether $A$ and $B$ are independent.
$P(A \cap B) = 0.4 + 0.5 - 0.7 = 0.2$. $P(A \mid B) = 0.2/0.5 = 0.4$. Check independence: $P(A) \times P(B) = 0.4 \times 0.5 = 0.2 = P(A \cap B)$. So $A$ and $B$ are independent.
[5 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q6
A biased die has $P(6) = p$. The die is rolled 15 times. Find the range of values of $p$ for which it is more likely to get at most 2 sixes than at least 3 sixes.
We need $P(X \leq 2) > 0.5$ where $X \sim B(15, p)$. This requires $p$ to be sufficiently small. Using tables or numerical methods, the critical value is approximately $p < 0.14$ (for exact boundary, solve $P(X \leq 2) = 0.5$).
[5 marks]
Q7
The heights of students at a school are normally distributed with mean 168 cm and standard deviation 8 cm. If the tallest 10% of students are selected for a basketball team, find the minimum height required.
$P(X > h) = 0.1 \Rightarrow P(Z > z) = 0.1 \Rightarrow z = 1.282$. So $h = 168 + 1.282 \times 8 = 168 + 10.26 \approx 178.3$ cm.
[4 marks]
Q8
A researcher claims that 30% of adults drink more than the recommended daily water intake. In a sample of 25 adults, only 4 drink enough. Test at the 5% significance level whether this provides evidence that the true proportion is less than 30%.
$H_0: p = 0.3$, $H_1: p < 0.3$. $X \sim B(25, 0.3)$ under $H_0$. $P(X \leq 4) = \sum_{r=0}^{4} \binom{25}{r}(0.3)^r(0.7)^{25-r} \approx 0.0905$. Since $0.0905 > 0.05$, we do not reject $H_0$. Insufficient evidence at 5% level to conclude the proportion is less than 30%.
[6 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q9
The random variable $X \sim B(n, p)$. Show that $P(X = r+1) = \dfrac{(n-r)p}{(r+1)(1-p)} \cdot P(X = r)$. Use this recurrence relation to find the mode of $B(20, 0.4)$.
$\dfrac{P(X=r+1)}{P(X=r)} = \dfrac{\binom{n}{r+1}p^{r+1}(1-p)^{n-r-1}}{\binom{n}{r}p^r(1-p)^{n-r}} = \dfrac{(n-r)p}{(r+1)(1-p)}$. For mode: this ratio $> 1$ when $(n-r)p > (r+1)(1-p)$, i.e. $np - rp > r + 1 - rp - p$, i.e. $r < (n+1)p - 1 = 21(0.4) - 1 = 7.4$. So $P$ increases up to $r = 7$ and decreases after. Mode is $X = 8$ (check: ratio at $r=7$ is $\frac{13 \times 0.4}{8 \times 0.6} = \frac{5.2}{4.8} > 1$; at $r=8$ is $\frac{12 \times 0.4}{9 \times 0.6} = \frac{4.8}{5.4} < 1$). Mode = 8.
[8 marks]
Q10
Let $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. By considering the transformation $Z = \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$, prove that $P(\mu - k\sigma < X < \mu + k\sigma) = 2\Phi(k) - 1$ where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Deduce the exact probability that $X$ lies within two standard deviations of the mean, given $\Phi(2) = 0.9772$.
$P(\mu - k\sigma < X < \mu + k\sigma) = P\!\left(-k < \frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} < k\right) = P(-k < Z < k) = \Phi(k) - \Phi(-k) = \Phi(k) - (1 - \Phi(k)) = 2\Phi(k) - 1$. For $k = 2$: $P = 2(0.9772) - 1 = 0.9544$. So approximately 95.44% of values lie within two standard deviations.
[7 marks]
