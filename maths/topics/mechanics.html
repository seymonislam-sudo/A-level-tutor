17.1 Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion without reference to forces. We describe motion using displacement, velocity, and acceleration.

Scalars vs Vectors Distance and speed are scalars (magnitude only). Displacement, velocity, and acceleration are vectors (magnitude and direction). In 1D problems, sign indicates direction — always define a positive direction first.
$$v = \dfrac{ds}{dt} \qquad a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \dfrac{d^2s}{dt^2}$$ $$s = \int v\, dt \qquad v = \int a\, dt$$

Displacement-Time and Velocity-Time Graphs

On a displacement-time graph: the gradient equals velocity. A horizontal line means the object is stationary.

On a velocity-time graph: the gradient equals acceleration; the area under the curve equals displacement (not distance — watch for sign changes).

Worked Example 17.1a
A particle moves so that $v = 3t^2 - 12t + 9$ m/s. Find the acceleration when $t = 2$ s and the displacement from $t = 0$ to $t = 3$ s (starting from the origin).
1
$a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = 6t - 12$. At $t = 2$: $a = 12 - 12 = 0$ m/s²
2
$s = \int_0^3 (3t^2 - 12t + 9)\, dt = \left[t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\right]_0^3$
3
$s = (27 - 54 + 27) - 0 = 0$ m
The particle returns to its starting position! Note: this is displacement, not total distance travelled.

17.2 SUVAT Equations of Motion

When acceleration is constant, the five SUVAT equations link displacement $s$, initial velocity $u$, final velocity $v$, acceleration $a$, and time $t$.

$$v = u + at$$ $$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$ $$s = vt - \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$ $$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ $$s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u + v)t$$
Strategy List the five variables $s, u, v, a, t$ and identify which three you know and which one you want. Pick the equation that contains those four variables.
⚠ Gravity In vertical problems, $g = 9.8$ m/s² (Edexcel uses 9.8, not 10). Take care with signs — if upward is positive, then $a = -9.8$ m/s² for a projectile under gravity only.
Worked Example 17.2a
A ball is thrown vertically upward at 14.7 m/s from a height of 1.2 m above the ground. Find the maximum height above the ground and the time to reach it.
1
Take upward as positive. $u = 14.7$, $a = -9.8$, $v = 0$ (at max height). Find $s$.
2
Use $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $0 = 14.7^2 + 2(-9.8)s \Rightarrow s = \dfrac{14.7^2}{19.6} = 11.025$ m
3
Maximum height above ground $= 1.2 + 11.025 = 12.225$ m
4
Time: $v = u + at \Rightarrow 0 = 14.7 - 9.8t \Rightarrow t = 1.5$ s

17.3 Forces & Newton's Laws

Forces are vector quantities measured in Newtons (N). The effect of all forces on an object is described by Newton's three laws.

$$\textbf{Newton's 1st Law: } \text{An object remains at rest or moves with constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force.}$$ $$\textbf{Newton's 2nd Law: } F = ma$$ $$\textbf{Newton's 3rd Law: } \text{Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.}$$

Types of Forces

Weight: $W = mg$ downward. Normal reaction $R$: perpendicular to the surface. Tension $T$: along a string or rod. Friction $F$: opposing motion. Driving force / Thrust.

Resolving Forces

To apply Newton's 2nd Law, resolve all forces in a chosen direction. The net force in that direction equals $ma$.

Worked Example 17.3a
A particle of mass 5 kg is on a smooth horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 20 N acts on it. Find the acceleration and the normal reaction.
1
Horizontally: $F = ma \Rightarrow 20 = 5a \Rightarrow a = 4$ m/s²
2
Vertically (no vertical acceleration): $R - mg = 0 \Rightarrow R = 5 \times 9.8 = 49$ N
Worked Example 17.3b
A particle of mass 3 kg is on a smooth plane inclined at 30° to the horizontal. Find the acceleration down the plane.
1
Resolve parallel to the plane (taking down the plane as positive): $mg\sin 30° = ma$
2
$a = g\sin 30° = 9.8 \times 0.5 = 4.9$ m/s²
On a smooth incline the mass cancels — acceleration is independent of mass.
3
Perpendicular to plane: $R = mg\cos 30° = 3 \times 9.8 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 25.5$ N

17.4 Connected Particles

When two or more particles are connected by a light inextensible string, they share the same acceleration (magnitude) and the string has the same tension throughout.

Method for Connected Particles Apply $F = ma$ to each particle separately, then solve the simultaneous equations. Alternatively, if you only need the acceleration (not the tension), you can treat the whole system as one.
Worked Example 17.4a
Particle A (mass 4 kg) sits on a smooth horizontal table. It is connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley at the edge of the table to particle B (mass 2 kg) hanging freely. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string.
1
For A (horizontal): $T = 4a$   ...(1)
2
For B (vertical, taking down as positive): $2g - T = 2a$   ...(2)
3
Add (1) and (2): $2g = 6a \Rightarrow a = \dfrac{2 \times 9.8}{6} = \dfrac{9.8}{3} \approx 3.27$ m/s²
4
From (1): $T = 4 \times \dfrac{9.8}{3} = \dfrac{39.2}{3} \approx 13.1$ N

17.5 Friction

Friction is a force that opposes motion (or potential motion) between surfaces in contact. The maximum friction force is called the limiting friction.

$$F \leq \mu R$$ $$F_{\max} = \mu R \quad \text{(when sliding or on the point of sliding)}$$

$\mu$ is the coefficient of friction (dimensionless, $0 \leq \mu$). $R$ is the normal reaction. Friction acts up the slope when the particle tends to slide down, and down the slope when it tends to slide up.

Static vs Kinetic Friction At A Level, we use one value of $\mu$ for both. An object is in limiting equilibrium when it is about to move and $F = \mu R$ exactly. If the object is moving, $F = \mu R$ (kinetic friction). If it is stationary and not on the point of moving, $F < \mu R$.
Worked Example 17.5a
A particle of mass 5 kg rests on a rough horizontal surface with $\mu = 0.3$. A horizontal force $P$ is applied. Find (a) the maximum value of $P$ for equilibrium, (b) the acceleration if $P = 25$ N.
1
$R = mg = 5 \times 9.8 = 49$ N
2
(a) $F_{\max} = \mu R = 0.3 \times 49 = 14.7$ N. So $P_{\max} = 14.7$ N.
3
(b) $P = 25 > 14.7$, so the particle moves. Net force $= 25 - 14.7 = 10.3$ N. $a = 10.3/5 = 2.06$ m/s²

17.6 Moments & Equilibrium

The moment of a force about a point is its turning effect. It equals the force multiplied by the perpendicular distance from the point to the line of action of the force.

$$M = Fd$$

Moments are measured in Newton-metres (N m) and can be clockwise (negative by convention) or anticlockwise (positive).

Conditions for Equilibrium

For a rigid body in equilibrium, two conditions must both hold simultaneously:

$$\sum F = 0 \quad \text{(no net force in any direction)}$$ $$\sum M = 0 \quad \text{(no net moment about any point)}$$
Exam Tip: Choosing Moments Pivot Take moments about a point where an unknown force acts — this eliminates that unknown from the moments equation, making calculation much simpler.
Worked Example 17.6a
A uniform beam AB of mass 10 kg and length 4 m rests horizontally on supports at A and at C, where C is 1 m from B. A particle of mass 6 kg hangs from B. Find the reactions at A and C.
1
Let $R_A$ and $R_C$ be the reactions at A and C. Weight of beam acts at midpoint (2 m from A). $g = 9.8$ m/s².
2
Take moments about A (eliminates $R_A$): clockwise moments = anticlockwise moments $$R_C \times 3 = 10g \times 2 + 6g \times 4$$
3
$3R_C = 20g + 24g = 44g \Rightarrow R_C = \dfrac{44 \times 9.8}{3} \approx 143.7$ N
4
Resolve vertically: $R_A + R_C = 10g + 6g = 156.8$ N $R_A = 156.8 - 143.7 \approx 13.1$ N

17.7 Projectile Motion

A projectile moves under gravity alone (no air resistance). The key insight is that horizontal and vertical motion are independent — we treat them separately.

$$\textbf{Horizontal: } a_x = 0, \quad x = u\cos\theta \cdot t$$ $$\textbf{Vertical: } a_y = -g, \quad y = u\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$ $$v_x = u\cos\theta \quad \text{(constant)}$$ $$v_y = u\sin\theta - gt$$

Key Results

$$\text{Time of flight} = \dfrac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$$ $$\text{Range} = \dfrac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g}$$ $$\text{Maximum height} = \dfrac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g}$$ $$\text{Maximum range occurs at } \theta = 45°$$
⚠ Don't Memorise the Range Formula In exams it's safer to derive the result from first principles using the two separate components — the formula only works when the launch and landing heights are the same. For any other scenario, derive from scratch.
Worked Example 17.7a
A ball is launched from the ground at $20$ m/s at $30°$ above the horizontal. Find (a) the maximum height, (b) the time of flight, (c) the horizontal range.
1
Initial components: $u_x = 20\cos 30° = 10\sqrt{3}$ m/s, $u_y = 20\sin 30° = 10$ m/s
2
(a) At max height, $v_y = 0$: $0 = 10 - 9.8t \Rightarrow t = 1.02$ s $H = 10(1.02) - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(1.02)^2 = 10.2 - 5.1 \approx 5.1$ m
3
(b) Time of flight $= 2 \times 1.02 = 2.04$ s (by symmetry)
4
(c) Range $= u_x \times t = 10\sqrt{3} \times 2.04 \approx 17.3 \times 2.04 \approx 35.3$ m

Trajectory Equation

Eliminating $t$ from the component equations gives the Cartesian equation of the trajectory:

$$y = x\tan\theta - \dfrac{gx^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta}$$

17.8 Vectors in Mechanics

In 2D mechanics problems, forces, velocities and accelerations are expressed as column vectors or using $\mathbf{i}$ and $\mathbf{j}$ unit vectors.

$$\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$$ $$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} \quad \text{(speed)}$$ $$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r_0} + \mathbf{u}t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$$
Worked Example 17.8a
A particle of mass 3 kg has position vector $\mathbf{r} = (t^2 + 2)\mathbf{i} + (3t - 1)\mathbf{j}$ metres at time $t$ seconds. Find the force acting on the particle at $t = 2$.
1
$\mathbf{v} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = 2t\,\mathbf{i} + 3\,\mathbf{j}$
2
$\mathbf{a} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = 2\,\mathbf{i} + 0\,\mathbf{j} = 2\,\mathbf{i}$ m/s² (constant — independent of $t$)
3
$\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} = 3 \times 2\,\mathbf{i} = 6\,\mathbf{i}$ N

Relative Velocity

The velocity of A relative to B is $\mathbf{v}_A - \mathbf{v}_B$. This is used in problems involving ships, planes, or any two moving objects.

$$\mathbf{v}_{A\text{ rel }B} = \mathbf{v}_A - \mathbf{v}_B$$

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
A car accelerates uniformly from rest to 24 m/s in 8 seconds. Find the acceleration and the distance travelled.
$a = (v - u)/t = 24/8 = 3$ m/s². $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + \frac{1}{2}(3)(64) = 96$ m.
[4 marks]
Q2
A particle of mass 4 kg rests on a smooth plane inclined at $\theta$ to the horizontal where $\tan\theta = 0.75$. A force $P$ acts up the plane, keeping the particle in equilibrium. Find $P$ and the normal reaction $R$.
$\tan\theta = 0.75 \Rightarrow \sin\theta = 0.6$, $\cos\theta = 0.8$. Parallel: $P = mg\sin\theta = 4(9.8)(0.6) = 23.52$ N. Perpendicular: $R = mg\cos\theta = 4(9.8)(0.8) = 31.36$ N.
[5 marks]
Q3
A uniform rod AB of mass 8 kg and length 3 m is hinged at A and rests horizontally, supported by a vertical string at B. Find the tension in the string and the reaction at the hinge.
Moments about A: $T \times 3 = 8g \times 1.5 \Rightarrow T = 4g = 39.2$ N. Vertical equilibrium: $R_A + T = 8g \Rightarrow R_A = 4g = 39.2$ N. (Hinge acts vertically upward.)
[5 marks]
Q4
A stone is thrown horizontally at 15 m/s from the top of a cliff 45 m high. Find the time to hit the sea and the horizontal distance from the base of the cliff.
Vertical: $45 = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \Rightarrow t^2 = 90/9.8 \approx 9.184 \Rightarrow t \approx 3.03$ s. Horizontal: $x = 15 \times 3.03 \approx 45.5$ m.
[5 marks]
Q5
Two particles of mass 5 kg and 3 kg are connected by a string over a smooth pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string when released from rest.
For 5 kg: $5g - T = 5a$. For 3 kg: $T - 3g = 3a$. Adding: $2g = 8a \Rightarrow a = g/4 = 2.45$ m/s². $T = 3g + 3a = 3(9.8) + 3(2.45) = 29.4 + 7.35 = 36.75$ N.
[6 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q6
A particle of mass 2 kg is on a rough inclined plane ($\mu = 0.4$, angle $30°$). It is given an initial velocity of 5 m/s up the plane. Find how far it travels before coming to rest, and determine whether it stays at rest or slides back down.
Going up: $R = mg\cos 30° = 2(9.8)(0.866) = 16.97$ N. Friction acts down: $F = \mu R = 0.4(16.97) = 6.79$ N. Deceleration: $a = g\sin 30° + \mu g\cos 30° = 4.9 + 3.39 = 8.29$ m/s². Distance: $v^2 = u^2 - 2as \Rightarrow s = 25/(2 \times 8.29) = 1.51$ m. Check at rest: force down slope $= mg\sin 30° = 9.8$ N; max friction up slope $= 6.79$ N. Since $9.8 > 6.79$, particle slides back down.
[8 marks]
Q7
A particle is projected at speed $u$ at angle $\alpha$ above the horizontal from a point O. Show that the particle passes through the point $(R, 0)$ (same height as O) when $R = \dfrac{u^2 \sin 2\alpha}{g}$ and find the two angles at which a target 80 m away can be hit at $u = 30$ m/s.
Time of flight: $0 = u\sin\alpha \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \Rightarrow t = 2u\sin\alpha/g$. Range: $R = u\cos\alpha \cdot t = \frac{u^2 \cdot 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha}{g} = \frac{u^2\sin 2\alpha}{g}$ ✓. For target: $\sin 2\alpha = \frac{80g}{u^2} = \frac{80 \times 9.8}{900} = \frac{784}{900} = 0.871$. $2\alpha = 60.5°$ or $119.5°$. So $\alpha \approx 30.3°$ or $59.7°$.
[8 marks]
Q8
A particle of mass 5 kg moves so that $\mathbf{r} = (2t^3 - t)\mathbf{i} + (t^2 + 4t)\mathbf{j}$ metres. Find the magnitude of the force on the particle at $t = 1$ s.
$\mathbf{v} = (6t^2 - 1)\mathbf{i} + (2t + 4)\mathbf{j}$. $\mathbf{a} = 12t\,\mathbf{i} + 2\,\mathbf{j}$. At $t = 1$: $\mathbf{a} = 12\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j}$. $\mathbf{F} = 5\mathbf{a} = 60\mathbf{i} + 10\mathbf{j}$. $|\mathbf{F}| = \sqrt{60^2 + 10^2} = \sqrt{3700} = 10\sqrt{37} \approx 60.8$ N.
[5 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q9
A particle is projected from point O at speed $u$ and angle $\alpha$. A second particle is projected simultaneously from the same point at speed $v$ and angle $\beta$ (where $\beta > \alpha$). Find the condition on $u$, $v$, $\alpha$, $\beta$ such that both particles reach the same maximum height. If this condition holds and $\alpha = 30°$, $\beta = 60°$, find the ratio $u : v$.
Max height: $H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\alpha}{2g}$. Equal heights: $u^2\sin^2\alpha = v^2\sin^2\beta \Rightarrow u\sin\alpha = v\sin\beta$ (taking positive square roots). With $\alpha = 30°$, $\beta = 60°$: $u \cdot \frac{1}{2} = v \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \Rightarrow u = v\sqrt{3}$. So $u:v = \sqrt{3}:1$.
[7 marks]
Q10
A uniform ladder of mass $m$ and length $2a$ leans against a smooth vertical wall at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal, with its foot on rough ground ($\mu$). A person of mass $M$ starts climbing the ladder. Find how far up the ladder the person can go before the ladder slips, and show that the ladder cannot slip if $\tan\theta > \dfrac{m + 2M}{2\mu(m + M)}$ is violated (i.e. determine the critical distance).
Let person be distance $d$ up the ladder. Normal at wall: $S$ (horizontal). Normal at ground: $R = (m+M)g$ (vertical equil.). Friction at ground: $F = S$ (horizontal equil.). Moments about foot: $S \cdot 2a\sin\theta = mg \cdot a\cos\theta + Mg \cdot d\cos\theta$. So $S = \frac{(ma + Md)\cos\theta}{2a\sin\theta} \cdot g$. Limiting friction: $F = \mu R \Rightarrow S = \mu(m+M)g$. Setting equal: $\frac{(ma + Md)g\cos\theta}{2a\sin\theta} = \mu(m+M)g$. Solving for $d$: $d = \frac{2\mu a(m+M)\tan\theta - ma}{M} = \frac{a(2\mu(m+M)\tan\theta - m)}{M}$.
[9 marks]
