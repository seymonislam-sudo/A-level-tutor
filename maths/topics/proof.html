Proof

The language of rigorous mathematics. Learn to construct watertight arguments using direct proof, proof by contradiction, proof by exhaustion, and disproof by counterexample.

Types of Proof

A proof is a logical argument that establishes a mathematical statement as true beyond doubt. The four methods you need for A Level are:

The Four Methods
  • Direct proof — start from known facts and deduce the result step by step
  • Proof by exhaustion — check every possible case
  • Proof by contradiction — assume the opposite is true, derive a contradiction
  • Disproof by counterexample — find a single example where the statement fails

Proofs must use precise mathematical language. Avoid vague words like "obviously" or "clearly" — every step must follow logically from the previous one.

Direct Proof

Start with known axioms or given information and apply logical steps until the desired result follows.

Useful algebraic identities for direct proof

Let $n$ be an integer. Then:

  • Even number: $n = 2k$ for some integer $k$
  • Odd number: $n = 2k + 1$ for some integer $k$
  • Consecutive integers: $n,\ n+1,\ n+2, \ldots$
Worked Example 1 — Direct Proof

Prove that the sum of two consecutive odd numbers is divisible by 4.

1
Let the two consecutive odd numbers be $2k+1$ and $2k+3$ for some integer $k$.
2
Their sum is: $$(2k+1)+(2k+3) = 4k+4 = 4(k+1)$$
3
Since $k$ is an integer, $k+1$ is also an integer, so $4(k+1)$ is divisible by 4. $\square$
Worked Example 2 — Direct Proof

Prove that for any integer $n$, $n^2 + n$ is always even.

1
Factorise: $n^2 + n = n(n+1)$.
2
$n$ and $n+1$ are consecutive integers, so one of them must be even.
3
Therefore their product $n(n+1)$ is even. $\square$

Proof by Exhaustion

Check every possible case. Only practical when there are a small, finite number of cases.

Worked Example 3 — Proof by Exhaustion

Prove that for all integers $n$ with $1 \leq n \leq 4$, $n^2 + n + 41$ is prime.

1
Check each case:
$n=1$: $1+1+41=43$ ✓ (prime)
$n=2$: $4+2+41=47$ ✓ (prime)
$n=3$: $9+3+41=53$ ✓ (prime)
$n=4$: $16+4+41=61$ ✓ (prime)
2
All four cases yield a prime number. $\square$
Examiner Note Proof by exhaustion only works when you can check every case. If asked to prove something for all integers, exhaustion will not work — you need a different method.

Proof by Contradiction

Assume the negation of what you want to prove. Use logical reasoning to arrive at a statement that is clearly false (a contradiction). This forces the original assumption to be wrong — so the original statement must be true.

Structure 1. Assume the opposite of what you want to prove.
2. Derive logical consequences.
3. Reach a contradiction (e.g. a number is both even and odd).
4. Conclude the original statement must be true.
Worked Example 4 — Proof by Contradiction

Prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

1
Assume for contradiction that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational. Then $\sqrt{2} = \dfrac{p}{q}$ where $p, q$ are integers with no common factors (i.e. the fraction is in lowest terms).
2
Squaring: $2 = \dfrac{p^2}{q^2}$, so $p^2 = 2q^2$.
3
Since $p^2$ is even, $p$ must be even. Write $p = 2m$ for some integer $m$.
4
Substituting: $(2m)^2 = 2q^2 \Rightarrow 4m^2 = 2q^2 \Rightarrow q^2 = 2m^2$. So $q^2$ is even, meaning $q$ is also even.
5
But now both $p$ and $q$ are even — contradicting our assumption that $\dfrac{p}{q}$ is in lowest terms. $\square$

Disproof by Counterexample

To disprove a statement, you only need to find one example where it fails. A single counterexample is enough — you do not need to show it fails in general.

Worked Example 5 — Disproof by Counterexample

Disprove the statement: "For all integers $n \geq 1$, $n^2 - n + 41$ is prime."

1
Try $n = 41$: $41^2 - 41 + 41 = 41^2 = 1681$.
2
$1681 = 41 \times 41$, which is not prime.
3
Therefore the statement is false. $\square$
Exam Tip When disproving, always state clearly what your counterexample is and show explicitly why the statement fails for that value. Don't just say "try $n=41$" — compute the result and state it is not prime.

Practice Questions

All Levels
Q1 — Foundation [3 marks]

Prove that the product of two odd numbers is always odd.

Let the two odd numbers be $2m+1$ and $2n+1$ for integers $m, n$.

Their product: $(2m+1)(2n+1) = 4mn + 2m + 2n + 1 = 2(2mn+m+n)+1$.

Since $2mn+m+n$ is an integer, the product is of the form $2k+1$, which is odd. $\square$
Q2 — Foundation [2 marks]

Disprove the statement: "For all integers $n$, $n^2 > n$."

Counterexample: let $n = 0$. Then $n^2 = 0$ and $n = 0$, so $n^2 = n$, not $n^2 > n$.

(Alternatively, $n = 1$ gives $n^2 = 1 = n$, or any $n$ with $0 \leq n \leq 1$.) The statement is false. $\square$
Q3 — Intermediate [4 marks]

Prove that for any integer $n$, $n^3 - n$ is divisible by 6.

Factorise: $n^3 - n = n(n^2-1) = n(n-1)(n+1) = (n-1)n(n+1)$.

This is the product of three consecutive integers.

Divisible by 2: Among any three consecutive integers, at least one is even, so the product is even.

Divisible by 3: Among any three consecutive integers, exactly one is divisible by 3.

Since the product is divisible by both 2 and 3, and $\gcd(2,3)=1$, the product is divisible by $6$. $\square$
Q4 — Intermediate [4 marks]

Prove by contradiction that there is no largest even integer.

Assume for contradiction that there exists a largest even integer, call it $N$.

Consider $N + 2$. Since $N$ is even, $N = 2k$ for some integer $k$, so $N+2 = 2k+2 = 2(k+1)$, which is also even.

But $N+2 > N$, contradicting the assumption that $N$ is the largest even integer. $\square$
Q5 — STEP-Style [6 marks]

Prove that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational. You may assume that if $p^2$ is divisible by 3, then $p$ is divisible by 3.

Assume for contradiction that $\sqrt{3}$ is rational. Write $\sqrt{3} = \dfrac{p}{q}$ in lowest terms (so $\gcd(p,q)=1$).

Then $3 = \dfrac{p^2}{q^2}$, so $p^2 = 3q^2$.

Thus $p^2$ is divisible by 3. By the given result, $p$ is divisible by 3. Write $p = 3m$.

Then $9m^2 = 3q^2 \Rightarrow q^2 = 3m^2$, so $q^2$ is divisible by 3, and hence $q$ is divisible by 3.

But then both $p$ and $q$ are divisible by 3, contradicting $\gcd(p,q)=1$. Therefore $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational. $\square$
