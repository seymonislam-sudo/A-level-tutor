Ethics & Computing Professionals

Ethics is concerned with what is morally right and wrong. Computing raises unique ethical challenges because software and systems can affect millions of people, often in ways their creators didn't anticipate.

```

Ethical Frameworks

FrameworkCore ideaApplied to computing
UtilitarianismThe right action maximises overall happiness/benefitCollect user data to improve services for all users — justifiable if benefit outweighs harm
Deontology (Kantian)Some actions are right or wrong regardless of consequences — follow universal rulesNever deceive users, even if deception would produce better outcomes
Virtue ethicsFocus on character — what would a person of good character do?A developer with integrity refuses to build a surveillance tool even if legal

Professional Codes of Conduct

Organisations like the BCS (British Computer Society) publish codes of conduct for computing professionals. Key principles include:

BCS Code of Conduct — Key Principles Public interest: act in the best interests of society, not just your employer.
Competence: only work within your areas of expertise; continue professional development.
Integrity: be honest and avoid conflicts of interest.
Professionalism: uphold the reputation of the computing profession.

Whistleblowing

When a computing professional discovers their employer is acting unethically or illegally, they face a dilemma: stay silent, raise internally, or expose publicly. Edward Snowden (NSA surveillance) is a famous example. Whistleblowing is legally protected in the UK in many circumstances but carries significant personal risk.

```

UK Legislation

AQA requires knowledge of the key UK laws governing computing. These are regularly tested in short-answer and extended questions.

```
Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR Governs how personal data must be collected, stored and processed. Key principles: data must be collected lawfully with consent; used only for its stated purpose; kept accurate and up to date; retained no longer than necessary; kept secure; not transferred outside the UK without adequate protection. Individuals have the right to access their data, request deletion ("right to be forgotten"), and object to processing. The ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) enforces the Act; fines can reach £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover.
Computer Misuse Act 1990 Three offences: (1) Unauthorised access to a computer system — up to 2 years imprisonment; (2) Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences — up to 5 years; (3) Unauthorised modification of computer material (including viruses, ransomware) — up to 10 years. Amended to cover denial-of-service attacks and supply of hacking tools.
Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 Software is protected by copyright from the moment it is created — no registration required. Copying, distributing or modifying software without a licence is illegal. Applies to code, databases, digital media. Duration: typically life of author + 70 years.
Freedom of Information Act 2000 Public authorities (government, NHS, universities) must release information when requested, subject to exemptions (national security, personal data, commercial sensitivity). Promotes transparency and government accountability.
ScenarioRelevant law
A hacker accesses a bank's servers without permissionComputer Misuse Act 1990 (offence 1)
A company sells customer email addresses to third parties without consentData Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR
Someone downloads and distributes pirated softwareCopyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
A journalist requests government documents about a policy decisionFreedom of Information Act 2000
An employee installs ransomware on a company networkComputer Misuse Act 1990 (offence 3)
```

Privacy, Surveillance & Personal Data

Digital technology has fundamentally changed the nature of privacy. Every online interaction generates data — and that data has enormous value.

```

How Personal Data is Collected

MethodExamples
Cookies & tracking pixelsAdvertisers track browsing across sites to build profiles
Social mediaLikes, shares, location check-ins, connections reveal personality and behaviour
Smartphone sensorsGPS, accelerometer, microphone, camera — constant location and activity tracking
Smart devices (IoT)Smart speakers, TVs, and appliances collect usage patterns
CCTV & facial recognitionPublic and private spaces monitor movement and identity
Loyalty cards & purchasesPurchasing history reveals preferences, health conditions, life changes

The Privacy Debate

Arguments for surveillance

  • Prevents and detects terrorism and serious crime
  • "Nothing to hide, nothing to fear" — law-abiding citizens unaffected
  • Enables personalised services that users value
  • Targeted advertising funds free internet services
  • Contact tracing during pandemics saves lives

Arguments against surveillance

  • Chilling effect — people self-censor knowing they're watched
  • Data breaches expose millions to identity theft
  • Algorithms can discriminate by race, gender, income
  • Mass data collection creates authoritarian infrastructure
  • Users cannot meaningfully consent when services are essential
The Digital Divide Not everyone has equal access to technology — this gap is the "digital divide." Factors include income, geography (rural broadband), age, disability, and digital literacy. As more services move online (banking, healthcare, government), those without access are increasingly excluded. This is a significant equity and social justice issue.
```

AI, Automation & the Future of Work

Artificial intelligence and automation are transforming industries at unprecedented speed. This raises profound questions about employment, fairness, accountability, and human agency.

```

Economic Impact of Automation

Optimistic view

  • Automation frees humans from dangerous, repetitive work
  • New industries and jobs created (AI development, data science)
  • Increased productivity lowers prices for consumers
  • Historical precedent: Industrial Revolution created more jobs than it destroyed

Pessimistic view

  • This wave is different — AI can replace cognitive tasks, not just physical
  • Job losses concentrated in communities with fewer alternatives
  • New jobs require skills displaced workers don't have
  • Benefits accrue to capital owners, not workers

Algorithmic Bias & Fairness

AI systems trained on historical data can perpetuate and amplify existing biases. Real documented cases include:

SystemBias foundImpact
Facial recognitionHigher error rates for darker skin tones, womenWrongful arrests; misidentification
Recruiting algorithmsPenalised CVs containing the word "women's" (e.g. women's chess)Discriminatory hiring
Criminal risk scoringHigher risk scores for Black defendants in US courtsLonger sentences; reduced parole
Credit scoringZip code used as proxy for raceUnequal loan access

Autonomous Systems & Accountability

As AI makes decisions with real consequences — self-driving cars, medical diagnosis, loan approvals — the question of accountability becomes critical. If an autonomous vehicle kills a pedestrian, who is responsible — the manufacturer, the programmer, the user, or the AI itself? Current law struggles with this question.

The Trolley Problem in AI Self-driving cars must be programmed to make life-or-death decisions. If a crash is unavoidable, should the car protect passengers or minimise total casualties? This is not a technical question — it is an ethical one, and someone must decide. This illustrates why ethics cannot be an afterthought in computing.
```

Environmental Impact

The computing industry has a significant and growing environmental footprint, often overlooked in discussions of technology's benefits.

```
SourceEnvironmental impact
Data centresConsume ~1–2% of global electricity; large water usage for cooling; often located for cheap power, not renewables
Cryptocurrency miningBitcoin alone uses more electricity than some countries; primarily proof-of-work is energy intensive
AI trainingTraining large language models produces carbon equivalent to multiple transatlantic flights
ManufacturingRare earth metals, water and energy in chip fabrication; significant CO₂ emissions
E-waste50+ million tonnes of electronic waste annually; toxic metals; poor recycling rates globally
Streaming videoAccounts for significant internet traffic; associated data centre and network energy use

Positive Contributions

Computing also enables environmental solutions: smart grids optimise energy distribution; sensors monitor deforestation and pollution; AI optimises logistics to cut fuel use; remote working reduces commuting. The net impact depends on how the technology is powered and deployed.

Circular Economy & E-waste The EU and UK are introducing legislation requiring manufacturers to make devices repairable and components recyclable. Right-to-repair legislation lets consumers fix devices without voiding warranties. Extending device lifetimes by even a year significantly reduces embodied carbon per device-year.
```

Open Source, Licensing & Intellectual Property

```

Software Licensing Types

Licence typeDescriptionExamples
Proprietary / Closed sourceSource code kept secret; use governed by EULA; cannot be modified or redistributedMicrosoft Windows, Adobe Photoshop
Open source (permissive)Source available; can be used, modified and redistributed freely; can be incorporated into commercial productsMIT, Apache, BSD licences; Linux kernel
Copyleft (GPL)Source available; any derivative work must also be open source — "share-alike"GNU GPL; Linux (GPL), WordPress
FreewareFree to use but source not available; may have restrictions on commercial useVLC (actually open source), many browser extensions
SharewareFree trial with limitations; must pay for full versionWinRAR, many games

Open source advantages

  • Peer review improves security — many eyes on code
  • Community-driven innovation and bug fixes
  • No vendor lock-in; customisable to specific needs
  • Free to use; lowers costs for individuals and organisations
  • Promotes digital inclusion globally

Open source disadvantages

  • Reduced commercial incentive to develop new features
  • Support often volunteer-based; no SLA guarantee
  • Source code also available to malicious actors
  • Fragmentation of forks; version compatibility issues
  • Documentation often worse than commercial products

Intellectual Property

TypeWhat it protectsDuration
CopyrightCreative works — code, writing, music, artLife + 70 years
PatentNovel inventions and processesUp to 20 years
TrademarkBrand names and logosIndefinite (renewed)
Trade secretConfidential business information (e.g. algorithms)Indefinite while secret
Software Patents — Controversial In the US, software algorithms can be patented. In the UK and EU, "pure" software patents are harder to obtain. Critics argue software patents stifle innovation and allow large companies to sue smaller competitors. Supporters say patents reward investment in R&D.
```
