The Eye

The eye is an optical instrument. Understanding its structure and optics is central to Medical Physics.

Structure and function

PartFunction
CorneaMain refracting surface — provides ~70% of total refractive power
LensFine-tunes focus by changing shape (accommodation)
RetinaContains rods (low light) and cones (colour) — converts light to nerve signals
FoveaRegion of highest cone density — sharpest vision
Iris/PupilControls amount of light entering the eye

Near point and far point

  • Near point — closest point the eye can focus on (~25 cm for a normal eye)
  • Far point — furthest point the eye can focus on (infinity for a normal eye)

Defects of vision

DefectCauseCorrection
Myopia (short sight)Eye too long — image forms in front of retinaDiverging (concave) lens
Hyperopia (long sight)Eye too short — image forms behind retinaConverging (convex) lens
AstigmatismNon-spherical cornea — different focal lengths in different planesCylindrical lens
PresbyopiaLoss of accommodation with age — lens becomes rigidReading glasses (converging)
$$P = \frac{1}{f} \qquad P = P_1 + P_2$$

Power $P$ in dioptres (D), focal length $f$ in metres. Lenses in contact: powers add.

▶ Worked Example

A myopic eye has a far point of 2.0 m. What power of correcting lens is needed?

1
The lens must form a virtual image of a distant object (at infinity) at the far point (2.0 m).
2
Using $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u}$: object at $u = \infty$, image at $v = -2.0$ m (virtual, same side as object).
3
$P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{-2.0} = -0.50\ \text{D}$ — a diverging lens of power −0.50 D.

X-rays

Production

X-rays are produced in an X-ray tube: electrons are accelerated through a high voltage and decelerate rapidly on hitting a metal (tungsten) target, emitting X-ray photons (Bremsstrahlung). Characteristic X-rays are also produced when inner-shell electrons are knocked out and higher-energy electrons fall to fill the gaps.

$$eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = hf_{\max} = \frac{hc}{\lambda_{\min}}$$

Maximum photon energy equals the kinetic energy of the electron. $V$ = accelerating voltage.

Attenuation

As X-rays pass through matter, their intensity decreases exponentially:

$$I = I_0 e^{-\mu x}$$

$I_0$ = incident intensity, $\mu$ = linear attenuation coefficient (m⁻¹), $x$ = thickness (m). The half-value thickness $x_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\mu}$.

Contrast and Image Quality Bones have higher $\mu$ than soft tissue — they absorb more X-rays and appear white on the image. Contrast agents (barium, iodine) increase $\mu$ in soft tissues (gut, blood vessels) to make them visible.
▶ Worked Example

X-rays of intensity $I_0$ pass through 4.0 cm of tissue with $\mu = 12\ \text{m}^{-1}$. Find the transmitted intensity as a fraction of $I_0$.

1
Convert: $x = 4.0\ \text{cm} = 0.040\ \text{m}$
2
$I = I_0 e^{-12 \times 0.040} = I_0 e^{-0.48} = 0.619\, I_0$
3
About 62% of the X-rays are transmitted.

Ultrasound

Ultrasound uses sound waves with frequency above 20 kHz (typically 1–15 MHz in medical imaging). Pulses are emitted and reflections (echoes) are detected — the time delay gives depth information.

$$d = \frac{v \cdot t}{2}$$

$d$ = depth of reflecting surface, $v$ = speed of sound in tissue (~1500 m s⁻¹), $t$ = time for pulse to travel to surface and back.

Acoustic impedance

The fraction of ultrasound reflected at a boundary depends on the acoustic impedance $Z$ of each medium:

$$Z = \rho v \qquad \frac{I_r}{I_i} = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^2$$

$\rho$ = density, $v$ = speed of sound. Large impedance mismatch → most energy reflected. Gel is used to match impedance between transducer and skin.

A-scan vs B-scan A-scan (amplitude scan): displays echo amplitude vs time — used in ophthalmology for eye measurements.
B-scan (brightness scan): many A-scans combined to produce a 2D image — used in obstetrics, abdominal imaging.

Radioactivity in Medicine

Diagnosis — Gamma cameras and PET

Radioactive tracers emitting gamma rays are injected and accumulate in specific organs. A gamma camera detects the gamma radiation to produce an image of organ function.

In a PET scan, a positron-emitting tracer (e.g. fluorine-18) is used. Positrons annihilate with electrons producing two gamma photons travelling in opposite directions — detected simultaneously by a ring of detectors.

Choice of Tracer An ideal medical tracer should:
  • Emit gamma rays only (not alpha or beta — they would be absorbed internally and damage tissue)
  • Have a short half-life (reduces patient dose) but long enough to complete the scan
  • Be chemically taken up by the target organ
  • Technetium-99m is the most widely used — $t_{1/2} = 6.0\ \text{hours}$, pure gamma emitter

Therapy — Radiotherapy

High-energy radiation (gamma or X-rays) is used to destroy cancer cells. The beam is directed at the tumour from multiple angles so the healthy tissue receives a lower dose while the tumour receives a high cumulative dose.

$$A = A_0 e^{-\lambda t} = A_0 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/t_{1/2}}$$

Activity $A$ (Bq), decay constant $\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}}$, half-life $t_{1/2}$.
