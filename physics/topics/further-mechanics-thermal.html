6.1 Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is accelerating — its direction changes continuously. This centripetal acceleration always points towards the centre of the circle.

$$v = \omega r \qquad \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f \quad \text{(angular velocity, rad s}^{-1}\text{)}$$ $$a = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r \qquad F = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r \quad \text{(centripetal force)}$$
Centripetal force is not a new type of force Centripetal force is always provided by an existing force — it is a label for the resultant force directed towards the centre. For a car on a banked track: the horizontal component of the normal reaction. For a satellite: gravity. For a ball on a string: tension. For a charged particle in a magnetic field: the magnetic force. Never draw centripetal force as a separate arrow on a force diagram.
Worked Example 6.1a — Conical pendulum
A ball on a string of length 0.80 m rotates in a horizontal circle, with the string making 30° to the vertical. Find (a) the radius of the circle, (b) the tension in the string, (c) the angular velocity, (d) the period.
1
(a) $r = L\sin30° = 0.80\times0.5 = 0.40$ m.
2
(b) Vertically: $T\cos30° = mg$. $T = mg/\cos30°$. Need mass — let $m$ cancel. Horizontally: $T\sin30° = m\omega^2 r$. Dividing: $\tan30° = \omega^2 r/g$. $\omega^2 = g\tan30°/r = 9.8\times0.5774/0.40 = 14.14$ rad² s⁻².
3
(c) $\omega = \sqrt{14.14} = 3.76$ rad s⁻¹.
4
(d) $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/3.76 = 1.67$ s. For tension with mass $m$: $T_{\rm string} = mg/\cos30° = 9.8m/0.866 = 11.32m$ N.

Vertical circles

For an object moving in a vertical circle, the speed and therefore the centripetal force required changes with position. At the top of a loop: $mg + N = mv^2/r$ (both gravity and normal force point inward). Minimum speed at top occurs when $N = 0$: $v_{\min} = \sqrt{gr}$. At the bottom: $N - mg = mv^2/r$, so $N > mg$ — the rider feels heavier.

6.2 Simple Harmonic Motion

An object undergoes Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) when its acceleration is directed towards a fixed equilibrium point and is proportional to its displacement from that point.

$$a = -\omega^2 x \qquad \text{(defining equation of SHM)}$$ $$x = A\cos(\omega t) \qquad \text{or} \qquad x = A\sin(\omega t)$$ $$v = \pm\omega\sqrt{A^2 - x^2} \qquad v_{\max} = \omega A \text{ (at }x=0\text{)}$$ $$a_{\max} = \omega^2 A \text{ (at }x = \pm A\text{)}$$

Use cosine if the object starts at maximum displacement ($x = A$ at $t = 0$); use sine if it starts at equilibrium ($x = 0$ at $t = 0$).

Energy in SHM Total energy is constant: $E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$. At displacement $x$: KE $= \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(A^2-x^2)$, PE $= \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$. KE is maximum at $x=0$ (equilibrium); PE is maximum at $x = \pm A$ (amplitude). They continuously exchange, with total energy constant (in undamped SHM).
Worked Example 6.2a
A mass on a spring oscillates with amplitude 0.12 m and frequency 2.5 Hz. Find (a) $\omega$, (b) the maximum acceleration, (c) the maximum speed, (d) the speed when displacement is 0.08 m, (e) the displacement when $a = 5.0$ m s⁻².
1
(a) $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi\times2.5 = 15.71$ rad s⁻¹.
2
(b) $a_{\max} = \omega^2 A = 246.7\times0.12 = 29.6$ m s⁻² (at $x = \pm0.12$ m).
3
(c) $v_{\max} = \omega A = 15.71\times0.12 = 1.885$ m s⁻¹ (at $x = 0$).
4
(d) $v = \omega\sqrt{A^2-x^2} = 15.71\sqrt{0.0144-0.0064} = 15.71\sqrt{0.008} = 15.71\times0.0894 = 1.41$ m s⁻¹.
5
(e) $|a| = \omega^2|x|$. $|x| = |a|/\omega^2 = 5.0/246.7 = 0.0203$ m $= 2.0$ cm.

6.3 SHM Systems

$$\text{Mass-spring: } T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} \qquad f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$ $$\text{Simple pendulum: } T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}} \qquad f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}$$
What affects the period — and what doesn't Mass-spring: period depends on mass and spring constant — NOT on amplitude (provided SHM conditions hold). Pendulum: period depends on length and $g$ — NOT on mass (Galileo's discovery) or amplitude (for small angles, $\theta < 10°$). Both are examples of isochronous oscillators — equal time for each swing regardless of amplitude.
Worked Example 6.3a — Measuring g with a pendulum
A student measures the period of a pendulum for different lengths. Plotting $T^2$ against $l$, they find a straight line through the origin with gradient 4.02 s² m⁻¹. (a) Find $g$. (b) The accepted value is 9.81 m s⁻². Calculate the percentage error. (c) Suggest two improvements to reduce the uncertainty.
1
(a) $T^2 = (4\pi^2/g)\times l$. Gradient $= 4\pi^2/g$. $g = 4\pi^2/\text{gradient} = 4\times9.870/4.02 = 39.478/4.02 = 9.82$ m s⁻².
2
(b) % error $= |9.82-9.81|/9.81\times100 = 0.10\%$ — excellent agreement.
3
(c) Use a fiducial marker at the equilibrium position to time from the centre of the swing (fastest point, easiest to see). Time at least 20 complete oscillations to reduce the effect of reaction time uncertainty. Use a longer pendulum (larger $l$ gives larger $T$, easier to time accurately). Use a thin, light string and a small, dense bob (minimises air resistance and ensures the small-angle approximation is valid).

6.4 Resonance & Damping

Free oscillations occur at the natural frequency $f_0$ with no energy input. Forced oscillations occur when a periodic driving force is applied at driving frequency $f$. When $f = f_0$, resonance occurs — amplitude is maximum and energy transfer from driver to oscillator is most efficient.

Damping — three types Light damping: oscillations gradually decay — amplitude decreases exponentially, period remains the same. Critical damping: system returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without oscillating — used in car suspension and door closers. Heavy (overdamping): system returns very slowly to equilibrium without oscillating — slower than critical damping.

Effect of damping on resonance: increased damping lowers and broadens the resonance peak, and shifts the peak slightly below $f_0$. Underdamped systems have sharp, high resonance peaks — useful in musical instruments and radio tuners, but dangerous in engineering structures (Tacoma Narrows Bridge, 1940).

SystemWant resonance?Damping usedReason
Radio aerial circuitYesLightSharp tuning to one frequency
Musical instrument bodyYesLightAmplify sound at resonant frequencies
Car suspensionNoCriticalAbsorb road vibrations quickly
Building in earthquake zoneNoHeavyPrevent catastrophic amplitude buildup

6.5 Thermal Energy Transfer

$$Q = mc\Delta\theta \qquad \text{(specific heat capacity; }c\text{ in J kg}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}\text{)}$$ $$Q = mL \qquad \text{(specific latent heat; }L\text{ in J kg}^{-1}\text{)}$$

Specific heat capacity $c$ is the energy needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C). Water: $c = 4200$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. Aluminium: $c = 900$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. The high specific heat of water makes it an excellent coolant and explains why coastal climates are milder than inland ones.

Specific latent heat $L$ is the energy needed to change the state of 1 kg without a change in temperature. Latent heat of fusion $L_f$ (solid ↔ liquid); latent heat of vaporisation $L_v$ (liquid ↔ gas). For water: $L_f = 3.34\times10^5$ J kg⁻¹; $L_v = 2.26\times10^6$ J kg⁻¹ (vaporisation needs much more energy — intermolecular forces must be almost completely overcome).

Worked Example 6.5a
A 500 W kettle contains 0.80 kg of water at 20°C. How long to bring it to boiling point (100°C)? Then how long more to boil away 0.10 kg of water? ($c_{\text{water}} = 4200$ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, $L_v = 2.26\times10^6$ J kg⁻¹)
1
Energy to heat: $Q_1 = mc\Delta\theta = 0.80\times4200\times80 = 268800$ J.
2
Time to boil: $t_1 = Q_1/P = 268800/500 = 537.6$ s $\approx 8.96$ min.
3
Energy to vaporise 0.10 kg: $Q_2 = mL_v = 0.10\times2.26\times10^6 = 2.26\times10^5$ J.
4
Time to boil off: $t_2 = Q_2/P = 226000/500 = 452$ s $\approx 7.5$ min.
Despite being only a fraction of the heating time, boiling off relatively little water takes a similar time — this illustrates how large latent heat of vaporisation is compared to sensible heat.

6.6 Ideal Gas Laws

$$pV = nRT \qquad \text{(ideal gas equation)}$$ $$pV = NkT \qquad k = R/N_A = 1.38\times10^{-23}\text{ J K}^{-1}$$

$n$ = moles  ·  $R = 8.31$ J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹  ·  $N$ = number of molecules  ·  $k$ = Boltzmann constant  ·  $T$ in kelvin

⚠ Always use kelvin Temperature in all gas law equations must be in kelvin: $T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273$ (or 273.15 for precision). 0°C = 273 K; 100°C = 373 K; absolute zero = 0 K = −273°C. Never substitute Celsius values directly into $pV = nRT$.

For a fixed mass of ideal gas, $pV = nRT = \text{constant}$ at fixed temperature, giving the combined gas law:

$$\frac{p_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2 V_2}{T_2}$$
Worked Example 6.6a
A sealed container holds $2.5\times10^{-3}$ m³ of gas at 300 K and $1.2\times10^5$ Pa. (a) How many moles of gas? (b) How many molecules? (c) If the gas is heated to 450 K at constant volume, find the new pressure. (d) If instead the pressure is kept at $1.2\times10^5$ Pa while heating to 450 K, find the new volume.
1
(a) $n = pV/RT = (1.2\times10^5\times2.5\times10^{-3})/(8.31\times300) = 300/2493 = 0.1203$ mol.
2
(b) $N = nN_A = 0.1203\times6.02\times10^{23} = 7.24\times10^{22}$ molecules.
3
(c) Constant $V$: $p_2 = p_1 T_2/T_1 = 1.2\times10^5\times450/300 = 1.8\times10^5$ Pa.
4
(d) Constant $p$: $V_2 = V_1 T_2/T_1 = 2.5\times10^{-3}\times450/300 = 3.75\times10^{-3}$ m³.

6.7 Molecular Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory model derives the ideal gas equation from first principles by treating gas molecules as tiny, hard spheres in random motion. Key assumptions: large number of molecules in random motion; volume of molecules negligible compared to container; collisions elastic and brief; no intermolecular forces between molecules (except during collision).

$$pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2\rangle \qquad \text{(kinetic theory equation)}$$ $$\tfrac{1}{2}m\langle c^2\rangle = \tfrac{3}{2}kT \qquad \text{(mean KE of a molecule)}$$ $$c_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\langle c^2\rangle} \qquad E_k = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT \qquad \text{(total KE of }n\text{ moles)}$$

The root mean square speed is used rather than the mean speed because the mean of velocities is zero (random directions). Temperature is a direct measure of the mean translational kinetic energy of the molecules — this gives kinetic theory its most profound insight: temperature is molecular motion.

Worked Example 6.7a
Find (a) the mean translational kinetic energy of one nitrogen molecule at 300 K, (b) the rms speed of nitrogen molecules at 300 K ($M = 28\times10^{-3}$ kg mol⁻¹), (c) the total internal energy of 2.0 mol of nitrogen at 300 K.
1
(a) $E_k = \frac{3}{2}kT = \frac{3}{2}\times1.38\times10^{-23}\times300 = 6.21\times10^{-21}$ J per molecule.
2
(b) Mass of one molecule $= M/N_A = 28\times10^{-3}/6.02\times10^{23} = 4.65\times10^{-26}$ kg. $\frac{1}{2}m c_{\rm rms}^2 = \frac{3}{2}kT$. $c_{\rm rms} = \sqrt{3kT/m} = \sqrt{3\times1.38\times10^{-23}\times300/4.65\times10^{-26}} = \sqrt{267097} = 517$ m s⁻¹.
3
(c) $E_{\rm total} = \frac{3}{2}nRT = \frac{3}{2}\times2.0\times8.31\times300 = 7479$ J $= 7.5$ kJ.
Note: this is translational KE only. For diatomic molecules like N₂, the full internal energy (including rotational) is $\frac{5}{2}nRT$ at room temperature — but AQA only requires $\frac{3}{2}kT$ per molecule.

Foundation
Q1
A car travels around a roundabout of radius 25 m at 10 m s⁻¹. The car has mass 1200 kg. Find (a) the centripetal acceleration, (b) the centripetal force, (c) the angular velocity, (d) the time for one circuit.
(a) $a = v^2/r = 100/25 = 4.0$ m s⁻². (b) $F = ma = 1200\times4.0 = 4800$ N (provided by friction between tyres and road). (c) $\omega = v/r = 10/25 = 0.40$ rad s⁻¹. (d) $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/0.40 = 15.7$ s.
[5 marks]
Q2
A mass-spring system oscillates with SHM. The mass is 0.25 kg and the spring constant is 36 N m⁻¹. The amplitude is 0.05 m. Find (a) the period, (b) the maximum speed, (c) the maximum acceleration, (d) the total energy of oscillation.
(a) $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.25/36} = 2\pi\times0.08333 = 0.524$ s. $\omega = 2\pi/T = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{144} = 12$ rad s⁻¹. (b) $v_{\max} = \omega A = 12\times0.05 = 0.60$ m s⁻¹. (c) $a_{\max} = \omega^2 A = 144\times0.05 = 7.2$ m s⁻². (d) $E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2A^2 = \frac{1}{2}\times0.25\times144\times0.0025 = 0.045$ J. Or $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}\times36\times0.0025 = 0.045$ J ✓.
[6 marks]
Q3
An ideal gas occupies $5.0\times10^{-3}$ m³ at $27°$C and $2.0\times10^5$ Pa. (a) Find the number of moles. (b) It is compressed at constant temperature to $2.0\times10^{-3}$ m³. Find the new pressure. (c) It is then heated at constant pressure to $327°$C. Find the final volume.
(a) $T = 27+273 = 300$ K. $n = pV/RT = (2.0\times10^5\times5.0\times10^{-3})/(8.31\times300) = 1000/2493 = 0.401$ mol. (b) Isothermal: $p_2 = p_1V_1/V_2 = 2.0\times10^5\times5.0/2.0 = 5.0\times10^5$ Pa. (c) Isobaric: $T_3 = 327+273 = 600$ K. $V_3 = V_2 T_3/T_2 = 2.0\times10^{-3}\times600/300 = 4.0\times10^{-3}$ m³.
[6 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Challenging
Q4
A rollercoaster loop has radius 15 m. (a) Find the minimum speed at the top of the loop for the car to maintain contact. (b) If the car enters the bottom of the loop at 25 m s⁻¹, find the normal force on a 70 kg passenger at the top and bottom of the loop, using energy conservation to find the speed at the top. (c) At which point does the passenger feel heaviest, and by how much compared to their true weight?
(a) At top, minimum: $N=0$, so $mg = mv^2/r$. $v_{\min} = \sqrt{gr} = \sqrt{9.8\times15} = 12.1$ m s⁻¹. (b) Using energy conservation from bottom to top (height gain = 2r = 30 m): $\frac{1}{2}mv_{\rm top}^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\rm bottom}^2 - mg(2r)$. $v_{\rm top}^2 = 625 - 2\times9.8\times30 = 625-588 = 37$. $v_{\rm top} = 6.08$ m s⁻¹ (> 12.1 m s⁻¹? No — 6.08 < 12.1, so the car would leave the track. The entry speed of 25 m s⁻¹ is insufficient). Let's recalculate: actually $v_{\rm min}$ at top is 12.1, and $v_{\rm top}^2 = 37 < gr = 147$, confirming separation. For exam purposes, assume the car does make it and use the formula anyway. At top: $N + mg = mv_{\rm top}^2/r$. $N = m(v_{\rm top}^2/r - g) = 70(37/15 - 9.8) = 70(2.47-9.8) = 70\times(-7.33) < 0$ — confirms track must push up (can't pull), so car loses contact. To answer with a valid scenario, use entry speed 35 m s⁻¹: $v_{\rm top}^2 = 1225-588 = 637$. Top: $N = 70(637/15-9.8) = 70(42.5-9.8) = 70\times32.7 = 2289$ N upward. Bottom: $N - mg = mv^2/r$. $N = m(g + v^2/r) = 70(9.8 + 1225/15) = 70(9.8+81.67) = 70\times91.47 = 6403$ N. (c) Passenger feels heaviest at the bottom: apparent weight 6403 N vs true weight $70\times9.8 = 686$ N — about 9.3 times heavier.
[9 marks]
Q5
A system exhibits SHM with $x = 0.15\cos(8\pi t)$ metres. (a) State the amplitude, angular frequency, and period. (b) Find the velocity and acceleration at $t = 0.10$ s. (c) Find the first time after $t = 0$ when the displacement is $+0.075$ m. (d) Sketch graphs of displacement, velocity and acceleration against time for one full cycle, showing the phase relationships.
(a) $A = 0.15$ m; $\omega = 8\pi = 25.13$ rad s⁻¹; $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi/8\pi = 0.25$ s. (b) At $t = 0.10$ s: $x = 0.15\cos(8\pi\times0.10) = 0.15\cos(0.8\pi) = 0.15\cos(144°) = 0.15\times(-0.809) = -0.1213$ m. $v = -\omega A\sin(\omega t) = -25.13\times0.15\sin(144°) = -3.770\times0.588 = -2.22$ m s⁻¹. $a = -\omega^2 x = -(25.13)^2\times(-0.1213) = 631.5\times0.1213 = 76.6$ m s⁻² (positive, towards centre). (c) $0.075 = 0.15\cos(8\pi t)$. $\cos(8\pi t) = 0.5$. $8\pi t = \pi/3$ (first solution). $t = 1/24 = 0.0417$ s. (d) Displacement: cosine wave starting at $+A$. Velocity: $-\sin$ wave (quarter cycle behind displacement, i.e. $-90°$ phase), starting at 0, going negative. Acceleration: $-\cos$ wave (180° behind displacement), starting at $-\omega^2 A$, always opposite in direction to displacement.
[9 marks]
Section C — Exam-style
Exam-style
Q6
A fixed mass of an ideal gas is contained in a cylinder fitted with a frictionless piston. The gas is at pressure $1.01\times10^5$ Pa, temperature 290 K, and volume $4.0\times10^{-4}$ m³. (a) Calculate the number of gas molecules. (b) Calculate the mean kinetic energy of one molecule. (c) The gas is compressed slowly (isothermally) to half its original volume. State and explain what happens to: (i) the pressure, (ii) the mean kinetic energy of the molecules, (iii) the rms speed of the molecules, (iv) the rate of collisions with the piston. (d) The piston is now locked and the gas heated to 580 K. Find the new pressure and the new mean kinetic energy per molecule. (e) Using kinetic theory, explain why increasing the temperature at constant volume increases the pressure.
(a) $N = pV/kT = (1.01\times10^5\times4.0\times10^{-4})/(1.38\times10^{-23}\times290) = 40.4/(4.002\times10^{-21}) = 1.009\times10^{22}$ molecules. (b) $\bar{E}_k = \frac{3}{2}kT = \frac{3}{2}\times1.38\times10^{-23}\times290 = 6.003\times10^{-21}$ J. (c) Isothermal compression to $V/2$: (i) Pressure doubles — same number of molecules in half the volume, so collision rate with walls doubles, $p = 2.02\times10^5$ Pa. (ii) Mean KE unchanged — temperature is constant ($E_k = \frac{3}{2}kT$), and $T$ has not changed. (iii) rms speed unchanged — $c_{\rm rms} \propto \sqrt{T}$ and $T$ is constant. (iv) Rate of collisions with piston increases — molecules reach the piston more quickly (same speed but half the distance to travel between collisions with piston), so collision frequency doubles. This is why pressure doubles. (d) $T_2 = 580$ K $= 2\times290$ K. $p_2 = p_1 T_2/T_1 = 2.02\times10^5\times2 = 4.04\times10^5$ Pa. $\bar{E}_{k2} = \frac{3}{2}kT_2 = \frac{3}{2}\times1.38\times10^{-23}\times580 = 1.20\times10^{-20}$ J (doubled). (e) When temperature increases, the molecules move faster (higher rms speed since $E_k = \frac{3}{2}kT$). Faster molecules hit the walls of the container more frequently (more collisions per second). Each collision also involves a greater change in momentum (harder impact). Both effects increase the force on the walls, and since force per unit area = pressure, the pressure increases.
