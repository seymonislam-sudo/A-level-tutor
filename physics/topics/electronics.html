// 1 — Semiconductor Devices

Discrete Semiconductor Devices

Semiconductors have conductivity between conductors and insulators. Doping introduces free charge carriers: n-type semiconductors have donor atoms (extra electrons); p-type semiconductors have acceptor atoms (extra holes).

The p-n Junction Diode

At the junction, electrons diffuse from n to p and holes from p to n, creating a depletion region with an internal electric field. This gives a potential barrier (~0.6 V for silicon) opposing further diffusion.

BiasDepletion regionCurrent
Forward (p+, n−)NarrowsFlows freely above ~0.6 V
Reverse (p−, n+)Widens~0 (only tiny leakage)
Breakdown—Large current at high reverse voltage

The I–V characteristic is exponential for forward bias: $I = I_0(e^{eV/k_BT} - 1)$. In practice the "knee" at ~0.6 V is used.

Light-Emitting Diode (LED)

When forward biased, electrons and holes recombine at the junction, releasing energy as photons. The photon energy equals the band-gap energy of the semiconductor:

Photon energy from LED
$eV = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$

where $V$ is the forward voltage at onset of emission.

Different semiconductor materials give different colours (band gaps): GaAs ≈ infrared; GaP ≈ red/green; GaN ≈ blue/white.

The npn Transistor as a Switch & Amplifier

A bipolar junction transistor (BJT) has emitter (E), base (B) and collector (C). A small base current $I_B$ controls a much larger collector current $I_C$.

Current gain (hFE or β)
$h_{FE} = \frac{I_C}{I_B}$

Typical values: 100–500. Also $I_E = I_B + I_C$.

In the switch mode the transistor is either fully off (cut-off: $V_{BE} < 0.6\text{ V}$) or fully on (saturation). In amplifier mode it operates in the active region, amplifying $I_B$ into $I_C$.

// 2 — Capacitors in Electronic Circuits

Capacitors and RC Circuits

Capacitors block DC but pass AC — they are used extensively for coupling, smoothing, and filtering.

Smoothing a Rectified Supply

A capacitor across a load stores charge during each half-cycle and releases it when the diode is not conducting, smoothing the output. The ripple voltage depends on the RC time constant.

Approximate ripple voltage
$\Delta V \approx \frac{I}{fC} = \frac{V}{fCR}$

where $f$ is the frequency of the rectified supply. Larger $C$ → smaller ripple.

RC Time Constant

Charge / discharge equations
$V_C = V_0\,e^{-t/RC}$ (discharging)    $V_C = V_0(1-e^{-t/RC})$ (charging)

Time constant $\tau = RC$. After one $\tau$, voltage falls to $\approx 37\%$ of $V_0$.

Filters

Filter typeCircuitPasses
Low-passR in series, C to earthLow frequencies (≪ $1/RC$)
High-passC in series, R to earthHigh frequencies (≫ $1/RC$)

The cut-off (−3 dB) frequency is $f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$. Above/below $f_c$ the output amplitude falls to $1/\sqrt{2}$ of the input.

// 3 — Operational Amplifiers

The Ideal Op-Amp

An operational amplifier (op-amp) has two inputs: inverting (−) and non-inverting (+). Its output is:

Open-loop output
$V_{out} = A_{OL}(V_+ - V_-)$

$A_{OL}$ (open-loop gain) is typically $10^5$–$10^6$. The op-amp saturates at ≈ ±$V_{supply}$ for very small differential inputs.

Ideal Op-Amp Properties

The Golden Rules (for circuits with negative feedback)

When negative feedback is applied:

  1. The two inputs are at the same potential: $V_+ = V_-$ (virtual short)
  2. No current enters either input terminal (infinite input impedance)

These rules simplify circuit analysis enormously.

Inverting Amplifier

Resistor $R_{in}$ connects the signal to the inverting input; feedback resistor $R_f$ connects the output back to the inverting input. The non-inverting input is grounded.

Closed-loop gain
$A = -\frac{R_f}{R_{in}}$

Negative sign = phase inversion. Virtual earth: $V_- \approx 0$ because $V_+ = 0$.

Derivation using virtual earth: Current through $R_{in}$ is $I = V_{in}/R_{in}$. Since no current enters the op-amp input, the same current flows through $R_f$, giving $V_{out} = -IR_f = -(R_f/R_{in})V_{in}$.

Non-Inverting Amplifier

The signal connects to the non-inverting (+) input. $R_f$ and $R_1$ form a voltage-divider feedback network to the inverting input.

Closed-loop gain
$A = 1 + \frac{R_f}{R_1}$

Always $\geq 1$. Output is in phase with input.

Setting $R_1 \to \infty$ (open) and $R_f = 0$ gives $A = 1$ — this is the voltage follower (buffer), which has very high input impedance and very low output impedance, useful for impedance matching.

Comparator (Open-Loop Op-Amp)

Without feedback, the op-amp acts as a comparator. The output swings to $+V_{sat}$ or $-V_{sat}$ depending on the sign of $(V_+ - V_-)$. Used in threshold-detection circuits.

Example: light-activated switch — LDR voltage compared to a reference set by a potential divider. When light level falls, output switches, turning on an LED or relay.

// 4 — Negative Feedback & Bandwidth

Effects of Negative Feedback

PropertyEffect of negative feedback
GainReduced and stabilised (set by resistor ratio, not $A_{OL}$)
BandwidthIncreased — gain–bandwidth product is constant
DistortionReduced
Input impedanceIncreased (non-inverting) or set by $R_{in}$ (inverting)
Output impedanceDecreased

Gain–Bandwidth Product (GBP)

Constant for a given op-amp
$\text{GBP} = A_{CL} \times f_{-3\text{dB}} = \text{constant}$

Higher closed-loop gain → lower bandwidth. Example: GBP = 1 MHz, $A_{CL}$ = 100 → $f_{-3\text{dB}}$ = 10 kHz.

// 5 — Oscillators

The Phase-Shift Oscillator

An oscillator is an amplifier with positive feedback. Oscillations are sustained when the Barkhausen criterion is satisfied:

  1. Loop gain = 1 (amplifier gain × feedback fraction = 1)
  2. Total phase shift around the loop = 0° (or 360°)

A phase-shift oscillator uses a chain of three RC sections, each providing 60° of phase shift at the oscillation frequency, giving the required 180° (the inverting amplifier provides the other 180°).

Oscillation frequency (3-stage RC phase-shift)
$f = \frac{1}{2\pi RC\sqrt{6}}$

The amplifier gain must be at least 29 to overcome the attenuation of the RC network.

The Wien Bridge Oscillator

Uses a Wien network (series RC + parallel RC) as the positive feedback element. The non-inverting amplifier gain must equal exactly 3 to sustain oscillations.

Wien bridge oscillation frequency
$f = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}$

Required gain $A = 1 + R_f/R_1 = 3$, so $R_f = 2R_1$.

// 6 — Output Devices & Signal Processing

Output Devices

Relays

A relay uses a small control current through an electromagnet to switch a mechanically separate, high-power circuit. A protection diode (flyback diode) is connected in reverse-bias across the relay coil to suppress the back-EMF spike when current switches off.

Thermistors and LDRs as Sensors

NTC thermistors have resistance that decreases with increasing temperature. LDRs have resistance that decreases with increasing light intensity. Both are used in potential-divider circuits to produce a varying voltage input to a comparator or op-amp.

Piezoelectric Transducers

Some crystals (e.g. quartz) generate a voltage when mechanically stressed (piezoelectric effect), and conversely deform when a voltage is applied. Used as sensors (microphones, accelerometers) and actuators (buzzers, ultrasound transducers).

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

SNR in decibels
$\text{SNR (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P_{signal}}{P_{noise}}\right) = 20 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_{signal}}{V_{noise}}\right)$

Higher SNR = cleaner signal. Amplifying a signal also amplifies noise, so SNR does not improve with amplification.

// Practice Questions
Level A — Foundation
1. An LED requires a forward voltage of 1.9 V and is connected to a 9 V supply in series with a resistor. Calculate the required resistance if the operating current is 20 mA.
Voltage across resistor: $V_R = 9 - 1.9 = 7.1$ V
Resistance: $R = V_R / I = 7.1 / 0.020 = 355$ Ω
Use the nearest standard value (e.g. 360 Ω or 390 Ω).
Level A — Foundation
2. An inverting op-amp circuit has $R_{in} = 10\text{ k}\Omega$ and $R_f = 47\text{ k}\Omega$. The input voltage is 0.2 V. Find the output voltage and state the phase relationship with the input.
Gain: $A = -R_f/R_{in} = -47/10 = -4.7$
Output: $V_{out} = -4.7 \times 0.2 = -0.94$ V
The output is 180° out of phase (inverted) relative to the input. The magnitude is 0.94 V.
Level A — Foundation
3. State the two "golden rules" used when analysing op-amp circuits with negative feedback.
1. The voltage at the inverting and non-inverting inputs are equal: $V_+ = V_-$ (the inputs are at a virtual short circuit).

2. No current flows into either input terminal (the input impedance is infinite).
Level B — Standard
4. An op-amp has a gain–bandwidth product of 2.0 MHz. The feedback resistors are set to give a closed-loop gain of 50. (a) Calculate the −3 dB bandwidth. (b) If the gain is reduced to 10, what is the new bandwidth?
(a) $f_{-3\text{dB}} = \text{GBP}/A_{CL} = 2.0 \times 10^6 / 50 = 40\text{ kHz}$

(b) $f_{-3\text{dB}} = 2.0 \times 10^6 / 10 = 200\text{ kHz}$
Reducing gain by a factor of 5 increases bandwidth by a factor of 5.
Level B — Standard
5. A smoothing capacitor of 470 µF is used with a full-wave rectifier supplying a load of 1.2 kΩ from a 50 Hz AC supply. Estimate the peak-to-peak ripple voltage.
Full-wave rectifier: frequency of ripple = $2f = 100$ Hz.
$\Delta V = \frac{V}{fCR}$. We need $V$; assuming the supply provides $V_{peak} \approx V_0$:
$\Delta V = \frac{V_0}{100 \times 470 \times 10^{-6} \times 1200} = \frac{V_0}{56.4}$
For example, if $V_0 = 12$ V: $\Delta V \approx 12/56.4 \approx 0.21$ V (peak-to-peak).
The formula gives the ripple as a fraction of the supply voltage; the absolute value depends on $V_0$.
Level B — Standard
6. A npn transistor has $h_{FE} = 200$. The circuit requires a collector current of 40 mA to drive a relay. (a) What base current is needed? (b) If $V_{BE} = 0.6$ V and the base resistor connects to a 5 V logic output, calculate the base resistor value required.
(a) $I_B = I_C / h_{FE} = 40 \times 10^{-3} / 200 = 0.2$ mA = 200 µA

(b) Voltage across $R_B$: $V_{R_B} = 5 - 0.6 = 4.4$ V
$R_B = 4.4 / (0.2 \times 10^{-3}) = 22\text{ k}\Omega$
Level C — Extended
7. A Wien bridge oscillator uses $R = 8.2\text{ k}\Omega$ and $C = 10\text{ nF}$. (a) Calculate the frequency of oscillation. (b) What closed-loop gain is required and how would you set this using a non-inverting amplifier with $R_1 = 10\text{ k}\Omega$? (c) Explain what happens if the gain is set slightly too high or too low.
(a) $f = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 8200 \times 10 \times 10^{-9}} = \frac{1}{5.15 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 1.94\text{ kHz}$

(b) Required gain $A = 3$. For a non-inverting amplifier: $A = 1 + R_f/R_1$
$3 = 1 + R_f/10\text{ k}\Omega \Rightarrow R_f = 20\text{ k}\Omega$

(c) If gain $> 3$: the loop gain exceeds 1, so oscillations grow until the op-amp saturates, producing a clipped (distorted) output. If gain $< 3$: the loop gain is less than 1, oscillations are damped and die away. The gain must equal exactly 3 for sustained, undistorted sinusoidal oscillations.
Level C — Extended
8. An op-amp summing amplifier has three inputs with equal resistors $R_{in} = 10\text{ k}\Omega$ and feedback resistor $R_f = 10\text{ k}\Omega$. The inputs are $V_1 = +1.0$ V, $V_2 = +2.0$ V, $V_3 = -0.5$ V. (a) Derive the general output formula for a summing amplifier. (b) Calculate $V_{out}$. (c) If the op-amp is powered by ±12 V rails, will it saturate?
(a) Virtual earth means $V_- \approx 0$. Total current into the summing junction: $I = V_1/R_{in} + V_2/R_{in} + V_3/R_{in}$. All this current flows through $R_f$:
$V_{out} = -R_f\!\left(\frac{V_1}{R_{in}} + \frac{V_2}{R_{in}} + \frac{V_3}{R_{in}}\right) = -\frac{R_f}{R_{in}}(V_1 + V_2 + V_3)$

(b) With equal $R_f = R_{in}$: $V_{out} = -(1.0 + 2.0 - 0.5) = -2.5$ V

(c) $|V_{out}| = 2.5$ V which is much less than 12 V, so the op-amp operates in the linear region and does not saturate.
