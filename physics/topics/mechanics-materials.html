4.1 Scalars & Vectors

A scalar has magnitude only. A vector has both magnitude and direction. Getting this distinction right matters throughout the course.

ScalarsVectors
Distance, speed, mass, time, energy, power, temperature, pressureDisplacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, weight, electric field

Resolving & combining vectors

Any vector can be resolved into two perpendicular components. For a vector of magnitude $F$ at angle $\theta$ to the horizontal:

$$F_x = F\cos\theta \qquad F_y = F\sin\theta$$ $$\text{Resultant: } R = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2} \qquad \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right)$$
Worked Example 4.1a — Resolving forces
A 120 N force acts at 35° above the horizontal. Find its horizontal and vertical components. A second force of 80 N acts horizontally to the left. Find the magnitude and direction of the resultant.
1
$F_{1x} = 120\cos35° = 98.3$ N (right). $F_{1y} = 120\sin35° = 68.8$ N (up).
2
$F_{2x} = -80$ N (left). $F_{2y} = 0$. Total: $R_x = 98.3 - 80 = 18.3$ N. $R_y = 68.8$ N.
3
$R = \sqrt{18.3^2 + 68.8^2} = \sqrt{335 + 4733} = \sqrt{5068} = 71.2$ N. $\theta = \arctan(68.8/18.3) = 75.1°$ above horizontal.

4.2 Moments & Equilibrium

$$\text{Moment} = F \times d \qquad \text{(force × perpendicular distance from pivot)}$$ $$\text{Principle of moments: } \sum\text{clockwise moments} = \sum\text{anticlockwise moments}$$

For a body in equilibrium, two conditions must both be satisfied: (1) the resultant force is zero (no net translation), and (2) the resultant moment about any point is zero (no net rotation).

Couple A couple is a pair of equal, parallel, opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide. A couple produces rotation but no translation. The moment of a couple $= F \times d$ where $d$ is the perpendicular distance between the forces. Unlike a single force, the moment of a couple is the same about any point.
Worked Example 4.2a — Beam in equilibrium
A uniform beam of weight 200 N and length 4.0 m is supported at its ends by two vertical forces $R_A$ and $R_B$. A 500 N load hangs 1.5 m from end A. Find $R_A$ and $R_B$.
1
Take moments about A (eliminates $R_A$). Clockwise: $500\times1.5 + 200\times2.0 = 750 + 400 = 1150$ N m. Anticlockwise: $R_B\times4.0$.
2
$R_B = 1150/4.0 = 287.5$ N.
3
Vertical equilibrium: $R_A + R_B = 500 + 200 = 700$ N. $R_A = 700 - 287.5 = 412.5$ N.
Check by taking moments about B: $R_A\times4.0 = 412.5\times4 = 1650$. Clockwise: $500\times2.5 + 200\times2.0 = 1250+400=1650$ ✓

4.3 Motion & SUVAT Equations

For uniform acceleration (constant $a$), the four SUVAT equations relate displacement $s$, initial velocity $u$, final velocity $v$, acceleration $a$, and time $t$:

$$v = u + at$$ $$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$ $$v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$ $$s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u+v)t$$

Each equation contains 4 of the 5 quantities — identify which 3 you know and which you want to find, then choose the equation that doesn't involve the unknown you don't need.

Displacement-time and velocity-time graphs On a displacement-time graph: gradient = velocity; a curve means changing velocity. On a velocity-time graph: gradient = acceleration; area under graph = displacement. A horizontal line means constant velocity ($a = 0$). These graph interpretations are tested constantly.
Worked Example 4.3a — Braking car
A car travelling at 28 m s⁻¹ brakes with deceleration 6.5 m s⁻². Find (a) the time to stop, (b) the stopping distance, (c) the distance covered in the first 2.0 s of braking.
1
(a) $v = u + at$: $0 = 28 + (-6.5)t$. $t = 28/6.5 = 4.31$ s.
2
(b) $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$: $0 = 784 + 2(-6.5)s$. $s = 784/13 = 60.3$ m.
3
(c) $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 28(2.0) + \frac{1}{2}(-6.5)(4.0) = 56 - 13 = 43$ m.
Note: at $t=2$ s the car has decelerated from 28 to $28 - 6.5\times2 = 15$ m s⁻¹ — it's still moving, so using the full stopping distance would be wrong.

Free fall and $g$

In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same acceleration $g = 9.81$ m s⁻² (use $g = 9.8$ m s⁻² unless told otherwise). This was demonstrated by Galileo and is a direct consequence of the equivalence of inertial and gravitational mass.

4.4 Projectile Motion

A projectile moves under gravity alone (no air resistance). The horizontal and vertical motions are completely independent — treat them separately using SUVAT.

HorizontalVertical
Acceleration$0$ (constant velocity)$g = 9.8$ m s⁻² downward
Initial velocity$u_x = u\cos\theta$$u_y = u\sin\theta$
Velocity at time $t$$v_x = u_x$ (constant)$v_y = u_y - gt$
Displacement at time $t$$x = u_x t$$y = u_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$
Worked Example 4.4a — Ball launched at an angle
A ball is launched at 18 m s⁻¹ at 40° above the horizontal from flat ground. Find (a) the maximum height, (b) the time of flight, (c) the range, (d) the speed and direction of the ball after 1.5 s.
1
$u_x = 18\cos40° = 13.79$ m s⁻¹. $u_y = 18\sin40° = 11.57$ m s⁻¹.
2
(a) At max height, $v_y = 0$: $v_y^2 = u_y^2 - 2gh$. $h = u_y^2/(2g) = 11.57^2/19.6 = 6.83$ m.
3
(b) Time to max height: $t_{up} = u_y/g = 11.57/9.8 = 1.181$ s. Total time $= 2t_{up} = 2.36$ s (symmetric trajectory on flat ground).
4
(c) Range $= u_x \times T = 13.79\times2.36 = 32.5$ m.
5
(d) At $t=1.5$ s: $v_x = 13.79$ m s⁻¹. $v_y = 11.57 - 9.8\times1.5 = 11.57 - 14.7 = -3.13$ m s⁻¹ (downward). Speed $= \sqrt{13.79^2 + 3.13^2} = \sqrt{190.2+9.8} = \sqrt{200} = 14.1$ m s⁻¹. Direction: $\arctan(3.13/13.79) = 12.8°$ below horizontal.

4.5 Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton's Three Laws 1st Law: A body remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a resultant force. 2nd Law: The resultant force equals the rate of change of momentum: $F = ma$ (for constant mass). 3rd Law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction — forces always occur in pairs on different objects, of the same type.
$$F = ma \qquad \text{(Newton's 2nd law, constant mass)}$$ $$W = mg \qquad \text{(weight)}$$

On an object moving through a fluid, drag increases with speed. Eventually drag equals the driving force — terminal velocity is reached when $F_{\text{net}} = 0$ and acceleration $= 0$.

⚠ Newton's 3rd Law pairs — common misconceptions The reaction pair must be: same type of force (both gravitational, both normal, etc.), equal magnitude, opposite direction, acting on different objects. "Weight down, normal force up" on a book on a table are NOT a Newton's 3rd pair — they act on the same object (the book). The 3rd law pair of the book's weight (Earth pulling book down) is the book pulling Earth up gravitationally.
Worked Example 4.5a — Connected particles
Two masses $m_1 = 4.0$ kg and $m_2 = 6.0$ kg are connected by a light inextensible string over a frictionless pulley (Atwood machine). Find (a) the acceleration, (b) the tension in the string.
1
Net force on system: $(m_2 - m_1)g = (6.0-4.0)\times9.8 = 19.6$ N. Total mass $= 10$ kg.
2
(a) $a = F/m = 19.6/10 = 1.96$ m s⁻².
3
(b) For $m_1$ (accelerating up): $T - m_1 g = m_1 a$. $T = m_1(g+a) = 4.0\times(9.8+1.96) = 4.0\times11.76 = 47.0$ N.
Check with $m_2$ (accelerating down): $m_2 g - T = m_2 a$. $T = m_2(g-a) = 6.0\times7.84 = 47.0$ N ✓

4.6 Work, Energy & Power

$$W = Fs\cos\theta \qquad \text{(work done by force }F\text{ over displacement }s\text{)}$$ $$E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \qquad E_p = mgh$$ $$P = \frac{W}{t} = Fv \qquad \eta = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} \times 100\%$$

The work-energy theorem: the net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy: $W_{\text{net}} = \Delta E_k$. When no energy is lost to friction or other dissipative forces, total mechanical energy (KE + PE) is conserved.

Worked Example 4.6a — Roller coaster
A roller coaster car (mass 800 kg) starts from rest at the top of a 45 m drop. At the bottom, its speed is 26 m s⁻¹. (a) Find the energy lost to friction and air resistance. (b) Find the average resistive force over the 45 m drop.
1
$\Delta E_p = mgh = 800\times9.8\times45 = 352800$ J. $E_k = \frac{1}{2}\times800\times26^2 = 270400$ J.
2
(a) Energy lost $= \Delta E_p - E_k = 352800 - 270400 = 82400$ J $\approx 82$ kJ.
3
(b) $W_{\text{friction}} = F\times s$. $F = 82400/45 = 1831$ N $\approx 1.8$ kN.
This is ~23% of the car's weight — significant but reasonable for a real rollercoaster with aerodynamic drag.

4.7 Momentum & Collisions

$$p = mv \qquad \text{(momentum, kg m s}^{-1}\text{)}$$ $$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \qquad \text{(Newton's 2nd law in full form)}$$ $$\text{Impulse} = F\Delta t = \Delta p$$

Conservation of momentum: in a closed system (no external forces), total momentum is constant. This applies to all collisions.

Collision typeMomentumKinetic energy
ElasticConservedConserved
InelasticConservedNot conserved (lost to heat/deformation)
Perfectly inelasticConservedMaximum loss — objects stick together
Worked Example 4.7a — Collision
A 2.0 kg ball moving at 5.0 m s⁻¹ collides with a stationary 3.0 kg ball. After the collision, the 2.0 kg ball moves at 1.0 m s⁻¹ in the same direction. (a) Find the velocity of the 3.0 kg ball. (b) Determine whether the collision is elastic.
1
(a) Conservation of momentum: $2.0\times5.0 + 3.0\times0 = 2.0\times1.0 + 3.0\times v_2$. $10 = 2 + 3v_2$. $v_2 = 8/3 = 2.67$ m s⁻¹.
2
(b) KE before: $\frac{1}{2}(2.0)(5.0^2) = 25$ J. KE after: $\frac{1}{2}(2.0)(1.0^2) + \frac{1}{2}(3.0)(2.67^2) = 1 + 10.7 = 11.7$ J.
3
KE is not conserved (25 J ≠ 11.7 J) — the collision is inelastic. Energy lost $= 13.3$ J (converted to heat, sound, and deformation).
Impulse and safety Impulse $= \Delta p = F\Delta t$. For a given change in momentum, increasing the collision time $\Delta t$ reduces the average force $F$. This is the physics behind crumple zones, airbags, crash mats, and padded clothing — all extend the time over which momentum changes to reduce peak forces on the body.

4.8 Properties of Materials

Hooke's Law & springs

$$F = kx \qquad \text{(Hooke's Law: force proportional to extension)}$$ $$E = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}Fx \qquad \text{(elastic strain energy)}$$

Hooke's Law holds up to the limit of proportionality. Beyond this, the relationship becomes non-linear. The elastic limit is the point beyond which the material no longer returns to its original shape when the force is removed (permanent deformation occurs). The elastic limit is close to but slightly beyond the limit of proportionality.

Stress, strain and the Young modulus

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \quad \text{(stress, Pa)} \qquad \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \quad \text{(strain, dimensionless)}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{FL}{A\Delta L} \quad \text{(Young modulus, Pa)}$$
Stress-strain curve features The stress-strain curve for a ductile material (e.g. mild steel) shows: elastic region (straight line, Hooke's law applies), limit of proportionality, elastic limit, yield point (material suddenly stretches with little extra stress — upper and lower yield points), plastic region (large strain for small stress increase), ultimate tensile stress (UTS — maximum stress), fracture point. Brittle materials (glass, ceramics) break at or near the limit of proportionality with no plastic deformation.
Worked Example 4.8a — Young modulus
A steel wire of length 2.50 m and diameter 0.80 mm is suspended vertically and a 40 N weight is hung from it. The extension is 1.4 mm. Find the Young modulus of steel.
1
Cross-sectional area: $A = \pi(d/2)^2 = \pi(0.40\times10^{-3})^2 = 5.027\times10^{-7}$ m².
2
Stress: $\sigma = F/A = 40/5.027\times10^{-7} = 7.96\times10^7$ Pa.
3
Strain: $\varepsilon = \Delta L/L = 1.4\times10^{-3}/2.50 = 5.6\times10^{-4}$.
4
$E = \sigma/\varepsilon = 7.96\times10^7/5.6\times10^{-4} = 1.42\times10^{11}$ Pa $= 142$ GPa.
The accepted value for steel is ~200 GPa. The discrepancy suggests the wire may not be pure steel, or the measurement of extension has significant uncertainty.

ElasticReturns to original shape after force removedRubber band, spring
PlasticPermanently deformed — doesn't return to original shapeClay, metal past yield point
DuctileCan be drawn into wires — large plastic deformation before fractureCopper, mild steel

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Foundation
Q1
A stone is thrown horizontally at 12 m s⁻¹ from the top of a cliff 80 m high. Find (a) the time to reach the sea, (b) the horizontal distance from the base of the cliff, (c) the speed of the stone just before hitting the water.
(a) Vertical: $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$. $t = \sqrt{2s/g} = \sqrt{160/9.8} = \sqrt{16.33} = 4.04$ s. (b) Horizontal: $x = u_x t = 12\times4.04 = 48.5$ m. (c) $v_y = gt = 9.8\times4.04 = 39.6$ m s⁻¹. Speed $= \sqrt{12^2+39.6^2} = \sqrt{144+1568} = \sqrt{1712} = 41.4$ m s⁻¹.
[6 marks]
Q2
A spring has spring constant 45 N m⁻¹. (a) Find the extension when a 3.6 N weight is hung from it. (b) Find the elastic potential energy stored. (c) Two identical such springs are connected in series and the same 3.6 N weight is hung. Find the total extension and energy stored in the combined system.
(a) $x = F/k = 3.6/45 = 0.080$ m $= 8.0$ cm. (b) $E = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}(45)(0.08^2) = 0.144$ J. (c) Springs in series: effective $k = k/2 = 22.5$ N m⁻¹. Total extension $= F/k_{\rm eff} = 3.6/22.5 = 0.16$ m (doubles). Each spring extends 0.08 m. Total energy $= \frac{1}{2}k_{\rm eff}x_{\rm total}^2 = \frac{1}{2}(22.5)(0.16^2) = 0.288$ J (doubles — makes sense: same force over twice the distance).
[5 marks]
Q3
A car of mass 1200 kg accelerates from rest to 30 m s⁻¹ in 8.5 s. (a) Find the average net force. (b) The engine provides 6500 N of driving force. Find the average resistive force. (c) Find the useful output power at 30 m s⁻¹.
(a) $a = \Delta v/t = 30/8.5 = 3.53$ m s⁻². $F_{\rm net} = ma = 1200\times3.53 = 4235$ N. (b) $F_{\rm resistance} = F_{\rm drive} - F_{\rm net} = 6500 - 4235 = 2265$ N. (c) $P = Fv = 6500\times30 = 195000$ W $= 195$ kW.
[5 marks]

Challenging
Q4
A uniform ladder of mass 25 kg and length 6.0 m leans against a smooth vertical wall, with its base on rough horizontal ground, making an angle of 65° with the horizontal. A painter of mass 72 kg stands 4.0 m up the ladder. Find (a) the normal reaction at the wall, (b) the normal reaction at the ground, (c) the minimum coefficient of friction at the ground needed to prevent slipping.
Let $R_W$ = reaction at wall (horizontal), $R_G$ = reaction at ground (vertical), $F_f$ = friction at ground (horizontal). (a) Take moments about base of ladder. Anticlockwise moments (wall): $R_W\times6.0\sin65° = R_W\times5.438$. Clockwise: ladder weight $25g\times3.0\cos65° = 245\times1.268 = 310.6$ N m; painter $72g\times4.0\cos65° = 705.6\times1.690 = 1192.5$... Let me redo. Moment arm for horizontal distances: $25g\times(3.0\cos65°) + 72g\times(4.0\cos65°) = 245\times1.2679 + 706\times1.6906 = 310.6 + 1193.5 = 1504$ N m. So $R_W = 1504/5.438 = 276.6$ N $\approx 277$ N. (b) Vertical equilibrium: $R_G = (25+72)g = 97\times9.8 = 950.6$ N. (c) Horizontal equilibrium: friction $= R_W = 277$ N. $\mu_{\min} = F_f/R_G = 277/950.6 = 0.291$.
[8 marks]
Q5
A metal wire of cross-sectional area $1.5\times10^{-7}$ m² and natural length 3.0 m is stretched. The stress-strain graph is linear up to stress $1.8\times10^8$ Pa with Young modulus $1.2\times10^{11}$ Pa. (a) Find the strain and extension at the limit of proportionality. (b) Find the elastic potential energy stored at this point. (c) Beyond the elastic limit, the wire extends by a further 0.05 m under constant stress. Describe the energy changes and what this indicates about the wire's behaviour.
(a) $\varepsilon = \sigma/E = 1.8\times10^8/1.2\times10^{11} = 1.5\times10^{-3}$. Extension $= \varepsilon L = 1.5\times10^{-3}\times3.0 = 4.5\times10^{-3}$ m $= 4.5$ mm. (b) Volume $= A\times L = 1.5\times10^{-7}\times3.0 = 4.5\times10^{-7}$ m³. Energy per unit volume $= \frac{1}{2}\sigma\varepsilon = \frac{1}{2}\times1.8\times10^8\times1.5\times10^{-3} = 1.35\times10^5$ J m⁻³. Total EPE $= 1.35\times10^5\times4.5\times10^{-7} = 0.0608$ J. Or: $E = \frac{1}{2}Fx = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma A)(\varepsilon L) = \frac{1}{2}(27)(4.5\times10^{-3}) = 0.0608$ J ✓. (c) The additional extension at constant stress indicates plastic deformation — the wire is stretching without any additional force (yield point behaviour). The work done by the applied force goes into permanently rearranging the crystal structure of the metal (dislocations moving), not into recoverable elastic energy. The wire will not return to its original length when unloaded — it has been permanently deformed.

Section C — Exam-style
Exam-style
Q6
A crash test dummy of mass 75 kg is travelling at 15 m s⁻¹ in a car that stops suddenly. (a) Calculate the momentum of the dummy before the crash. (b) Without a seatbelt, the dummy hits the dashboard and stops in 0.012 s. Calculate the average force on the dummy. (c) With a seatbelt and airbag, the stopping time increases to 0.18 s. Calculate the new average force and compare with part (b). (d) The seatbelt extends by 15 cm as it applies force to the dummy. Use the work-energy theorem to find the average force the seatbelt exerts. Compare this with your answer to (c) and comment. (e) Explain how crumple zones, in addition to seatbelts and airbags, further reduce injury in a collision.
(a) $p = mv = 75\times15 = 1125$ kg m s⁻¹. (b) $F = \Delta p/\Delta t = 1125/0.012 = 93750$ N $\approx 94$ kN. (c) $F = 1125/0.18 = 6250$ N $\approx 6.3$ kN — about 15 times smaller than without safety equipment. (d) Work-energy theorem: $W = \Delta E_k$. The dummy decelerates from 15 m s⁻¹ to rest: $\Delta E_k = \frac{1}{2}\times75\times15^2 = 8437.5$ J. $F = W/d = 8437.5/0.15 = 56250$ N $\approx 56$ kN. This is much larger than the 6.3 kN from (c) — the discrepancy arises because (c) assumed the dummy was brought to rest by the seatbelt alone over 0.18 s; (d) calculates the average force over just the seatbelt extension of 15 cm. In reality, the airbag and seat back also absorb energy over greater distances, so the actual average seatbelt force is smaller. (e) Crumple zones increase the distance over which the car decelerates ($W = Fs$, so for same energy absorbed, larger $s$ means smaller $F$). They also increase the collision time, further reducing the average deceleration force. Crumple zones absorb kinetic energy through plastic deformation of the car body, so less energy is transmitted to the occupants.
