Dimensional Analysis — Checking Equations

The dimensions on both sides of any valid equation must match. This is a powerful tool for checking answers and deriving unknown relationships.

Worked Example 1.1a — Check by dimensions
Show that the equation $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ is dimensionally consistent.
1
Left side: $[v^2] = (\text{m s}^{-1})^2 = \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$
2
Right side: $[u^2] = \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$ and $[2as] = \text{m s}^{-2} \times \text{m} = \text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$ ✓
3
All terms have dimensions $\text{m}^2\text{s}^{-2}$ — the equation is dimensionally consistent.
Note: consistency doesn't prove the equation is correct — it just rules out certain errors.

1.2 Errors & Uncertainties

No measurement is perfectly exact. Understanding the type and source of error is essential for good experimental physics.

Random vs Systematic Errors

Random ErrorSystematic Error
CauseUnpredictable fluctuations in measurementConsistent offset in one direction
EffectResults spread around the true valueAll results shifted from the true value
Reduced byRepeating measurements & averagingCalibration; better technique
Shown on graphScatter around a line of best fitLine of best fit offset from expected
ExampleReaction time variation in stopwatch timingZero error on a ruler; a wrongly calibrated balance

Precision, Accuracy & Resolution

Three key distinctions Precision: how repeatable measurements are (small spread, small random error). Accuracy: how close measurements are to the true value (small systematic error). Resolution: the smallest change a measuring instrument can detect (e.g. a ruler with 1 mm markings has 1 mm resolution). A precise instrument can still be inaccurate if it has a systematic error.

Absolute & Percentage Uncertainty

$$\text{Absolute uncertainty: } \Delta x \quad \text{written as } x \pm \Delta x$$ $$\text{Percentage uncertainty: } \frac{\Delta x}{x} \times 100\%$$

For a single measurement, the uncertainty is typically half the smallest scale division. For repeated measurements, the uncertainty is half the range: $\Delta x = \frac{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}{2}$.

Worked Example 1.2a
A student measures the length of a wire five times: 24.2, 24.5, 24.1, 24.4, 24.3 cm. Find the mean, the absolute uncertainty, and the percentage uncertainty.
1
Mean: $\bar{x} = \dfrac{24.2+24.5+24.1+24.4+24.3}{5} = \dfrac{121.5}{5} = 24.3$ cm
2
Range $= 24.5 - 24.1 = 0.4$ cm. Absolute uncertainty $= \dfrac{0.4}{2} = 0.2$ cm. So $l = (24.3 \pm 0.2)$ cm.
3
Percentage uncertainty $= \dfrac{0.2}{24.3} \times 100\% = 0.82\%$

1.3 Combining Uncertainties

When calculated quantities depend on multiple measured values, uncertainties must be combined using these rules:

$$\text{Adding or subtracting: } \Delta(x \pm y) = \Delta x + \Delta y \quad \text{(add absolute uncertainties)}$$ $$\text{Multiplying or dividing: } \frac{\Delta(xy)}{xy} = \frac{\Delta x}{x} + \frac{\Delta y}{y} \quad \text{(add percentage uncertainties)}$$ $$\text{Power: } \frac{\Delta(x^n)}{x^n} = n\,\frac{\Delta x}{x} \quad \text{(multiply percentage uncertainty by power)}$$
⚠ Always add — never subtract — uncertainties When combining uncertainties, you always add them (whether absolute or percentage). This gives the worst-case bound. Never subtract uncertainties even if the errors might partially cancel — that would underestimate the true uncertainty.
Worked Example 1.3a
A cylinder has radius $r = (2.5 \pm 0.1)$ cm and height $h = (8.0 \pm 0.2)$ cm. Find the volume and its percentage uncertainty. Hence find the absolute uncertainty in the volume.
1
$V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (2.5)^2 (8.0) = 157.1$ cm³
2
Percentage uncertainty in $r^2$: $2 \times \dfrac{0.1}{2.5} \times 100\% = 8\%$. Percentage uncertainty in $h$: $\dfrac{0.2}{8.0} \times 100\% = 2.5\%$.
3
Total percentage uncertainty in $V = 8\% + 2.5\% = 10.5\%$
4
Absolute uncertainty $= 0.105 \times 157.1 = 16.5$ cm³. So $V = (157 \pm 17)$ cm³ (2 s.f.).
The radius contributes far more to the uncertainty than the height — reducing the uncertainty in $r$ would be the priority if improving the experiment.
Worked Example 1.3b — Uncertainty in a difference
Two temperatures are measured: $T_1 = (85 \pm 2)°C$ and $T_2 = (23 \pm 1)°C$. Find the temperature difference and its uncertainty.
1
$\Delta T = T_1 - T_2 = 85 - 23 = 62°C$
2
Uncertainty in difference (add absolute): $\Delta(\Delta T) = 2 + 1 = 3°C$
3
Result: $\Delta T = (62 \pm 3)°C$. Percentage uncertainty $= \dfrac{3}{62} \times 100\% = 4.8\%$.
Notice: subtracting nearly equal quantities makes the percentage uncertainty much larger than either individual measurement — this is why measuring small differences is hard.

1.4 Graphs & Gradients

Graphs are central to physics — they allow us to determine relationships between variables, find constants, and estimate uncertainties from lines of best fit.

Lines of best fit & uncertainty

Draw the line of best fit through the data points, then draw the steepest and shallowest lines that still fit within the error bars. The uncertainty in the gradient is:

$$\Delta m = \frac{m_{\text{max}} - m_{\text{min}}}{2} \qquad \text{uncertainty in gradient}$$ $$\Delta c = \frac{c_{\text{max}} - c_{\text{min}}}{2} \qquad \text{uncertainty in intercept}$$

Linearising relationships

Many relationships in physics are not linear. To plot a straight line, rearrange into the form $y = mx + c$.

Worked Example 1.4a — Finding $g$ from a pendulum graph
A student plots $T^2$ (s²) against $l$ (m) for a simple pendulum and finds a gradient of $4.10$ s² m⁻¹. The maximum gradient line gives $4.25$ and the minimum gives $3.95$. Find $g$ and its uncertainty.
1
Theory: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{l/g} \Rightarrow T^2 = \dfrac{4\pi^2}{g} l$. Gradient $= \dfrac{4\pi^2}{g}$.
2
$g = \dfrac{4\pi^2}{4.10} = \dfrac{39.48}{4.10} = 9.63$ m s⁻²
3
Uncertainty in gradient: $\Delta m = \dfrac{4.25 - 3.95}{2} = 0.15$ s² m⁻¹. Percentage uncertainty $= \dfrac{0.15}{4.10} \times 100\% = 3.66\%$
4
$\Delta g = 0.0366 \times 9.63 = 0.35$ m s⁻². So $g = (9.6 \pm 0.4)$ m s⁻² (2 s.f.).
Compare with $g = 9.81$ m s⁻²: the result is within 2% — good for a school experiment.

1.5 Orders of Magnitude

An order of magnitude is a power of ten. Being able to estimate quantities and check whether answers are physically reasonable is a key skill.

Useful estimates to know Diameter of a proton: ~$10^{-15}$ m (1 fm). Diameter of an atom: ~$10^{-10}$ m (1 Å). Diameter of a cell: ~$10^{-5}$ m. Height of a person: ~$1.7$ m. Distance Earth–Sun: ~$1.5 \times 10^{11}$ m. Speed of light: $3 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹. Mass of electron: $9.11 \times 10^{-31}$ kg. Mass of proton: $1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg.
Worked Example 1.5a — Fermi estimation
Estimate the number of atoms in a 1 kg iron bar. (Molar mass of Fe ≈ 56 g mol⁻¹, $N_A \approx 6 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹)
1
Moles of Fe: $n = \dfrac{1000\text{ g}}{56\text{ g mol}^{-1}} \approx 17.9$ mol
2
Number of atoms $= 17.9 \times 6 \times 10^{23} \approx 1.1 \times 10^{25}$ atoms
3
Order of magnitude: $\sim 10^{25}$ atoms. This is roughly 10 billion times more than there are stars in the observable universe ($\sim 10^{24}$ stars).

1.6 Significant Figures & Resolution

Significant figures (s.f.) indicate how precisely a quantity is known. The rules for counting sig figs:

Counting sig figs rules All non-zero digits are significant. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant (e.g. 1.03 has 3 s.f.). Leading zeros are NOT significant (e.g. 0.0042 has 2 s.f.). Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant (e.g. 3.40 has 3 s.f.). Trailing zeros before a decimal point may or may not be — use standard form to be clear (e.g. $3.40 \times 10^3$ has 3 s.f.).
⚠ Never give more sig figs than your data justifies Your final answer should be quoted to the same number of significant figures as the least precise measurement used. In practice, AQA mark schemes usually accept answers to 2 or 3 s.f. Quoting 6 s.f. from a 2 s.f. measurement is physically meaningless.

The resolution of an instrument is the smallest change it can detect. A digital ammeter reading to 0.01 A has a resolution of 0.01 A. The uncertainty from a single reading is typically $\pm\frac{1}{2}$ of the resolution for analogue instruments and $\pm 1$ in the last digit for digital instruments.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Foundation
Q1
Convert the following: (a) 450 nm to metres, (b) 2.3 GHz to Hz, (c) 0.056 mA to amperes, (d) 7.2 × 10⁻⁴ m to μm.
(a) $450 \times 10^{-9}$ m $= 4.50 \times 10^{-7}$ m. (b) $2.3 \times 10^9$ Hz. (c) $0.056 \times 10^{-3}$ A $= 5.6 \times 10^{-5}$ A. (d) $7.2 \times 10^{-4}$ m $\div 10^{-6}$ = $720$ μm.
[4 marks]
Q2
A student measures the diameter of a wire using a micrometer: 1.42, 1.40, 1.43, 1.41, 1.42 mm. (a) Find the mean diameter. (b) Find the absolute uncertainty. (c) Find the percentage uncertainty. (d) State whether the spread is due to random or systematic error.
(a) Mean $= (1.42+1.40+1.43+1.41+1.42)/5 = 7.08/5 = 1.416 \approx 1.42$ mm. (b) Range $= 1.43-1.40=0.03$ mm. Uncertainty $= 0.03/2 = 0.015 \approx 0.02$ mm. (c) $\%$ uncertainty $= 0.015/1.416 \times 100\% = 1.1\%$. (d) Random error — readings are scattered either side of the mean with no consistent bias.
[5 marks]
Q3
The resistance of a wire is calculated using $R = V/I$ where $V = (6.0 \pm 0.2)$ V and $I = (0.30 \pm 0.01)$ A. Find (a) the resistance, (b) the percentage uncertainty in $R$, (c) the absolute uncertainty in $R$.
(a) $R = 6.0/0.30 = 20$ Ω. (b) $\%$ uncertainty $= (0.2/6.0 + 0.01/0.30) \times 100 = (3.33 + 3.33)\% = 6.7\%$. (c) Absolute $= 0.067 \times 20 = 1.3$ Ω. So $R = (20 \pm 1)$ Ω.
[4 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Challenging
Q4
A student investigates how the period $T$ of a mass-spring system depends on mass $m$. Theory predicts $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$. The student plots $\ln T$ against $\ln m$ and finds a gradient of $0.51 \pm 0.03$ and a y-intercept of $-0.72 \pm 0.05$. (a) Show this is consistent with the theory. (b) Find the spring constant $k$ and its uncertainty.
(a) Taking logs: $\ln T = \frac{1}{2}\ln m + \ln(2\pi/\sqrt{k})$. Theory predicts gradient $= 0.5$ and intercept $= \ln(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\ln k$. Gradient $0.51 \pm 0.03$ is consistent with $0.5$ (within uncertainty) ✓. (b) Intercept $= \ln(2\pi/\sqrt{k}) = -0.72$. $2\pi/\sqrt{k} = e^{-0.72} = 0.487$. $\sqrt{k} = 2\pi/0.487 = 12.90$. $k = 166$ N m⁻¹. Uncertainty in intercept $= 0.05$. Since intercept $= \ln(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\ln k$, the $\%$ uncertainty in $k = 2 \times \Delta(\text{intercept}) / 1 = 2 \times 0.05/0.72 \times 100\% = 13.9\%$. So $\Delta k = 0.139 \times 166 \approx 23$ N m⁻¹. $k = (170 \pm 20)$ N m⁻¹ (2 s.f.).
[6 marks]
Q5
Show by dimensional analysis that the time for a pendulum to swing could depend on length $l$, mass $m$ and $g$, but not on the angle of swing (which is dimensionless). Hence show that $T \propto \sqrt{l/g}$.
Assume $T = k\,l^a m^b g^c$. Dimensions: $[\text{T}] = \text{s}$, $[l^a] = \text{m}^a$, $[m^b] = \text{kg}^b$, $[g^c] = (\text{m s}^{-2})^c = \text{m}^c\text{s}^{-2c}$. Matching dimensions: Time: $1 = -2c \Rightarrow c = -1/2$. Length: $0 = a + c \Rightarrow a = 1/2$. Mass: $0 = b \Rightarrow b = 0$ (mass doesn't appear!). So $T = k\sqrt{l/g}$. Since angle is dimensionless, it cannot appear in a dimensional argument — it only affects the numerical constant $k$ for large angles. For small angles, $k = 2\pi$.
[5 marks]
Section C — Exam-style
Exam-style
Q6
A student measures the density of a metal cylinder. She measures the diameter $d = (2.40 \pm 0.02)$ cm, height $h = (5.15 \pm 0.05)$ cm, and mass $m = (186.3 \pm 0.5)$ g. (a) Calculate the volume of the cylinder. (b) Calculate the density. (c) Find the percentage uncertainty in the density. (d) Find the absolute uncertainty in the density and write the final answer in an appropriate form. (e) A data book gives the density of the metal as 7900 kg m⁻³. Comment on whether the student's result is consistent with this value.
(a) $V = \pi(d/2)^2 h = \pi(1.20)^2(5.15) = \pi(1.44)(5.15) = 23.31$ cm³. (b) $\rho = m/V = 186.3/23.31 = 7.993$ g cm⁻³ $= 7993$ kg m⁻³. (c) $\%$ uncertainty: diameter enters as $r^2$, so $2 \times (0.02/2.40) \times 100 = 1.67\%$. Height: $(0.05/5.15)\times100 = 0.97\%$. Mass: $(0.5/186.3)\times100 = 0.27\%$. Total: $1.67+0.97+0.27 = 2.91\%$. (d) $\Delta\rho = 0.0291 \times 7993 = 233$ kg m⁻³. So $\rho = (8000 \pm 200)$ kg m⁻³ (to 2 s.f. on uncertainty). (e) The data book value of 7900 kg m⁻³ is within the student's range of $(7800\text{ to }8200)$ kg m⁻³, so the results are consistent. The metal is likely to be steel or iron.
[8 marks]
Q7
A student investigates the relationship between the current $I$ through a filament bulb and the voltage $V$ across it. She expects the relationship to be $V = IR$ where $R$ depends on temperature. (a) Describe the difference between random and systematic error in this experiment, giving one example of each. (b) She plots $V$ (y-axis) against $I$ (x-axis) and finds the graph curves upward. Explain what this tells us about how $R$ changes with current. (c) Suggest how she could linearise this graph to investigate the relationship between $R$ and $I$.
(a) Random error: unpredictable fluctuations causing measurements to scatter around the true value; example: fluctuations in the power supply voltage causing slight variations in current readings. Systematic error: consistent offset in one direction, e.g. a zero error on the ammeter meaning all current readings are 0.05 A too high. (b) If the graph curves upward ($V/I$ increases as $I$ increases), then the gradient $R = V/I$ is increasing with current. Since higher current → more power dissipated → higher temperature, this means $R$ increases with temperature — consistent with the behaviour of a metal conductor. (c) Calculate $R = V/I$ for each data point and plot $R$ against $I$ (or $R$ against $I^2$ if R depends on the power dissipated). Alternatively, if $R \propto T$ and $T \propto I^2R$ (power), then $R \propto I^2R$ which gives $1 \propto I^2$ — not helpful. Better: plot $\ln R$ against $\ln I$ to find the power law relationship between resistance and current.
[6 marks]
