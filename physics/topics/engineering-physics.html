Rotational Dynamics

Rotational dynamics is the rotational analogue of linear mechanics. Every linear quantity has a rotational equivalent.

LinearSymbolRotational equivalentSymbol
Displacement$s$Angle$\theta$
Velocity$v$Angular velocity$\omega$
Acceleration$a$Angular acceleration$\alpha$
Mass$m$Moment of inertia$I$
Force$F$Torque$\tau$
Newton's 2nd law: $F=ma$$\tau = I\alpha$
KE $= \frac{1}{2}mv^2$Rotational KE $= \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$
Momentum $p = mv$Angular momentum $L = I\omega$

Moment of Inertia

The moment of inertia $I$ depends on both the mass and its distribution relative to the axis of rotation:

$$I = \sum m_i r_i^2$$

Common cases: solid disc $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$, solid sphere $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$, thin ring $I = MR^2$, thin rod about centre $I = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$.

Conservation of Angular Momentum

In the absence of external torques, angular momentum is conserved: $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$. This explains why a spinning figure skater speeds up when pulling their arms in.

▶ Worked Example

A solid disc of mass 2.0 kg and radius 0.30 m rotates at 12 rad s⁻¹. A torque of 1.8 N m is applied. Find the angular acceleration and the new angular velocity after 4.0 s.

1
$I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2.0)(0.30)^2 = 0.090\ \text{kg m}^2$
2
$\alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = \frac{1.8}{0.090} = 20\ \text{rad s}^{-2}$
3
$\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t = 12 + (20)(4.0) = 92\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$

Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

$$\Delta U = Q + W$$

$\Delta U$ = increase in internal energy, $Q$ = heat supplied to system, $W$ = work done on system. (AQA sign convention: $W$ done on gas is positive.)

Thermodynamic processes

ProcessConditionKey result
IsothermalConstant temperature ($\Delta U = 0$)$Q = -W$ (heat out = work done by gas)
AdiabaticNo heat exchange ($Q = 0$)$\Delta U = W$ (work done on gas increases internal energy)
Isovolumetric (isochoric)Constant volume ($W = 0$)$\Delta U = Q$
IsobaricConstant pressure$W = p\Delta V$
Work done by/on a gas Work done by a gas expanding: $W = p\Delta V$ (for constant pressure).
On a $p$–$V$ diagram, the work done is the area under the curve.

Heat Engines & Cycles

A heat engine takes in heat $Q_H$ from a hot reservoir, does useful work $W$, and rejects waste heat $Q_C$ to a cold reservoir.

$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \qquad \eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$

Efficiency $\eta$ (as a fraction). Carnot efficiency is the maximum theoretical efficiency for temperatures $T_C$ and $T_H$ in Kelvin.

The Carnot Cycle

The most efficient possible cycle between two temperatures. It consists of four reversible processes on a $p$–$V$ diagram:

  1. Isothermal expansion at $T_H$ — absorbs heat $Q_H$
  2. Adiabatic expansion — temperature falls from $T_H$ to $T_C$
  3. Isothermal compression at $T_C$ — rejects heat $Q_C$
  4. Adiabatic compression — temperature rises from $T_C$ to $T_H$
Second Law of Thermodynamics It is impossible to convert heat entirely into work in a cyclic process — some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir. This is why no real engine can reach 100% efficiency.
▶ Worked Example

A heat engine operates between 500 K and 300 K. It takes in 2000 J per cycle. Calculate the Carnot efficiency and the maximum work output per cycle.

1
$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} = 1 - \frac{300}{500} = 0.40$ (40%)
2
$W_{\max} = \eta \times Q_H = 0.40 \times 2000 = 800\ \text{J}$

Fluid Flow

Viscosity

Viscosity is a fluid's resistance to flow. For a viscous fluid flowing past a surface, there is a velocity gradient and a drag force (Stokes' law for a sphere):

$$F = 6\pi\eta r v$$

$F$ = drag force, $\eta$ = dynamic viscosity (Pa s), $r$ = sphere radius, $v$ = velocity.

Equation of Continuity

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

For an incompressible fluid: where the pipe is narrower, the fluid flows faster. $A$ = cross-sectional area, $v$ = flow speed.

Bernoulli's Equation

$$p + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}$$

Where flow speed is higher, pressure is lower. Explains lift on aircraft wings, flow through constrictions (Venturi effect).

Laminar vs Turbulent Flow Laminar flow: smooth, orderly layers — occurs at low speeds. Turbulent flow: chaotic eddies — occurs above a critical speed. The Reynolds number $Re = \frac{\rho v d}{\eta}$ predicts the transition: $Re < 1000$ laminar, $Re > 2000$ turbulent.
