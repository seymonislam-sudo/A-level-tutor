5.1 Current, Charge & Voltage

Electric current is the rate of flow of charge. In metals, charge is carried by free electrons drifting in the opposite direction to conventional current (which flows from + to −).

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \qquad Q = It$$ $$V = \frac{W}{Q} \qquad \text{(potential difference = energy per unit charge)}$$ $$P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \qquad W = IVt = I^2Rt$$

The unit of charge is the coulomb (C). One coulomb is the charge carried by approximately $6.24\times10^{18}$ electrons (since $e = 1.60\times10^{-19}$ C).

Drift velocity

In a conductor carrying current $I$, the charge carriers (electrons) move with average drift velocity $v$:

$$I = nqvA$$

$n$ = number density of charge carriers (m⁻³)  ·  $q$ = charge per carrier (C)  ·  $v$ = drift velocity (m s⁻¹)  ·  $A$ = cross-sectional area (m²)

Worked Example 5.1a — Drift velocity
A copper wire of diameter 1.2 mm carries a current of 3.0 A. The number density of free electrons in copper is $8.5\times10^{28}$ m⁻³. Find the drift velocity of the electrons.
1
$A = \pi(d/2)^2 = \pi(0.6\times10^{-3})^2 = 1.131\times10^{-6}$ m²
2
$v = \dfrac{I}{nqA} = \dfrac{3.0}{8.5\times10^{28}\times1.6\times10^{-19}\times1.131\times10^{-6}}$
3
$v = \dfrac{3.0}{1.537\times10^4} = 1.95\times10^{-4}$ m s⁻¹ $\approx 0.2$ mm s⁻¹
Electrons drift incredibly slowly — far less than 1 mm per second. Yet the effect of switching on a light is almost instantaneous because the electric field propagates at close to the speed of light.

5.2 Resistance & Ohm's Law

$$R = \frac{V}{I} \qquad \text{(definition of resistance, always true)}$$ $$\text{Ohm's Law: } V \propto I \text{ (at constant temperature) — only for ohmic conductors}$$

Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω). An ohmic conductor obeys Ohm's Law — its resistance is constant regardless of the voltage or current applied, provided the temperature doesn't change. Metals at constant temperature are ohmic.

Why resistance increases with temperature in metals In a metal, free electrons drift through a lattice of positive ions. As temperature rises, the ions vibrate more vigorously, increasing the frequency of collisions between electrons and ions. This impedes electron flow, increasing resistance. In semiconductors (e.g. thermistors), the opposite occurs — more charge carriers are released at higher temperatures, decreasing resistance.

5.3 I–V Characteristics

An I–V characteristic graph shows how current varies with voltage for a component. The gradient at any point equals $1/R$ at that point.

ComponentI–V shapeBehaviour
Resistor (ohmic)Straight line through originConstant resistance; $R = V/I$ = constant gradient
Filament bulbS-shaped curve, decreasing gradientResistance increases with temperature (and therefore current)
DiodeFlat near zero, steep above ~0.6 V forward; flat below 0 reverseConducts in one direction only; threshold ~0.6 V for silicon
Thermistor (NTC)Not a simple I–V graph — resistance decreases with temperatureUsed as temperature sensors
Filament bulb — why resistance increases At low voltages (low current, low temperature), the filament has low resistance and the I–V graph is nearly linear. As current increases, the filament heats up to ~2500°C, increasing resistance significantly. The gradient ($= 1/R$) decreases, giving the characteristic S-shape. The bulb is non-ohmic because resistance depends on temperature, which depends on current.

5.4 Resistivity

Resistivity $\rho$ is a property of the material, not the component. It quantifies how strongly a material opposes current flow:

$$R = \frac{\rho L}{A} \qquad \rho = \frac{RA}{L}$$

$\rho$ = resistivity (Ω m)  ·  $L$ = length (m)  ·  $A$ = cross-sectional area (m²)

MaterialResistivity (Ω m) at 20°CType
Silver$1.6\times10^{-8}$Conductor
Copper$1.7\times10^{-8}$Conductor
Nichrome$1.1\times10^{-6}$Resistive alloy
Silicon$6.4\times10^{2}$Semiconductor
Glass$\sim10^{12}$Insulator
Worked Example 5.4a
A constantan wire of resistivity $4.9\times10^{-7}$ Ω m has resistance 8.0 Ω and diameter 0.30 mm. Find (a) the length of wire required, (b) the resistance if the diameter is halved with the same length.
1
(a) $A = \pi(0.15\times10^{-3})^2 = 7.069\times10^{-8}$ m². $L = RA/\rho = 8.0\times7.069\times10^{-8}/4.9\times10^{-7} = 1.155$ m $\approx 1.2$ m.
2
(b) Halving diameter quarters the area: $A' = A/4$. Since $R = \rho L/A$, quartering $A$ quadruples $R$. New $R = 4\times8.0 = 32$ Ω.
This is a very common exam trap — halving the diameter multiplies resistance by 4, not 2. Always think in terms of area.

5.5 Series & Parallel Circuits

$$\text{Series: } R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots \qquad I \text{ same through all; } V \text{ splits}$$ $$\text{Parallel: } \frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots \qquad V \text{ same across all; } I \text{ splits}$$

Kirchhoff's laws underpin all circuit analysis:

Kirchhoff's two laws 1st Law (current): The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum leaving — conservation of charge. 2nd Law (voltage): The sum of EMFs around any closed loop equals the sum of potential drops (IR) around that loop — conservation of energy. These two laws are sufficient to solve any circuit.
Worked Example 5.5a — Mixed circuit
Three resistors: $R_1 = 6$ Ω in series with a parallel combination of $R_2 = 4$ Ω and $R_3 = 12$ Ω, connected to a 24 V supply. Find the total resistance, total current, and the current through each parallel resistor.
1
Parallel combination: $\frac{1}{R_{23}} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{3+1}{12} = \frac{4}{12}$. $R_{23} = 3$ Ω.
2
Total resistance: $R_T = R_1 + R_{23} = 6 + 3 = 9$ Ω.
3
Total current: $I = V/R_T = 24/9 = 2.67$ A. Voltage across parallel pair: $V_{23} = I\times R_{23} = 2.67\times3 = 8.0$ V.
4
$I_2 = V_{23}/R_2 = 8.0/4 = 2.0$ A. $I_3 = V_{23}/R_3 = 8.0/12 = 0.667$ A. Check: $I_2+I_3 = 2.67$ A ✓
Current divides inversely with resistance — more current flows through the lower resistance branch.

5.6 EMF & Internal Resistance

A real battery has internal resistance $r$ — resistance within the battery itself that causes the terminal voltage to drop when current flows. The EMF ($\varepsilon$) is the total energy provided per unit charge by the battery.

$$\varepsilon = V_{\text{terminal}} + Ir = I(R+r)$$ $$V_{\text{terminal}} = \varepsilon - Ir \qquad \text{("lost volts" = }Ir\text{)}$$
Measuring EMF and internal resistance Connect a battery to a variable resistor $R$ with an ammeter and voltmeter. Vary $R$ and record $I$ and $V$. Plotting $V$ against $I$ gives a straight line: $V = \varepsilon - Ir$. Gradient $= -r$ (internal resistance). Y-intercept $= \varepsilon$ (EMF — the terminal voltage when $I = 0$). X-intercept $= \varepsilon/r$ (short-circuit current).
Worked Example 5.6a
A battery of EMF 12.0 V and internal resistance 0.8 Ω is connected to an external resistance of 5.2 Ω. Find (a) the current, (b) the terminal voltage, (c) the power wasted in the internal resistance, (d) the efficiency of the battery.
1
(a) $I = \varepsilon/(R+r) = 12.0/(5.2+0.8) = 12.0/6.0 = 2.0$ A.
2
(b) $V = \varepsilon - Ir = 12.0 - 2.0\times0.8 = 12.0 - 1.6 = 10.4$ V. (Lost volts $= 1.6$ V.)
3
(c) $P_r = I^2r = 4.0\times0.8 = 3.2$ W. Total power $= \varepsilon I = 12\times2 = 24$ W. Useful power $= IV = 2\times10.4 = 20.8$ W.
4
(d) Efficiency $= P_{\text{useful}}/P_{\text{total}} = 20.8/24 = 86.7\%$. Or $= R/(R+r) = 5.2/6.0 = 86.7\%$.

5.7 Potential Divider

A potential divider uses two (or more) resistors in series to produce a fraction of the supply voltage. It is the most important circuit in A Level Physics for sensing and control applications.

$$V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \times \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

The output is taken across $R_2$. If $R_2$ is replaced by a variable resistor or a sensor (thermistor, LDR), the output voltage changes with conditions:

SensorConditionSensor resistance$V_\text{out}$ (across sensor)
Thermistor (NTC)Temperature risesDecreasesDecreases
LDRLight intensity increasesDecreasesDecreases
LDR (top position)Light intensity increasesDecreasesIncreases (output across fixed $R_2$)
Worked Example 5.7a — Thermistor circuit
A potential divider has a fixed resistor $R_1 = 10$ kΩ in series with an NTC thermistor $R_T$. The supply is 9.0 V and the output is taken across $R_T$. At 20°C, $R_T = 15$ kΩ; at 80°C, $R_T = 1.5$ kΩ. Find the output voltage at each temperature and explain the circuit's use as a temperature sensor.
1
At 20°C: $V_{\text{out}} = 9.0\times\dfrac{15}{10+15} = 9.0\times0.6 = 5.4$ V.
2
At 80°C: $V_{\text{out}} = 9.0\times\dfrac{1.5}{10+1.5} = 9.0\times0.130 = 1.17$ V.
3
As temperature rises, $R_T$ falls → smaller fraction of supply appears across $R_T$ → $V_\text{out}$ decreases. This output voltage can trigger a comparator or microprocessor to switch heating on (if $V_\text{out}$ falls below a threshold) or cooling off. Swapping $R_1$ and $R_T$ positions would give an increasing $V_\text{out}$ with temperature.
The output voltage is a continuous, analogue signal proportional to temperature — useful for electronic control systems.

5.8 Alternating Current

In an alternating current (AC) circuit, the current and voltage vary sinusoidally with time. The mains supply in the UK is 50 Hz, 230 V (rms).

$$V_{\text{rms}} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} \qquad I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}$$ $$P_{\text{mean}} = \frac{1}{2}I_0 V_0 = I_{\text{rms}}V_{\text{rms}} = I_{\text{rms}}^2 R = \frac{V_{\text{rms}}^2}{R}$$

The root mean square (rms) values of AC voltage and current are the equivalent DC values that would deliver the same power to a resistor. The peak voltage $V_0$ is $\sqrt{2}$ times the rms value. For UK mains (230 V rms): $V_0 = 230\sqrt{2} \approx 325$ V.

Worked Example 5.8a
A 60 W light bulb is connected to the 230 V rms mains supply. Find (a) the rms current, (b) the peak current, (c) the peak voltage, (d) the resistance of the bulb filament when operating.
1
(a) $P = V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}$. $I_{\text{rms}} = P/V_{\text{rms}} = 60/230 = 0.261$ A.
2
(b) $I_0 = I_{\text{rms}}\sqrt{2} = 0.261\times1.414 = 0.369$ A.
3
(c) $V_0 = 230\sqrt{2} = 325$ V.
4
(d) $R = V_{\text{rms}}/I_{\text{rms}} = 230/0.261 = 882$ Ω. Or $R = V_{\text{rms}}^2/P = 230^2/60 = 882$ Ω.
This is the hot resistance. The cold resistance of a tungsten filament is much lower — the filament heats to ~2500°C when operating, vastly increasing resistivity.

Section A — Foundation
Foundation
Q1
A charge of 180 C flows through a resistor of 12 Ω in 60 s. Find (a) the current, (b) the potential difference across the resistor, (c) the power dissipated, (d) the total energy transferred.
(a) $I = Q/t = 180/60 = 3.0$ A. (b) $V = IR = 3.0\times12 = 36$ V. (c) $P = IV = 3.0\times36 = 108$ W. (d) $W = Pt = 108\times60 = 6480$ J. Or $W = VIt = 36\times3\times60 = 6480$ J ✓.
[5 marks]
Q2
Three resistors of 6 Ω, 12 Ω, and 4 Ω are connected in parallel across a 12 V battery with negligible internal resistance. Find (a) the total resistance, (b) the total current from the battery, (c) the current through each resistor, (d) the power dissipated in the 4 Ω resistor.
(a) $\frac{1}{R} = \frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{12}+\frac{1}{4} = \frac{2+1+3}{12} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}$. $R = 2$ Ω. (b) $I = V/R = 12/2 = 6$ A. (c) $I_6 = 12/6 = 2$ A; $I_{12} = 12/12 = 1$ A; $I_4 = 12/4 = 3$ A. Check: $2+1+3=6$ A ✓. (d) $P = V^2/R = 144/4 = 36$ W.
[6 marks]
Q3
A battery of EMF 6.0 V and internal resistance 1.5 Ω drives a current of 1.2 A through an external circuit. Find (a) the terminal voltage, (b) the external resistance, (c) the power wasted internally, (d) the efficiency.
(a) $V = \varepsilon - Ir = 6.0 - 1.2\times1.5 = 6.0 - 1.8 = 4.2$ V. (b) $R = V/I = 4.2/1.2 = 3.5$ Ω. (c) $P_r = I^2r = 1.44\times1.5 = 2.16$ W. (d) Efficiency $= R/(R+r) = 3.5/5.0 = 70\%$.
[5 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Challenging
Q4
A student sets up a potential divider with a fixed resistor $R_1 = 4.7$ kΩ and an LDR in series from a 5.0 V supply. The output is taken across the LDR. In bright light the LDR has resistance 500 Ω; in darkness it has resistance 200 kΩ. (a) Calculate the output voltage in bright light and darkness. (b) The student wants the output to be high in darkness and low in bright light. Suggest how to modify the circuit. (c) The student adds a voltmeter of resistance 50 kΩ across the LDR in darkness. Calculate the new output voltage and comment on the effect.
(a) Bright light: $V = 5.0\times500/(4700+500) = 5.0\times500/5200 = 0.481$ V. Darkness: $V = 5.0\times200000/(4700+200000) = 5.0\times200000/204700 = 4.885$ V ≈ 4.9 V. (b) Swap $R_1$ and the LDR — put the LDR at the top (connected to +) and take output across the fixed $R_1$. In darkness LDR is high resistance, most voltage drops across LDR, leaving low voltage across $R_1$. In bright light LDR resistance drops, more voltage appears across $R_1$ → high output. Wait, that gives high in bright. For high in darkness: keep original arrangement (output across LDR) — output is already high in darkness (4.9 V) ✓. So original circuit already satisfies requirement. (c) In darkness, voltmeter (50 kΩ) in parallel with LDR (200 kΩ): $R_{\rm parallel} = (50\times200)/(50+200) = 10000/250 = 40$ kΩ. New $V_{\rm out} = 5.0\times40/(4.7+40) = 200/44.7 = 4.47$ V. The voltmeter loads the circuit significantly — it draws current and reduces the output from 4.9 V to 4.47 V (a drop of ~0.4 V or 9%). A voltmeter should have very high resistance compared to the circuit resistances to minimise loading effects.
[9 marks]
Q5
Two cells are connected in a circuit. Cell A has EMF 9.0 V and internal resistance 2.0 Ω. Cell B has EMF 6.0 V and internal resistance 1.0 Ω. They are connected in series opposing (positive terminals facing each other) with an external 7.0 Ω resistor. (a) Find the current in the circuit. (b) Find the terminal voltage of each cell. (c) Find which cell is being charged and explain why. (d) Find the power delivered to the external resistor.
(a) Net EMF in series opposing = $9.0 - 6.0 = 3.0$ V. Total resistance $= 2.0+1.0+7.0 = 10$ Ω. $I = 3.0/10 = 0.30$ A (flows in direction of Cell A, i.e. Cell A drives the current). (b) Terminal voltage of A: $V_A = \varepsilon_A - Ir_A = 9.0 - 0.30\times2.0 = 8.4$ V. Terminal voltage of B: current flows into B's positive terminal (being charged). $V_B = \varepsilon_B + Ir_B = 6.0 + 0.30\times1.0 = 6.3$ V. (c) Cell B is being charged — current flows into its positive terminal (against its EMF direction), forcing current through it backwards. Cell A supplies energy to both the resistor and to recharge cell B. (d) $P = I^2R = 0.09\times7.0 = 0.63$ W.
[8 marks]
Section C — Exam-style
Exam-style
Q6
A student investigates the I–V characteristics of three components: a resistor, a filament bulb, and a diode. She plots current (y-axis) against voltage (x-axis) for each. (a) Sketch the expected I–V graph for each component on the same axes, labelling clearly. (b) She measures the resistance of the filament bulb at two points: when $V = 1.0$ V, $I = 0.50$ A; when $V = 6.0$ V, $I = 0.80$ A. Calculate the resistance at each point and explain the difference. (c) The student uses an AC supply of peak voltage 6.0 V to drive the diode circuit. Describe and explain the current waveform she would observe on an oscilloscope. (d) Suggest one modification to the diode circuit that would produce a smoother DC output, and explain how it works.
(a) Resistor: straight line through origin, positive gradient. Filament bulb: curves with decreasing gradient (S-shape) — steeper near origin, flattening at higher V. Diode: essentially zero current for negative and small positive V, then sharp exponential rise above ~0.6 V; for negative (reverse bias), flat at near-zero current. (b) At 1 V: $R = V/I = 1.0/0.50 = 2.0$ Ω. At 6 V: $R = 6.0/0.80 = 7.5$ Ω. Resistance is much higher at the higher voltage because higher current causes greater power dissipation, raising the filament temperature significantly (from perhaps 500°C to 2500°C). The resistivity of tungsten increases with temperature, so resistance rises. The bulb is a non-ohmic conductor. (c) During the positive half-cycles (when voltage exceeds ~0.6 V), the diode conducts and current flows. During negative half-cycles, the diode is reverse biased and no current flows. The oscilloscope would show half-wave rectification: positive pulses of current (sinusoidal shape, clipped at ~0 for negative half) alternating with zero current, at 50 Hz. (d) Add a capacitor in parallel with the load resistor. During the conducting half-cycle, the capacitor charges up to the peak voltage. During the non-conducting half-cycle, the capacitor slowly discharges through the load, maintaining a roughly constant voltage. The larger the capacitance (and the larger the load resistance), the slower the discharge and the smoother the output (smaller ripple voltage).
