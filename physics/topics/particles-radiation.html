2.1 Atomic Structure

The atom consists of a tiny, dense nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons in shells. The nucleus is approximately $10^{-15}$ m across — about 10,000 times smaller than the atom ($\sim 10^{-10}$ m).

ParticleSymbolCharge / eRest mass / kgRest mass / u
Protonp+1$1.673 \times 10^{-27}$1.007
Neutronn0$1.675 \times 10^{-27}$1.009
Electrone⁻−1$9.109 \times 10^{-31}$0.000549

An atom is described by its atomic number $Z$ (protons) and mass number $A$ (protons + neutrons). Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons — same $Z$, different $A$.

$${}^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \qquad \text{where } A = \text{mass number}, \; Z = \text{atomic number}$$ $$\text{Number of neutrons} = A - Z$$

The Specific Charge

The specific charge of a particle is its charge-to-mass ratio, measured in C kg⁻¹.

$$\text{Specific charge} = \frac{Q}{m} \quad \text{(C kg}^{-1}\text{)}$$
Worked Example 2.1a
Calculate the specific charge of (a) a proton, (b) an alpha particle ${}^4_2\text{He}^{2+}$.
1
(a) Proton: $Q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.673 \times 10^{-27}$ kg. Specific charge $= \dfrac{1.60 \times 10^{-19}}{1.673 \times 10^{-27}} = 9.57 \times 10^7$ C kg⁻¹.
2
(b) Alpha particle: $Q = 2 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19} = 3.20 \times 10^{-19}$ C. Mass $= 4 \times 1.661 \times 10^{-27} = 6.64 \times 10^{-27}$ kg (using $1\,\text{u} = 1.661 \times 10^{-27}$ kg). Specific charge $= \dfrac{3.20 \times 10^{-19}}{6.64 \times 10^{-27}} = 4.82 \times 10^7$ C kg⁻¹.
The proton has a higher specific charge than the alpha — important in mass spectrometry and particle accelerators.

2.2 Quarks & Hadrons

Protons and neutrons are not fundamental — they are made of quarks. At AQA A Level you need to know three quark flavours:

Antiquarks have opposite charge, baryon number ($-\frac{1}{3}$) and strangeness.

Hadrons — particles made of quarks

Baryons are made of three quarks (qqq). Mesons are made of a quark–antiquark pair ($q\bar{q}$).

Conservation laws in particle interactions In all interactions, the following quantities are conserved: charge $Q$, baryon number $B$, lepton number $L$ (separately for each lepton family). Strangeness is conserved in strong interactions but NOT in weak interactions (strangeness can change by ±1 in weak decays).
Worked Example 2.2a — Beta minus decay
In beta-minus decay, a neutron decays: $\text{n} \rightarrow \text{p} + \text{e}^- + \bar{\nu}_e$. Show this is consistent in terms of quark composition.
1
Neutron: udd. Proton: uud. One d quark changes to a u quark, emitting a W⁻ boson (weak interaction).
2
The W⁻ then decays: W⁻ → e⁻ + $\bar{\nu}_e$. Check charge: $-1 = -1 + 0$ ✓. Check lepton number: $0 = 1 + (-1)$ ✓. Check baryon number: $1 = 1 + 0 + 0$ ✓.
3
The underlying quark change is d → u + W⁻. This is a weak interaction (mediated by the W⁻ boson).
Beta-plus decay is the reverse: u → d + W⁺, then W⁺ → e⁺ + νₑ.

2.3 Leptons & Neutrinos

Leptons are fundamental particles — they are not made of quarks. There are three generations, each with a charged lepton and its associated neutrino.

LeptonSymbolChargeLepton number $L_e$Lepton number $L_\mu$
Electrone⁻−1+10
Electron neutrinoνₑ0+10
Muonμ⁻−10+1
Muon neutrinoν_μ00+1

Antileptons (positron e⁺, antineutrinos $\bar{\nu}_e$, $\bar{\nu}_\mu$) have lepton number −1.

Neutrino properties Neutrinos have zero charge and almost zero mass. They interact only via the weak force, meaning they pass through matter almost undetected — billions pass through your body every second. They were originally postulated by Pauli to explain the continuous energy spectrum of electrons in beta decay (if only two particles were produced, they would each have a fixed energy — but they don't).
Worked Example 2.3a — Is this decay allowed?
Check whether the decay $\mu^- \rightarrow e^- + \nu_\mu + \bar{\nu}_e$ is allowed by conservation laws.
1
Charge: Left $= -1$. Right $= -1 + 0 + 0 = -1$ ✓
2
Muon lepton number $L_\mu$: Left $= +1$. Right $= 0 + 1 + 0 = +1$ ✓
3
Electron lepton number $L_e$: Left $= 0$. Right $= +1 + 0 + (-1) = 0$ ✓
4
Baryon number $B$: Left $= 0$. Right $= 0$ ✓. All conservation laws satisfied — the decay is allowed.
This is the actual decay mode of the muon, with a mean lifetime of ~2.2 μs.

2.4 The Four Fundamental Forces

All interactions in nature are governed by four fundamental forces, each with its own exchange particle (gauge boson).

Weak nuclearQuarks, leptons~$10^{-18}$ mW⁺, W⁻, Z⁰ bosons$10^{-6}$

The Strong Nuclear Force

The strong force holds the nucleus together against the electromagnetic repulsion between protons. It acts between nucleons (protons and neutrons) and has a very short range. Key features:

Strong force characteristics Attractive from ~0.5 fm to ~3 fm (femtometres). Repulsive below ~0.5 fm (preventing nucleons from merging). Approximately equal between p-p, p-n, and n-n pairs (charge independent). Does not act on electrons (leptons). At the quark level, it is mediated by gluons; at the nucleon level, by virtual pion exchange.

Feynman Diagrams

Feynman diagrams are a pictorial way of representing particle interactions. Time runs left to right. Straight lines are fermions (quarks/leptons); wavy or dashed lines are bosons (exchange particles).

Key interactions to know: beta-minus decay (d → u + W⁻, then W⁻ → e⁻ + $\bar{\nu}_e$), beta-plus decay (u → d + W⁺, then W⁺ → e⁺ + νₑ), and electron capture (p + e⁻ → n + νₑ via W⁺).

2.5 Antimatter & Annihilation

Every particle has an antiparticle with the same mass but opposite charge (and opposite quantum numbers such as baryon and lepton number). When a particle meets its antiparticle, they annihilate, producing two photons.

$$\text{Annihilation: } \text{e}^- + \text{e}^+ \rightarrow 2\gamma \qquad E_\gamma = m_e c^2 + E_{k,\text{particle}}$$ $$\text{Pair production: } \gamma \rightarrow \text{e}^- + \text{e}^+ \qquad E_\gamma \geq 2m_e c^2$$ $$\text{Minimum photon energy for pair production: } E_{\min} = 2m_e c^2 = 2 \times 0.511 \text{ MeV} = 1.02 \text{ MeV}$$
Why two photons in annihilation? Momentum must be conserved. If an electron and positron annihilate at rest, the total momentum is zero. A single photon always carries momentum ($p = E/c \neq 0$), so one photon is impossible. Two photons travelling in opposite directions carry equal and opposite momenta — total momentum zero ✓. Each photon has energy $E = m_e c^2 = 0.511$ MeV (if the particles are at rest).
Worked Example 2.5a — Annihilation photon energy
An electron and positron, each with kinetic energy 0.20 MeV, annihilate to produce two photons. Find the energy and frequency of each photon.
1
Total energy available $= 2(m_e c^2 + E_k) = 2(0.511 + 0.20) = 2(0.711) = 1.422$ MeV. Split equally between two photons: each photon has $E = 0.711$ MeV $= 0.711 \times 10^6 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19} = 1.138 \times 10^{-13}$ J.
2
Frequency: $f = E/h = \dfrac{1.138 \times 10^{-13}}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}} = 1.72 \times 10^{20}$ Hz. This is a gamma ray.

2.6 Photons & Electromagnetic Radiation

Light (and all electromagnetic radiation) is quantised — it comes in discrete packets called photons. The energy of a photon is proportional to its frequency.

$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$ $$h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s} \quad c = 3.00 \times 10^8 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

The electronvolt (eV) is the energy gained by an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 1 V:

$$1 \text{ eV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$$ $$1 \text{ MeV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J} \qquad 1 \text{ GeV} = 1.60 \times 10^{-10} \text{ J}$$

2.7 The Photoelectric Effect

When light of sufficiently high frequency shines on a metal surface, electrons are emitted. This cannot be explained by the wave model of light — it requires photons.

The three key observations (1) Emission is instantaneous — no delay, even at low intensity. (2) Below the threshold frequency $f_0$, no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity. (3) Above $f_0$, the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons depends on frequency, not intensity. Increasing intensity increases the number of electrons but not their energy. These facts are inexplicable with waves but follow naturally if light consists of photons, each transferring its energy to a single electron.
$$hf = \phi + E_{k,\max}$$ $$\phi = hf_0 \quad \text{(work function — minimum energy to free an electron)}$$ $$E_{k,\max} = hf - \phi = h(f - f_0) \quad \text{(Einstein's photoelectric equation)}$$
Worked Example 2.7a
Light of wavelength 250 nm shines on a sodium surface (work function $\phi = 2.28$ eV). Find (a) the energy of each photon in eV, (b) the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons, (c) the threshold wavelength for sodium.
1
(a) $E = hc/\lambda = (6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3.00 \times 10^8)/(250 \times 10^{-9}) = 7.956 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= 7.956 \times 10^{-19} / 1.60 \times 10^{-19} = 4.97$ eV.
2
(b) $E_{k,\max} = 4.97 - 2.28 = 2.69$ eV $= 2.69 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19} = 4.30 \times 10^{-19}$ J.
3
(c) At threshold: $hf_0 = \phi$. $\lambda_0 = hc/\phi = (6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3.00 \times 10^8)/(2.28 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19}) = 1.99 \times 10^{-19}/3.648 \times 10^{-19} = 545$ nm (visible green light).

2.8 Energy Levels & Atomic Spectra

Electrons in atoms can only occupy discrete energy levels. When an electron transitions between levels, it absorbs or emits a photon with energy exactly equal to the energy difference.

$$\Delta E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = E_\text{upper} - E_\text{lower}$$

Emission spectra are produced when excited atoms emit photons as electrons drop to lower levels — seen as bright coloured lines against a dark background. Absorption spectra occur when white light passes through a cool gas — the same wavelengths are absorbed, leaving dark lines. Each element has a unique spectrum, acting as a fingerprint.

Ground state and excitation The ground state is the lowest energy level an electron can occupy. Excitation means an electron absorbs energy and jumps to a higher level. Ionisation occurs when an electron gains enough energy to escape the atom entirely (energy $\geq 0$ eV on the standard scale where the ionisation level is 0 eV). For hydrogen, the ground state energy is $-13.6$ eV.
Worked Example 2.8a — Hydrogen spectrum
In hydrogen, the energies of the levels are $E_n = -13.6/n^2$ eV. Find the wavelength of the photon emitted when an electron falls from $n = 3$ to $n = 2$.
1
$E_3 = -13.6/9 = -1.511$ eV. $E_2 = -13.6/4 = -3.400$ eV.
2
$\Delta E = E_3 - E_2 = -1.511 - (-3.400) = 1.889$ eV $= 1.889 \times 1.60 \times 10^{-19} = 3.022 \times 10^{-19}$ J.
3
$\lambda = hc/\Delta E = (6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3.00 \times 10^8)/(3.022 \times 10^{-19}) = 658$ nm (red — the H-alpha line).
This is the most prominent line in the Balmer series and gives hydrogen nebulae their characteristic red colour.

2.9 Wave-Particle Duality

Light behaves as a wave (diffraction, interference) and as a particle (photoelectric effect, photon momentum). De Broglie proposed that matter also has wave properties — every moving particle has an associated wavelength.

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} \qquad \text{de Broglie wavelength}$$

Electron diffraction provides direct evidence for the wave nature of matter. When electrons are directed at a crystal or graphite, they diffract — producing a diffraction pattern just as X-rays do. The spacing of the rings matches the prediction from de Broglie's formula.

Increasing accelerating voltage reduces wavelength Electrons accelerated through voltage $V$ gain kinetic energy $eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, so $v = \sqrt{2eV/m}$ and $\lambda = h/mv = h/\sqrt{2meV}$. Higher voltage → faster electrons → shorter wavelength → diffraction rings become smaller (closer together) because $\lambda$ is smaller relative to the atomic spacing.
Worked Example 2.9a — de Broglie wavelength of an electron
An electron is accelerated through 5000 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.
1
Kinetic energy: $E_k = eV = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \times 5000 = 8.00 \times 10^{-16}$ J.
2
Momentum: $p = \sqrt{2m_e E_k} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 8.00 \times 10^{-16}} = \sqrt{1.458 \times 10^{-45}} = 1.207 \times 10^{-23}$ kg m s⁻¹.
3
$\lambda = h/p = (6.63 \times 10^{-34})/(1.207 \times 10^{-23}) = 5.49 \times 10^{-11}$ m $= 0.055$ nm. This is comparable to atomic spacings in crystals — which is why electron diffraction works.

State the quark composition of (a) a proton, (b) a neutron, (c) a π⁺ meson, (d) a K⁻ meson. For each, verify the charge using quark charges.

Q2
Light of frequency $6.5 \times 10^{14}$ Hz is shone onto a metal surface with work function 1.8 eV. (a) Calculate the energy of each photon in eV. (b) Calculate the maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons in joules. (c) Calculate the stopping potential.
(a) $E = hf = 6.63\times10^{-34}\times6.5\times10^{14} = 4.31\times10^{-19}$ J $= 4.31\times10^{-19}/1.60\times10^{-19} = 2.69$ eV. (b) $E_{k,\max} = 2.69 - 1.8 = 0.89$ eV $= 0.89\times1.60\times10^{-19} = 1.42\times10^{-19}$ J. (c) Stopping potential: $eV_s = E_{k,\max}$. $V_s = 1.42\times10^{-19}/1.60\times10^{-19} = 0.89$ V.

Q3
An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 2500 V. Find (a) its kinetic energy in joules, (b) its momentum, (c) its de Broglie wavelength.
(a) $E_k = eV = 1.60\times10^{-19}\times2500 = 4.00\times10^{-16}$ J. (b) $p = \sqrt{2m_eE_k} = \sqrt{2\times9.11\times10^{-31}\times4.00\times10^{-16}} = \sqrt{7.288\times10^{-46}} = 8.537\times10^{-23}$ kg m s⁻¹. Q4
Determine whether the following interactions are possible. For each one that is not, state which conservation law is violated. (a) p + p → p + p + π⁰ (b) p → n + e⁺ + νₑ (c) n → p + μ⁻ + $\bar{\nu}_\mu$ (d) K⁺ → π⁺ + π⁰

ALLOWED (this is beta-plus decay). (c) Charge: 0=1+(-1)+0 ✓. Baryon: 1=1+0+0 ✓. Lepton $L_\mu$: 0 = 0+1+(-1) = 0 ✓. Lepton $L_e$: 0 = 0+0+0 ✓. But the muon is too heavy — mass-energy not conserved since muon (105.7 MeV) > neutron-proton mass difference (~1.3 MeV). NOT ALLOWED (energy conservation). (d) Charge: +1 = +1+0 ✓. Baryon: 0=0+0 ✓. Strangeness: K⁺ has S=+1, π⁺ and π⁰ have S=0. So ΔS = -1. This is a weak decay (strangeness not conserved) — ALLOWED via weak interaction.

Q5
A photon of wavelength 1.20 × 10⁻¹² m undergoes pair production, creating an electron-positron pair. (a) Calculate the energy of the photon in MeV. (b) The rest energy of an electron is 0.511 MeV. Show that pair production is energetically possible. (c) Calculate the total kinetic energy of the electron-positron pair produced.
(a) $E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34}\times3.00\times10^8)/(1.20\times10^{-12}) = 1.658\times10^{-13}$ J $= 1.658\times10^{-13}/1.60\times10^{-13}$ MeV $= 1.036$ MeV. (b) Minimum energy for pair production $= 2m_ec^2 = 2\times0.511 = 1.022$ MeV. Since $1.036 > 1.022$ MeV, pair production is possible ✓. (c) Total KE $= 1.036 - 1.022 = 0.014$ MeV $= 14$ keV shared between the pair.

Exam-style
Q6
A student investigates the photoelectric effect using different metals. She finds that the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons varies linearly with the frequency of light used. (a) Sketch a graph of $E_{k,\max}$ against $f$, labelling the gradient, x-intercept and y-intercept in terms of physical quantities. (b) Explain why the graph does not extend below the x-axis. (c) She uses two metals: sodium ($\phi = 2.28$ eV) and gold ($\phi = 5.10$ eV). For light of frequency $1.50 \times 10^{15}$ Hz, calculate $E_{k,\max}$ for each metal and explain why only one produces photoelectrons. (d) Explain why increasing the intensity of light below the threshold frequency never produces photoelectrons, even after a long time.
(a) Straight line, positive gradient = $h$ (Planck's constant). x-intercept at $f = f_0 = \phi/h$ (threshold frequency). y-intercept at $-\phi$ (negative work function — but this region is unphysical since $f < f_0$ gives no emission). The physical graph starts at $(f_0, 0)$ and rises with gradient $h$. (b) Below the threshold frequency, photon energy $hf < \phi$, so no electron has enough energy to escape the surface. $E_{k,\max}$ cannot be negative (kinetic energy is always ≥ 0), so the graph simply does not exist below $f_0$. (c) Photon energy: $E = hf = 6.63\times10^{-34}\times1.50\times10^{15} = 9.945\times10^{-19}$ J $= 6.22$ eV. Sodium: $E_{k,\max} = 6.22 - 2.28 = 3.94$ eV ✓ — photoelectrons emitted. Gold: $E_{k,\max} = 6.22 - 5.10 = 1.12$ eV ✓ — photoelectrons also emitted! Both metals produce photoelectrons for this frequency. For gold to show no emission, frequency must satisfy $hf < 5.10$ eV, i.e. $f < 5.10\times1.60\times10^{-19}/6.63\times10^{-34} = 1.23\times10^{15}$ Hz. (d) In the photon model, each photon interacts with one electron and transfers all its energy to that one electron. Below the threshold, each photon has insufficient energy to liberate the electron regardless of how many photons arrive (intensity). Unlike the wave model, photons do not accumulate energy in an electron over time — it's a one-photon, one-electron interaction. Increasing intensity only increases the number of photons per second, not the energy of each photon.
