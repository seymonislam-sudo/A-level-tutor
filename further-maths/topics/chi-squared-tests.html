Chi-squared Tests

A powerful family of hypothesis tests for categorical data — testing whether observed frequencies match a theoretical model, or whether two categorical variables are independent.

10.1 The Chi-squared Distribution

If $Z_1, Z_2, \ldots, Z_\nu$ are independent standard normal random variables, then $X = Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 + \cdots + Z_\nu^2$ follows a chi-squared distribution with $\nu$ degrees of freedom, written $X \sim \chi^2(\nu)$.

$$X \sim \chi^2(\nu) \implies E(X) = \nu, \quad \text{Var}(X) = 2\nu$$

The chi-squared distribution is right-skewed, defined only for $x \geq 0$, and becomes more symmetric as $\nu$ increases. Critical values are read from tables — in an exam, you'll be given the $\chi^2$ tables.

Reading the table The table gives $\chi^2_{\nu,\alpha}$ — the value exceeded with probability $\alpha$. For a test at the 5% level with $\nu$ degrees of freedom, compare your test statistic to $\chi^2_{\nu, 0.05}$. If your statistic exceeds this critical value, reject $H_0$.

10.2 Goodness of Fit Tests

A goodness of fit test tests whether observed data are consistent with a specified theoretical distribution. The test statistic measures how far observed frequencies deviate from expected frequencies.

$$X^2 = \sum_{\text{all cells}} \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$$

where $O$ = observed frequency, $E$ = expected frequency. Under $H_0$, $X^2 \sim \chi^2(\nu)$ approximately.

Full test procedure (1) State $H_0$ (data follows the proposed distribution) and $H_1$ (it does not). (2) Calculate expected frequencies under $H_0$. (3) Combine any cells with $E < 5$. (4) Calculate $X^2 = \sum(O-E)^2/E$. (5) Find degrees of freedom $\nu$. (6) Compare $X^2$ to critical value from tables. (7) State conclusion in context.
⚠ The E < 5 rule Any cell with expected frequency less than 5 must be merged with an adjacent cell before computing $X^2$. This ensures the chi-squared approximation is valid. After merging, recalculate expected frequencies and adjust degrees of freedom accordingly.
Worked Example 10.2a
A die is rolled 120 times. The observed frequencies for faces 1–6 are: 18, 22, 17, 25, 19, 19. Test at the 5% level whether the die is fair.
1
$H_0$: The die is fair (each face equally likely). $H_1$: The die is not fair.
2
Expected frequency for each face: $E = 120/6 = 20$. All $E \geq 5$, so no merging needed.
3
$$X^2 = \frac{(18-20)^2}{20} + \frac{(22-20)^2}{20} + \frac{(17-20)^2}{20} + \frac{(25-20)^2}{20} + \frac{(19-20)^2}{20} + \frac{(19-20)^2}{20}$$ $$= \frac{4+4+9+25+1+1}{20} = \frac{44}{20} = 2.2$$
4
Degrees of freedom: $\nu = 6 - 1 = 5$. Critical value at 5%: $\chi^2_{5, 0.05} = 11.070$.
5
$2.2 < 11.070$, so we do not reject $H_0$. There is insufficient evidence at the 5% level to conclude the die is unfair.

10.3 Degrees of Freedom

The degrees of freedom for a goodness of fit test is:

$$\nu = (\text{number of cells after merging}) - 1 - (\text{number of parameters estimated from data})$$

The "minus 1" accounts for the constraint that all frequencies must sum to $n$ (the total). Each parameter estimated from the data (e.g. estimating $\lambda$ for Poisson, or $\mu$ and $\sigma$ for Normal) costs one additional degree of freedom.

Distribution testedParameters estimated$\nu$
Discrete uniform / specified proportions0$k - 1$
Poisson (estimating $\lambda$ from data)1 ($\lambda$)$k - 2$
Binomial (estimating $p$ from data)1 ($p$)$k - 2$
Normal (estimating $\mu$ and $\sigma$)2$k - 3$

$k$ = number of cells after merging. If a parameter is given (not estimated), do not subtract it.

10.4 Estimating Parameters from Data

When the distribution parameters are not specified in $H_0$, estimate them from the observed data using standard estimators, then use these to calculate expected frequencies.

Worked Example 10.4a
The table shows the number of accidents per week at a factory over 50 weeks. Test at 5% whether a Poisson distribution is a good fit.
1
Accidents ($x$)01234+
Frequency ($O$)18161042
2
Estimate $\lambda$: $\hat{\lambda} = \bar{x} = \dfrac{0(18)+1(16)+2(10)+3(4)+4(2)}{50} = \dfrac{0+16+20+12+8}{50} = \dfrac{56}{50} = 1.12$
3
Calculate expected frequencies using $\text{Po}(1.12)$:
$E_0 = 50e^{-1.12} = 50(0.3263) = 16.31$
$E_1 = 50 \cdot 1.12 \cdot e^{-1.12} = 18.27$
$E_2 = 50 \cdot \frac{1.12^2}{2}e^{-1.12} = 10.23$
$E_3 = 50 \cdot \frac{1.12^3}{6}e^{-1.12} = 3.82$
$E_{4+} = 50 - (16.31+18.27+10.23+3.82) = 1.37$
4
Merge cells: $E_{4+} = 1.37 < 5$, so merge "3" and "4+" into "3+": $O = 6$, $E = 5.19$. Now 4 cells.
5
$X^2 = \dfrac{(18-16.31)^2}{16.31} + \dfrac{(16-18.27)^2}{18.27} + \dfrac{(10-10.23)^2}{10.23} + \dfrac{(6-5.19)^2}{5.19}$ $\approx 0.175 + 0.282 + 0.005 + 0.126 = 0.588$
6
$\nu = 4 - 1 - 1 = 2$. Critical value: $\chi^2_{2,0.05} = 5.991$. Since $0.588 < 5.991$, do not reject $H_0$. A Poisson distribution is a good fit.
The $-1$ is for the cell constraint; the extra $-1$ is because we estimated $\lambda$ from the data.

10.5 Contingency Tables

A contingency table records the joint frequencies of two categorical variables. A chi-squared test on a contingency table tests whether the two variables are independent.

$$H_0: \text{the two variables are independent} \qquad H_1: \text{they are not independent}$$ $$E_{ij} = \frac{R_i \times C_j}{N} \qquad \nu = (r-1)(c-1)$$

where $R_i$ = row $i$ total, $C_j$ = column $j$ total, $N$ = grand total, $r$ = number of rows, $c$ = number of columns.

No parameters to estimate Unlike goodness of fit tests, the expected frequencies in a contingency table are calculated from the observed row and column totals — no distribution parameters need to be estimated. So there is no extra $-1$ in the degrees of freedom formula.

10.6 Yates' Correction

For a 2×2 contingency table ($\nu = 1$), the chi-squared approximation can be poor. Yates' continuity correction improves accuracy by subtracting $\frac{1}{2}$ from each $|O - E|$ before squaring:

$$X^2_{\text{Yates}} = \sum \frac{\left(|O - E| - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2}{E}$$
⚠ Only for 2×2 tables Yates' correction only applies to 2×2 contingency tables (giving $\nu = 1$). Do not apply it to larger tables or goodness of fit tests. The correction always reduces the test statistic, making it harder to reject $H_0$ — it makes the test more conservative.
Worked Example 10.6a
100 patients are given either Drug A or a placebo. Results: Drug A — 30 improved, 20 did not. Placebo — 25 improved, 25 did not. Test at 5% using Yates' correction.
1
ImprovedNot improvedTotal
Drug A302050
Placebo252550
Total5545100
2
Expected frequencies: $E_{11}=50\times55/100=27.5$, $E_{12}=22.5$, $E_{21}=27.5$, $E_{22}=22.5$.
3
Apply Yates' correction: $$X^2 = \frac{(|30-27.5|-0.5)^2}{27.5}+\frac{(|20-22.5|-0.5)^2}{22.5}+\frac{(|25-27.5|-0.5)^2}{27.5}+\frac{(|25-22.5|-0.5)^2}{22.5}$$ $$= \frac{4}{27.5}+\frac{4}{22.5}+\frac{4}{27.5}+\frac{4}{22.5} = 0.1455+0.1778+0.1455+0.1778 = 0.647$$
4
$\nu = 1$. Critical value $\chi^2_{1,0.05} = 3.841$. Since $0.647 < 3.841$, do not reject $H_0$. Insufficient evidence that drug outcome and treatment are associated.

A spinner with 5 equal sectors (labelled 1–5) is spun 200 times. Observed frequencies are 36, 45, 38, 42, 39. Test at the 5% significance level whether the spinner is fair.
$H_0$: spinner is fair; $H_1$: not fair. $E=40$ for each sector. $X^2=\frac{16+25+4+4+1}{40}=\frac{50}{40}=1.25$. $\nu=4$. Critical value $=9.488$. Since $1.25 < 9.488$, do not reject $H_0$. Insufficient evidence that the spinner is unfair.

Q5
Explain why the chi-squared test statistic $X^2 = \sum(O-E)^2/E$ is always non-negative, and explain in words why a very large value of $X^2$ provides evidence against $H_0$. Also explain why we only ever carry out a one-tailed test in the upper tail.
$X^2\geq0$ because each term $(O-E)^2/E$ is a squared quantity divided by a positive number. A large $X^2$ means the observed frequencies deviate substantially from the expected frequencies under $H_0$, suggesting the model is a poor fit. We only test in the upper tail because $X^2=0$ would mean perfect agreement ($O=E$ for all cells), which is actually too good to be real data and wouldn't lead us to reject the model. We're only looking for evidence of poor fit (too large), not suspiciously perfect fit (though a very small $X^2$ can indicate data fabrication).
[4 marks]

Hard
Q6
A researcher claims that birth month is uniformly distributed across the 12 months. A sample of 360 people shows the following frequencies (months grouped into quarters): Q1: 102, Q2: 84, Q3: 96, Q4: 78. (i) Test at 5% whether births are uniformly distributed across quarters. (ii) The researcher then splits into all 12 months. Explain why having more cells generally increases the power of the test to detect non-uniformity, but also why there is a risk of having too many cells.
(i) $H_0$: uniform across quarters. $E=90$ for each. $X^2=\frac{(102-90)^2}{90}+\frac{(84-90)^2}{90}+\frac{(96-90)^2}{90}+\frac{(78-90)^2}{90}=\frac{144+36+36+144}{90}=\frac{360}{90}=4$. $\nu=3$. Critical value $=7.815$. Since $4<7.815$, do not reject $H_0$. No significant evidence of non-uniformity across quarters. (ii) More cells (months) give the test more opportunities to detect deviations from uniformity in specific months, increasing power. However, more cells means smaller expected frequencies per cell — if $E<5$, cells must be merged (reducing information), and the chi-squared approximation becomes unreliable. There is also a risk of overfitting: with 12 cells and only 360 observations, random variation looks more like a pattern.
[9 marks]
Q7
In an $r \times c$ contingency table with grand total $N$, expected frequency $E_{ij} = R_i C_j / N$. (i) Show that $\sum_j E_{ij} = R_i$ (row totals are preserved). (ii) Show that when $H_0$ (independence) is true and $N$ is large, the test statistic $X^2 = \sum_{i,j}(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2/E_{ij}$ has approximately a $\chi^2((r-1)(c-1))$ distribution by explaining why the degrees of freedom equals $(r-1)(c-1)$.
(i) $\sum_j E_{ij}=\sum_j\frac{R_iC_j}{N}=\frac{R_i}{N}\sum_jC_j=\frac{R_i}{N}\cdot N=R_i$ ✓. Similarly $\sum_i E_{ij}=C_j$. (ii) There are $rc$ cells. The row totals impose $r$ constraints ($\sum_j O_{ij}=R_i$ for each $i$) and column totals impose $c$ constraints ($\sum_i O_{ij}=C_j$ for each $j$), but these share 1 constraint (grand total $N$), so the total number of independent constraints is $r+c-1$. Degrees of freedom $=rc-(r+c-1)=rc-r-c+1=(r-1)(c-1)$. Roughly speaking, once we know any $(r-1)(c-1)$ entries of the table (plus all marginal totals), the remaining entries are determined — so there are only $(r-1)(c-1)$ free cells.
