13.1 Momentum & Conservation

The linear momentum of a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ is $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$. Momentum is a vector quantity measured in kg m s⁻¹.

$$\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v} \qquad \text{(kg m s}^{-1}\text{)}$$ $$\text{Conservation of momentum: } m_1\mathbf{u}_1 + m_2\mathbf{u}_2 = m_1\mathbf{v}_1 + m_2\mathbf{v}_2$$

The principle of conservation of linear momentum states that the total momentum of a system of particles is constant provided no external forces act. This follows directly from Newton's third law — internal forces between particles cancel in pairs.

When is momentum conserved? Momentum is conserved when there is no net external force on the system. In collision problems, the collision itself is so brief that external forces (gravity, friction) have negligible impulse during the impact, so momentum is always conserved during collisions. However, between collisions, external forces act and momentum changes.

13.2 Impulse & the Impulse-Momentum Theorem

The impulse of a force $\mathbf{F}$ acting over time interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is:

$$\mathbf{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \mathbf{F}\,dt \qquad \text{(N s = kg m s}^{-1}\text{)}$$

For a constant force: $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{F}(t_2 - t_1) = \mathbf{F}\Delta t$.

The impulse-momentum theorem connects impulse to the change in momentum:

$$\mathbf{J} = m\mathbf{v} - m\mathbf{u} = \Delta\mathbf{p}$$

This follows directly from Newton's second law: $\mathbf{F} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt}$, so $\int \mathbf{F}\,dt = \Delta\mathbf{p}$.

Worked Example 13.2a
A force $F = (6t^2 - 4t)$ N acts on a 2 kg particle for $0 \leq t \leq 3$ s. If the particle has initial velocity 5 m s⁻¹, find its final velocity.
1
Impulse: $J = \displaystyle\int_0^3 (6t^2-4t)\,dt = \left[2t^3 - 2t^2\right]_0^3 = (54-18) - 0 = 36$ N s
2
Impulse-momentum: $J = m(v - u)$. $36 = 2(v - 5)$. $v = 5 + 18 = 23$ m s⁻¹

13.3 Collisions in 1D & Newton's Law of Restitution

For a direct collision between two particles moving along the same line, we have two equations:

$$\text{Conservation of momentum: } m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$$ $$\text{Newton's Law of Restitution: } v_2 - v_1 = e(u_1 - u_2)$$

where $e$ is the coefficient of restitution, $0 \leq e \leq 1$. The law states: the speed of separation equals $e$ times the speed of approach.

Value of $e$Type of collisionEnergy
$e = 1$Perfectly elasticNo kinetic energy lost
$0 < e < 1$Partially elasticSome KE lost as heat/sound
$e = 0$Perfectly inelastic (coalesce)Maximum KE lost
Worked Example 13.3a
Particle A (mass 3 kg, velocity 6 m s⁻¹) collides directly with particle B (mass 2 kg, velocity 1 m s⁻¹) moving in the same direction. Given $e = 0.5$, find the velocities after collision and the kinetic energy lost.
1
Conservation of momentum: $3(6) + 2(1) = 3v_A + 2v_B \Rightarrow 20 = 3v_A + 2v_B$   (1)
2
Newton's law: $v_B - v_A = 0.5(6-1) = 2.5$   (2)
3
From (2): $v_B = v_A + 2.5$. Sub into (1): $3v_A + 2(v_A + 2.5) = 20 \Rightarrow 5v_A = 15 \Rightarrow v_A = 3$ m s⁻¹, $v_B = 5.5$ m s⁻¹.
4
KE before: $\frac{1}{2}(3)(36) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(1) = 54 + 1 = 55$ J. KE after: $\frac{1}{2}(3)(9) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(30.25) = 13.5 + 30.25 = 43.75$ J. KE lost $= 55 - 43.75 = 11.25$ J.

13.4 Oblique Collisions

When a smooth sphere collides obliquely with a smooth plane (or another sphere), the impulse acts only along the line of centres (perpendicular to the surface). Components parallel to the surface are unchanged.

$$\text{Along the line of centres: Newton's Law of Restitution applies}$$ $$\text{Perpendicular to line of centres: velocity component unchanged}$$
Smooth sphere hitting a smooth wall Take the wall as horizontal. The component of velocity parallel to the wall is unchanged. The component perpendicular to the wall satisfies $v_\perp = e \cdot u_\perp$ (speed of separation = $e$ × speed of approach, reversed in direction).
Worked Example 13.4a — Ball striking a wall
A ball strikes a smooth vertical wall with velocity $(4\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j})$ m s⁻¹, where $\mathbf{i}$ is perpendicular to the wall (towards it) and $\mathbf{j}$ is parallel. Given $e = 0.6$, find the velocity after impact and the angle of rebound.
1
Component perpendicular to wall: $u_\perp = 4$ m s⁻¹ (towards wall). After: $v_\perp = -e \cdot u_\perp = -0.6(4) = -2.4$ m s⁻¹ (away from wall).
2
Component parallel to wall: unchanged. $v_\parallel = -3$ m s⁻¹.
3
Velocity after: $(-2.4\mathbf{i} - 3\mathbf{j})$ m s⁻¹. Speed $= \sqrt{5.76 + 9} = \sqrt{14.76} \approx 3.84$ m s⁻¹.
4
Angle to wall: $\tan\theta = \dfrac{2.4}{3} \Rightarrow \theta = \arctan(0.8) \approx 38.7°$.
Note the angle of rebound ≠ angle of incidence (which was $\arctan(4/3) \approx 53.1°$ to the wall) because $e < 1$.
Worked Example 13.4b — Oblique collision between spheres
Two equal smooth spheres collide when their line of centres makes 30° to the direction of motion of sphere A (which was moving at 10 m s⁻¹; sphere B was at rest). With $e = 0.8$, find the velocities after impact.
1
Resolve A's velocity: along line of centres: $10\cos30° = 5\sqrt{3}$ m s⁻¹; perpendicular: $10\sin30° = 5$ m s⁻¹.
2
Along line of centres, momentum: $m(5\sqrt{3}) = mv_A' + mv_B'$, so $v_A' + v_B' = 5\sqrt{3}$. Newton's law: $v_B' - v_A' = 0.8(5\sqrt{3}) = 4\sqrt{3}$.
3
Adding: $2v_B' = 9\sqrt{3}$, so $v_B' = \dfrac{9\sqrt{3}}{2}$ m s⁻¹ along line of centres. $v_A' = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ m s⁻¹ along line of centres.
4
Perpendicular component of A unchanged: still 5 m s⁻¹. B had no perpendicular component and still has none. Compose final velocities to get speed and direction for each sphere.

13.5 Coefficient of Restitution — Deeper Results

Energy lost in a direct collision

$$\text{KE lost} = \frac{m_1 m_2 (1-e^2)(u_1-u_2)^2}{2(m_1+m_2)}$$

This confirms: KE lost $= 0$ when $e = 1$ (elastic), and KE lost is maximum when $e = 0$ (perfectly inelastic). The loss is always non-negative since $e \leq 1$.

Collision with a fixed surface

For a particle bouncing on a horizontal floor: if it falls from height $h_0$, the height after the $n$th bounce is $h_n = e^{2n} h_0$. The total distance travelled is:

$$d_{\text{total}} = h_0\left(\frac{1+e^2}{1-e^2}\right)$$
Worked Example 13.5a
A ball is dropped from 2 m. The coefficient of restitution between the ball and the floor is $e = 0.6$. Find the height after the 3rd bounce and the total distance travelled before the ball comes to rest.
1
Height after $n$th bounce: $h_n = e^{2n}h_0 = (0.36)^n \times 2$.
2
After 3rd bounce: $h_3 = (0.36)^3 \times 2 = 0.046656 \times 2 \approx 0.0933$ m.
3
Total distance: $d = 2 \times \dfrac{1+0.36}{1-0.36} = 2 \times \dfrac{1.36}{0.64} = 2 \times 2.125 = 4.25$ m.

13.6 Successive Collisions

When three or more particles are in a line, collisions occur sequentially. After each collision, check whether the resulting velocities cause a further collision — a particle can only catch up with the one ahead if it's moving faster.

⚠ Order matters Always determine which collision happens first. After each impact, recheck all relative velocities to see if subsequent collisions are possible. It is physically impossible for a faster particle to pass through a slower one — if $v_A > v_B$ after a collision and they're moving in the same direction, another collision will occur.
Worked Example 13.6a
Three particles A (3 kg, 4 m s⁻¹), B (2 kg, 0 m s⁻¹), C (1 kg, 0 m s⁻¹) lie in that order on a smooth surface. $e = 0.5$ for all collisions. Find all velocities after all collisions are complete.
1
A hits B: Momentum: $3(4)+0 = 3v_A+2v_B \Rightarrow 12=3v_A+2v_B$. Newton's: $v_B-v_A=0.5(4)=2$. Solving: $v_A = \frac{8}{5}=1.6$ m s⁻¹, $v_B = 3.6$ m s⁻¹.
2
Check: $v_B = 3.6 > v_A = 1.6$ ✓ — A and B separate. B moves towards C.
3
B hits C: Momentum: $2(3.6)+0 = 2v_B+v_C \Rightarrow 7.2=2v_B+v_C$. Newton's: $v_C-v_B=0.5(3.6)=1.8$. Solving: $v_B=\frac{7.2-1.8}{3}=\frac{5.4}{3}=1.8$ m s⁻¹, $v_C = 3.6$ m s⁻¹.
4
Check if A catches B: $v_A = 1.6 < v_B = 1.8$ ✓ — no further collisions. Final: A at 1.6, B at 1.8, C at 3.6 m s⁻¹.

13.7 Variable Mass (Introduction)

When a body's mass changes with time (e.g. a rocket expelling fuel, or a raindrop collecting mass), Newton's second law must be applied carefully. The rocket equation is:

$$m\frac{dv}{dt} = F_{\text{ext}} - u\frac{dm}{dt}$$

where $m$ is the current mass, $v$ is the body's velocity, $F_{\text{ext}}$ is the net external force, and $u$ is the speed of the ejected mass relative to the rocket. The term $-u\frac{dm}{dt}$ is the thrust. Since $\frac{dm}{dt} < 0$ (mass decreasing), thrust is positive (forward).

Derivation insight Consider a rocket of mass $m$ at time $t$. In time $\delta t$ it ejects mass $|\delta m|$ at relative speed $u$. By conservation of momentum applied to the rocket + ejected gas system: $mv = (m+\delta m)(v+\delta v) + |\delta m|(v - u)$. Expanding and taking limits gives the rocket equation.
Worked Example 13.7a
A rocket in deep space (no gravity) has initial mass 1000 kg (including 700 kg fuel). It ejects gas at 3000 m s⁻¹ relative to itself. Find its speed when all fuel is exhausted.
1
No external forces: $m\dfrac{dv}{dt} = -u\dfrac{dm}{dt}$ where $u = 3000$ m s⁻¹ and $\dfrac{dm}{dt} < 0$.
2
Rearrange: $dv = -u\dfrac{dm}{m}$. Integrate: $v - 0 = -u\ln\!\left(\dfrac{m_f}{m_0}\right) = u\ln\!\left(\dfrac{m_0}{m_f}\right)$
3
$m_0 = 1000$ kg, $m_f = 300$ kg (only shell remains). $v = 3000\ln\!\left(\dfrac{1000}{300}\right) = 3000\ln(10/3) \approx 3000 \times 1.204 = 3612$ m s⁻¹.
This is the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation — the fundamental result of rocket propulsion theory.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
A force $F = (12 - 3t)$ N acts on a 4 kg particle for $0 \leq t \leq 4$ s. The particle starts from rest. Find (a) the impulse of the force, (b) the final velocity, (c) the velocity when $F = 0$.
(a) $J=\int_0^4(12-3t)dt=[12t-\frac{3t^2}{2}]_0^4=48-24=24$ N s. (b) $J=mv-mu$: $24=4v-0$, so $v=6$ m s⁻¹. (c) $F=0$ when $t=4$ s, which is the end of the interval, so $v=6$ m s⁻¹.
[6 marks]
Q2
Particle P (mass 5 kg, velocity 8 m s⁻¹) collides directly with particle Q (mass 3 kg, velocity 2 m s⁻¹) moving in the same direction. After collision P has velocity 5 m s⁻¹. Find (a) Q's velocity after collision, (b) the coefficient of restitution, (c) kinetic energy lost.
(a) Momentum: $5(8)+3(2)=5(5)+3v_Q \Rightarrow 46=25+3v_Q \Rightarrow v_Q=7$ m s⁻¹. (b) Speed of approach $=8-2=6$; speed of separation $=7-5=2$. $e=2/6=1/3$. (c) KE before $=\frac{1}{2}(5)(64)+\frac{1}{2}(3)(4)=160+6=166$ J. KE after $=\frac{1}{2}(5)(25)+\frac{1}{2}(3)(49)=62.5+73.5=136$ J. KE lost $=30$ J.
[7 marks]
Q3
A ball is dropped from height 5 m onto a floor with coefficient of restitution $e = 0.7$. Find (a) the height reached after the first bounce, (b) the height after the 4th bounce, (c) the total distance travelled.
(a) $h_1=e^2\times5=0.49\times5=2.45$ m. (b) $h_4=(0.49)^4\times5=(0.0576)(5)=0.288$ m. (c) Total $=5\times\frac{1+0.49}{1-0.49}=5\times\frac{1.49}{0.51}=5\times2.922=14.6$ m.
[6 marks]
Q4
A ball moving at $12$ m s⁻¹ strikes a smooth wall at 60° to the wall. Given $e = 0.5$, find the speed and direction of the ball after impact.
Component parallel to wall: $12\cos60°=6$ m s⁻¹ (unchanged). Component perpendicular to wall (towards wall): $12\sin60°=6\sqrt{3}$ m s⁻¹. After: $v_\perp=e\times6\sqrt{3}=3\sqrt{3}$ m s⁻¹ (away). Speed $=\sqrt{36+27}=\sqrt{63}=3\sqrt{7}\approx7.94$ m s⁻¹. Angle to wall: $\tan\theta=\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{6}=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, so $\theta=\arctan(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})\approx40.9°$.
[6 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q5
Particles A (mass $m$, velocity $u$) and B (mass $2m$, at rest) collide directly with coefficient of restitution $e$. (a) Find the velocities of A and B after the collision in terms of $m$, $u$ and $e$. (b) Show that A reverses direction if and only if $e > \frac{1}{2}$. (c) Find the fraction of kinetic energy lost.
(a) Momentum: $mu=mv_A+2mv_B \Rightarrow u=v_A+2v_B$. Newton's: $v_B-v_A=eu$. From second: $v_B=v_A+eu$. Sub: $u=v_A+2v_A+2eu \Rightarrow v_A=\frac{u(1-2e)}{3}$, $v_B=\frac{u(1+e)}{3}$. (b) A reverses if $v_A<0$, i.e. $1-2e<0$, i.e. $e>\frac{1}{2}$ ✓. (c) Initial KE $=\frac{1}{2}mu^2$. Final KE $=\frac{1}{2}m\left(\frac{u(1-2e)}{3}\right)^2+\frac{1}{2}(2m)\left(\frac{u(1+e)}{3}\right)^2=\frac{mu^2}{18}[(1-2e)^2+2(1+e)^2]=\frac{mu^2}{18}[1-4e+4e^2+2+4e+2e^2]=\frac{mu^2}{18}[3+6e^2]=\frac{mu^2(1+2e^2)}{6}$. Fraction lost $=1-\frac{(1+2e^2)/6}{1/2}=1-\frac{(1+2e^2)}{3}=\frac{2(1-e^2)}{3}$.
[10 marks]
Q6
A rocket of initial mass 500 kg (400 kg fuel) ejects gas at 2500 m s⁻¹ relative to the rocket. It fires vertically upward against gravity ($g = 9.8$ m s⁻¹²). The fuel burns at a rate of 20 kg s⁻¹. Find the initial acceleration and the velocity when the fuel runs out (use the rocket equation).
Thrust $=u|\dot{m}|=2500\times20=50000$ N. Initial weight $=500\times9.8=4900$ N. Net upward force $=50000-4900=45100$ N. Initial acceleration $=\frac{45100}{500}=90.2$ m s⁻². For velocity when fuel exhausted: use rocket equation $m\frac{dv}{dt}=-mg-u\frac{dm}{dt}$ (gravity acts down). Fuel lasts $400/20=20$ s. Integrating (approximately, treating $g$ as constant): $\Delta v=u\ln\frac{m_0}{m_f}-g\Delta t=2500\ln\frac{500}{100}-9.8(20)=2500\ln5-196=2500(1.609)-196=4023-196=3827$ m s⁻¹.
[8 marks]

Q7
Three identical particles A, B, C of mass $m$ lie in a line with B between A and C. A moves at speed $u$ towards B (which is at rest), and C is at rest. All collisions have the same coefficient of restitution $e$. (i) Show that after A hits B, B moves at $\frac{u(1+e)}{2}$ and A at $\frac{u(1-e)}{2}$. (ii) Show that B then hits C, and find the velocity of C after B hits C. (iii) Determine the condition on $e$ for A to hit B a second time. (iv) Find the total kinetic energy remaining after all collisions are complete when $e = 1$ (elastic). Comment.
(i) Momentum: $mu=mv_A+mv_B\Rightarrow u=v_A+v_B$. Newton's: $v_B-v_A=eu$. Adding: $2v_B=u(1+e)\Rightarrow v_B=\frac{u(1+e)}{2}$, $v_A=\frac{u(1-e)}{2}$ ✓. (ii) $v_B=\frac{u(1+e)}{2}>0$ always (since $e\geq0$), so B moves towards C ✓. B hits C: identical particles, same $e$. By the same formula with $v_B$ as initial speed: after, C gets speed $\frac{v_B(1+e)}{2}=\frac{u(1+e)^2}{4}$, and B gets $\frac{v_B(1-e)}{2}=\frac{u(1+e)(1-e)}{4}=\frac{u(1-e^2)}{4}$. (iii) A hits B again if $v_A>v_B'$ (A is faster than B after B-C collision): $\frac{u(1-e)}{2}>\frac{u(1-e^2)}{4}\Rightarrow 2(1-e)>(1-e^2)=(1-e)(1+e)\Rightarrow 2>(1+e)$ (dividing by $1-e>0$ for $e<1$): $e<1$. So A always hits B again unless $e=1$ (elastic case). (iv) For $e=1$: $v_A=0$, $v_B=0$, $v_C=u$. Total KE $=\frac{1}{2}mu^2$ — all KE transferred to C. Energy is conserved in elastic collisions ✓. The original KE of A has been perfectly transferred to C, with A and B stationary — a classic elastic chain result.
[12 marks]
Q8
A smooth ball is projected from a point on a smooth horizontal floor with speed $V$ at angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal. It bounces repeatedly. The coefficient of restitution between the ball and the floor is $e$. (i) Show that the horizontal distances between successive bounces form a geometric sequence, and find the common ratio. (ii) Find the total horizontal distance travelled before the ball comes to rest. (iii) Find the total time of flight.
(i) Horizontal velocity $V_x=V\cos\alpha$ is unchanged throughout (floor is smooth, no horizontal impulse). Vertical velocity just before first bounce: $V_y=V\sin\alpha$ (taking downward as positive at impact). After first bounce: vertical speed $=eV\sin\alpha$. Time of $n$th flight: $t_n=\frac{2e^{n-1}V\sin\alpha}{g}$ (using $e^{n-1}$ for the vertical speed before the $n$th impact). Horizontal distance of $n$th flight: $d_n=V\cos\alpha\cdot t_n=\frac{2V^2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha}{g}\cdot e^{n-1}=\frac{V^2\sin2\alpha}{g}\cdot e^{n-1}$. This is geometric with first term $d_1=\frac{V^2\sin2\alpha}{g}$ and common ratio $e$ ✓. (ii) Total $=d_1\cdot\frac{1}{1-e}=\frac{V^2\sin2\alpha}{g(1-e)}$ (geometric series, $e<1$). (iii) Total time $=\sum_{n=1}^\infty t_n=\frac{2V\sin\alpha}{g}\sum_{n=0}^\infty e^n=\frac{2V\sin\alpha}{g(1-e)}$.
