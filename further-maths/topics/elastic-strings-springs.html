15.1 Hooke's Law

When an elastic string or spring is stretched (or a spring compressed) by an extension $x$ beyond its natural length $l$, the tension (or thrust) is proportional to the extension:

$$T = \frac{\lambda x}{l} \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad T = kx$$

Here $\lambda$ is the modulus of elasticity (in newtons), $l$ is the natural length, and $k = \lambda/l$ is the spring constant or stiffness (N m⁻¹). Both forms are used at A Level — know which your question uses.

Dimensions of $\lambda$ vs $k$ The modulus $\lambda$ has units of force (N), independent of length. The spring constant $k$ has units of N m⁻¹. If a question gives $\lambda$, use $T = \lambda x / l$; if it gives $k$, use $T = kx$. They describe the same physics: $k = \lambda/l$.
Worked Example 15.1a
An elastic string has natural length 1.5 m and modulus of elasticity 12 N. Find the tension when the string has length 2.1 m. Also find the extension needed to produce a tension of 6 N.
1
Extension: $x = 2.1 - 1.5 = 0.6$ m. Tension: $T = \dfrac{12 \times 0.6}{1.5} = \dfrac{7.2}{1.5} = 4.8$ N
2
For $T = 6$ N: $6 = \dfrac{12x}{1.5} = 8x$. So $x = 0.75$ m. Length of string $= 1.5 + 0.75 = 2.25$ m.

15.2 Elastic Potential Energy

The elastic potential energy (EPE) stored in a string or spring with extension $x$ is the work done in stretching it:

$$\text{EPE} = \int_0^x T\,dx' = \int_0^x \frac{\lambda x'}{l}\,dx' = \frac{\lambda x^2}{2l}$$ $$\text{or equivalently: EPE} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \qquad \text{(using }k = \lambda/l\text{)}$$

EPE is always non-negative and is stored as potential energy — it can do work when released. This is a conservative force, so energy methods apply.

Energy conservation with elastic forces Total mechanical energy includes KE, GPE, and EPE: $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh + \frac{\lambda x^2}{2l} = \text{constant}$ (no friction). Always check whether the string/spring is stretched or at natural length when writing EPE terms.

15.3 Strings vs Springs

The crucial difference: an elastic string can only pull (tension), never push. It goes slack (tension $= 0$) when at or below its natural length. A spring can both pull (when stretched) and push — thrust (compression) — when compressed.

⚠ Always check if a string is taut When working with elastic strings, you must check at each stage whether the string is taut (length $>$ natural length, $T > 0$) or slack ($T = 0$). The equation of motion changes as the string becomes slack — the elastic force disappears and the particle moves freely.
Two phases of motion for a string Phase 1 (string taut): elastic force acts, use $F = ma$ with Hooke's Law tension. Phase 2 (string slack): no elastic force, particle moves under remaining forces only (gravity, friction, etc.). The transition occurs when the string returns to natural length.

15.4 Vertical Motion with Elastic Forces

A particle hanging on a vertical elastic string or spring reaches equilibrium where tension balances weight:

$$T = mg \implies \frac{\lambda e}{l} = mg \implies e = \frac{mgl}{\lambda}$$

where $e$ is the equilibrium extension. This defines the equilibrium position.

Worked Example 15.4a — Finding equilibrium
A particle of mass 0.5 kg hangs in equilibrium on an elastic string of natural length 1.2 m and modulus 9.8 N. Find the equilibrium extension and the total length of the string.
1
At equilibrium: $T = mg$. $\dfrac{9.8 \times e}{1.2} = 0.5 \times 9.8$. So $e = \dfrac{0.5 \times 1.2}{1} = 0.6$ m.
2
Total length $= 1.2 + 0.6 = 1.8$ m.
Worked Example 15.4b — Particle released from rest
The particle from Example 15.4a is held at the natural length position (1.2 m below ceiling) and released. Find its speed when it has fallen a further 0.8 m.
1
After falling 0.8 m, extension $x = 0.8$ m (string stretched since $0.8 > 0$). EPE $= \dfrac{9.8 \times 0.64}{2 \times 1.2} = \dfrac{6.272}{2.4} = 2.613$ J
2
Energy conservation (taking initial position as datum): $\frac{1}{2}(0.5)v^2 = 0.5(9.8)(0.8) - 2.613 = 3.92 - 2.613 = 1.307$ J
3
$v^2 = \dfrac{2 \times 1.307}{0.5} = 5.228$. $v \approx 2.29$ m s⁻¹ downward.

15.5 SHM from Elastic Forces

A particle on a vertical spring (or taut string) displaced from equilibrium undergoes simple harmonic motion. Let $x$ be displacement from the equilibrium position (positive downward).

$$m\ddot{x} = mg - \frac{\lambda(e+x)}{l} = mg - \frac{\lambda e}{l} - \frac{\lambda x}{l} = -\frac{\lambda x}{l}$$ $$\therefore \ddot{x} = -\frac{\lambda}{ml}x = -\omega^2 x \qquad \text{where } \omega = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{ml}} = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$

This is SHM with centre at the equilibrium position, angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{\lambda/(ml)}$, period $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{ml/\lambda}$.

⚠ SHM only holds while the string is taut For an elastic string (not spring), SHM only applies while the string is stretched (particle below natural length position). If the amplitude is large enough that the particle rises above the natural length position, the string goes slack and motion becomes projectile-like. Split the motion into phases.
Worked Example 15.5a
A 0.4 kg particle hangs on a spring with $k = 40$ N m⁻¹. It is pulled 0.05 m below equilibrium and released. Find (a) the period, (b) the amplitude, (c) the maximum speed, (d) the maximum acceleration.
1
$\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = \sqrt{40/0.4} = \sqrt{100} = 10$ rad s⁻¹.
2
(a) Period $T = 2\pi/10 = \pi/5 \approx 0.628$ s. (b) Amplitude $a = 0.05$ m (released from rest at this displacement).
3
(c) Max speed $= \omega a = 10 \times 0.05 = 0.5$ m s⁻¹ (at equilibrium).
4
(d) Max acceleration $= \omega^2 a = 100 \times 0.05 = 5$ m s⁻² (at extremes).

15.6 Energy Methods

Energy conservation is often the most powerful tool for elastic problems — especially to find speeds at specific positions without solving the differential equation. Always include all three energy terms: KE + GPE + EPE.

Worked Example 15.6a — Maximum extension
A particle of mass 2 kg is attached to one end of an elastic string (natural length 1 m, modulus 20 N). The other end is fixed to a ceiling. The particle is held at the ceiling and released. Find the maximum extension of the string.
1
At the ceiling (start): KE $= 0$, GPE $= 0$ (datum), EPE $= 0$ (string slack). Total energy $= 0$.
2
At maximum extension $x$ (momentarily at rest): KE $= 0$. Distance fallen $= 1 + x$ (natural length + extension). GPE $= -2(9.8)(1+x)$. EPE $= \dfrac{20x^2}{2 \times 1} = 10x^2$.
3
Energy conservation: $0 = 0 - 2(9.8)(1+x) + 10x^2$. $10x^2 - 19.6x - 19.6 = 0$. $x^2 - 1.96x - 1.96 = 0$.
4
$x = \dfrac{1.96 + \sqrt{1.96^2 + 4(1.96)}}{2} = \dfrac{1.96 + \sqrt{11.6816}}{2} = \dfrac{1.96 + 3.418}{2} \approx 2.69$ m.
Take the positive root since extension must be positive.
Worked Example 15.6b — Speed at natural length
Using the same particle and string as above, find the speed of the particle when it passes through the natural length position on the way down.
1
At natural length position: fallen distance $= 1$ m, extension $= 0$, so EPE $= 0$.
2
Energy: $\frac{1}{2}(2)v^2 - 2(9.8)(1) + 0 = 0$. $v^2 = 2(9.8)(1) = 19.6$. $v = \sqrt{19.6} \approx 4.43$ m s⁻¹.

15.7 Two Strings or Springs

When a particle is connected to two elastic strings or springs (e.g. between two fixed points, or one above and one below), both forces must be included. Care is needed to determine which strings are taut.

Equilibrium with two strings Set up coordinates with the equilibrium position as origin. Let the particle be at distance $d_1$ below one fixed point and $d_2$ above the other. Write tension in each: $T_1 = \lambda_1 x_1 / l_1$ and $T_2 = \lambda_2 x_2 / l_2$. At equilibrium: $T_1 = T_2 + mg$ (or $T_1 + T_2 = mg$ depending on configuration). For SHM analysis, displace by $x$ from equilibrium and resolve forces.
Worked Example 15.7a
A particle of mass 1 kg is attached to two elastic strings. String 1: natural length 0.5 m, modulus 10 N, attached to ceiling. String 2: natural length 0.5 m, modulus 10 N, attached to floor 2 m below ceiling. Find the equilibrium position and show the particle performs SHM if displaced.
1
Let $d$ = distance of particle below ceiling at equilibrium. Both strings taut requires $0.5 < d < 1.5$ (particle between natural length positions).
2
Extension of string 1: $x_1 = d - 0.5$. Extension of string 2: $x_2 = (2 - d) - 0.5 = 1.5 - d$. Tensions: $T_1 = 10(d-0.5)/0.5 = 20(d-0.5)$, $T_2 = 20(1.5-d)$.
3
Equilibrium: $T_1 = mg + T_2$. $20(d-0.5) = 9.8 + 20(1.5-d)$. $20d - 10 = 9.8 + 30 - 20d$. $40d = 49.8$. $d = 1.245$ m. (Check: $0.5 < 1.245 < 1.5$ ✓)
4
Displace by $\xi$ down from equilibrium. Net upward force $= T_1' - T_2' - mg$. But $T_1' - T_2' - mg = 20(d+\xi-0.5) - 20(1.5-d-\xi) - 9.8 = 20\xi + 20\xi + [20(d-0.5)-20(1.5-d)-9.8]$. The bracket $= 0$ (equilibrium condition). So net force $= 40\xi$ downward? Recheck: $T_1$ increases by $20\xi$ (more stretched), $T_2$ decreases by $20\xi$ (less stretched). Net restoring force upward $= -20\xi - 20\xi + 2(20\xi) = -40\xi$... Net $= -40\xi$ (towards equilibrium). $\ddot{\xi} = -40\xi$. SHM with $\omega = \sqrt{40} = 2\sqrt{10}$ rad s⁻¹.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
An elastic string has natural length 2 m and modulus of elasticity 30 N. A particle of mass 3 kg is attached to one end; the other end is fixed to a ceiling. (a) Find the equilibrium extension. (b) Find the EPE stored at equilibrium. (c) Find the period of small oscillations about equilibrium.
(a) $\frac{30e}{2}=3(9.8)\Rightarrow15e=29.4\Rightarrow e=1.96$ m. (b) EPE $=\frac{30(1.96)^2}{2\times2}=\frac{30\times3.8416}{4}=28.81$ J. (c) $\omega=\sqrt{\lambda/(ml)}=\sqrt{30/(3\times2)}=\sqrt{5}$ rad s⁻¹. $T=2\pi/\sqrt{5}\approx2.81$ s.
[7 marks]
Q2
A particle of mass 0.5 kg is attached to a spring ($k = 50$ N m⁻¹, natural length 0.4 m) lying on a smooth horizontal surface, with the other end fixed. The particle is pulled 0.1 m beyond the natural length and released. Find (a) $\omega$ and the period, (b) the speed when the spring is at natural length, (c) the maximum compression of the spring.
(a) $\omega=\sqrt{50/0.5}=10$ rad s⁻¹. $T=2\pi/10=\pi/5\approx0.628$ s. (b) Energy: $\frac{1}{2}(0.5)v^2=\frac{1}{2}(50)(0.1)^2=0.25$ J. $v=\sqrt{1}=1$ m s⁻¹. (c) By symmetry (horizontal, smooth surface): max compression $=0.1$ m (same as initial extension). The motion is symmetric SHM about the natural length.
[6 marks]
Q3
An elastic string (natural length 1 m, $\lambda = 20$ N) has one end fixed to the ceiling. A 2 kg particle is attached to the other end and held at the ceiling, then released from rest. (a) Find the speed when the string becomes taut. (b) Find the maximum extension of the string. (c) Does the particle return to the ceiling? Justify your answer.
(a) String becomes taut after falling 1 m (natural length). Free fall: $v^2=2(9.8)(1)=19.6\Rightarrow v=\sqrt{19.6}\approx4.43$ m s⁻¹. (b) At max extension $x$, take ceiling as datum: $0=\frac{1}{2}(2)v_{\text{taut}}^2-2(9.8)(1+x)+\frac{20x^2}{2\times1}$. Using energy from start: $0=0-2(9.8)(1+x)+10x^2\Rightarrow10x^2-19.6x-19.6=0\Rightarrow x^2-1.96x-1.96=0$. $x=\frac{1.96+\sqrt{3.8416+7.84}}{2}=\frac{1.96+\sqrt{11.6816}}{2}=\frac{1.96+3.418}{2}\approx2.69$ m. (c) By energy conservation, the particle returns to its initial position (ceiling) since no energy is lost. So yes, it returns to the ceiling.

Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q4
A particle of mass $m$ is attached to the midpoint of an elastic string of natural length $2l$ and modulus $\lambda$. The ends of the string are fixed to two points $A$ and $B$ on a smooth horizontal table, distance $3l$ apart. (a) Show the particle is in equilibrium at the midpoint. (b) The particle is displaced a distance $d$ (where $d \ll l$) along $AB$ and released. Show it performs SHM and find $\omega$.
(a) At midpoint, each half of string has natural length $l$ and the particle is at distance $3l/2$ from each end. Extension of each half $= 3l/2 - l = l/2$. Tensions equal and opposite by symmetry $\Rightarrow$ equilibrium ✓. (b) Displace $x$ along $AB$ toward $A$. Length of $AP$ half $= 3l/2-x$, extension $= l/2-x$. Tension $T_A = \frac{\lambda(l/2-x)}{l}$. Length of $PB$ half $= 3l/2+x$, extension $= l/2+x$. Tension $T_B=\frac{\lambda(l/2+x)}{l}$. Net force toward $A$: $T_B-T_A=\frac{\lambda}{l}(l/2+x-l/2+x)=\frac{2\lambda x}{l}$ toward $B$ (restoring). So $m\ddot{x}=-\frac{2\lambda}{l}x$. SHM with $\omega=\sqrt{2\lambda/(ml)}$.
[8 marks]
Q5
A particle of mass 1 kg hangs on an elastic string (natural length 0.5 m, modulus 15 N). It is held at the natural length position and projected downward at 3 m s⁻¹. (a) Find the maximum extension. (b) Find whether the particle ever reaches the natural length position again on the way up — if so, find its speed there. (c) Find the amplitude and centre of the SHM phase.
Equilibrium: $\frac{15e}{0.5}=1(9.8)\Rightarrow e=\frac{9.8\times0.5}{15}=\frac{4.9}{15}=\frac{49}{150}\approx0.327$ m below natural length. Starting at natural length with speed 3 m s⁻¹ downward: particle is $e=0.327$ m above equilibrium, moving down. Amplitude of SHM: need to find energy. At natural length position, displacement from equil. $= e = 0.327$ m above, i.e. $x=-0.327$ (taking down as positive). $v=3$ m s⁻¹. For SHM: $v^2=\omega^2(a^2-x^2)$. $\omega=\sqrt{15/(1\times0.5)}=\sqrt{30}$. $9=30(a^2-0.327^2)=30a^2-3.208\Rightarrow30a^2=12.208\Rightarrow a^2=0.4069\Rightarrow a=0.638$ m. Max extension = $e+a=0.327+0.638=0.965$ m. (b) Returning through natural length position (amplitude $a=0.638>e=0.327$, so yes, particle does reach there). At $x=-0.327$ on way up: $v^2=30(0.4069-0.1069)=30\times0.300=9$, $v=3$ m s⁻¹ upward (same speed by energy conservation). (c) Amplitude $a=0.638$ m, centre at equilibrium ($0.327$ m below natural length).

Q6
A particle of mass $m$ hangs from a fixed point by an elastic string of natural length $l$ and modulus $\lambda$. The particle is held at the point of support and released from rest. (i) Show the string becomes taut at velocity $v_1 = \sqrt{2gl}$. (ii) Show the motion while the string is taut is SHM and find $\omega$. (iii) Let $e_0 = mgl/\lambda$ be the equilibrium extension. Show the amplitude of SHM is $a = e_0 + l$ (measuring from equilibrium). (iv) Show the particle rises above the support if and only if $\lambda < mg$, and interpret this physically.
(i) Free fall from support through distance $l$: $v_1^2=2gl\Rightarrow v_1=\sqrt{2gl}$ ✓. (ii) Let $x$ = downward displacement from equilibrium ($x=0$ at equilibrium, extension $e_0+x$ when $x>-e_0$). $m\ddot{x}=mg-\frac{\lambda(e_0+x)}{l}=mg-\frac{\lambda e_0}{l}-\frac{\lambda x}{l}=0-\frac{\lambda x}{l}$ (using $\lambda e_0/l=mg$). So $\ddot{x}=-\frac{\lambda}{ml}x$, SHM with $\omega=\sqrt{\lambda/(ml)}$ ✓. (iii) At natural length position, $x=-e_0$ (below equilibrium by $e_0$), $v=v_1=\sqrt{2gl}$. Using $v^2=\omega^2(a^2-x^2)$: $2gl=\frac{\lambda}{ml}(a^2-e_0^2)\Rightarrow a^2=e_0^2+\frac{2mgl^2}{\lambda}=e_0^2+2e_0l$ (since $e_0=mgl/\lambda\Rightarrow mgl^2/\lambda=e_0l$). So $a^2=(e_0+l)^2-l^2+... =e_0^2+2e_0l=(e_0+l)^2-l^2$. Actually: $a^2=e_0^2+2e_0l=(e_0+l)^2-l^2$... let me redo: $2gl=\omega^2(a^2-e_0^2)=\frac{\lambda}{ml}(a^2-e_0^2)$. $a^2-e_0^2=\frac{2mgl^2}{\lambda}=2e_0l$. $a^2=e_0^2+2e_0l=(e_0+l)^2-l^2$... hmm, that's $(e_0+l)^2-l^2\neq(e_0+l)^2$. Recheck: $a^2=e_0^2+2e_0l+l^2-l^2=(e_0+l)^2-l^2$. This doesn't simplify to $(e_0+l)^2$. The question statement may have the answer $a=e_0+l$ only if we take the highest point. Actually the correct amplitude is $a=\sqrt{e_0^2+2e_0l}=\sqrt{e_0(e_0+2l)}$. (iv) Particle rises above support if the highest point of SHM is above support, i.e. equilibrium $- a > l$ above equilibrium, meaning $a > e_0 + l$. From $a^2=e_0^2+2e_0l$: $a>e_0+l\Rightarrow e_0^2+2e_0l>(e_0+l)^2=e_0^2+2e_0l+l^2\Rightarrow0>l^2$ — impossible! So the particle never rises above the support during SHM phase. But the string goes slack when $x=-e_0$ (at natural length) on way up, and the particle then has speed $v_1=\sqrt{2gl}$ upward, rising $l$ under gravity — returning exactly to the support. So regardless of $\lambda$, the particle just reaches the support. The physical interpretation: energy is conserved, so it always returns to its starting height.

Q7
An elastic string of natural length $l$ and modulus $\lambda$ connects a particle of mass $m$ to a fixed peg at the origin. The particle rests on a smooth horizontal table. It is projected horizontally with speed $u$ perpendicular to the string when the string is taut with length $2l$. (i) Explain why the string length is not constant. (ii) Write down the conserved energy (taking EPE into account). (iii) Find the maximum and minimum distances of the particle from the peg during the subsequent motion, given the string remains taut throughout.
(i) The tension acts radially (along the string toward the peg), while the velocity is perpendicular. As the particle moves, the radial component of velocity changes — the string is free to stretch or compress, so the particle can move radially as well as tangentially. The string length changes because there is a radial component of motion (the tension does work as $r$ changes). (ii) By conservation of angular momentum (tension is central): $mr^2\dot{\theta}=mru_\theta=m(2l)u$ — but angular momentum alone isn't sufficient since the string length changes. Total energy: $E=\frac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2+r^2\dot{\theta}^2)+\frac{\lambda(r-l)^2}{2l}$. At start: $E=\frac{1}{2}mu^2+\frac{\lambda l^2}{2l}=\frac{1}{2}mu^2+\frac{\lambda l}{2}$. Angular momentum: $L=mr^2\dot{\theta}=m(2l)u$ (conserved since tension has no tangential component). (iii) At turning points $\dot{r}=0$: $E=\frac{1}{2}m\frac{L^2}{m^2r^2}+\frac{\lambda(r-l)^2}{2l}=\frac{2m l^2u^2}{r^2}+\frac{\lambda(r-l)^2}{2l}$. Setting equal to $E$ gives a quartic in $r$ which has turning points at $r=2l$ (initial) and another root — this is an open problem requiring numerical solution in general, but conceptually: $r_{\min}$ is where the elastic PE helps maintain a smaller orbit vs centrifugal effects, and $r_{\max}$ is limited by energy available.
