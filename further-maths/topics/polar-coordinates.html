Polar Coordinates

A completely different way to describe points and curves — using distance and angle rather than $x$ and $y$. Unlocks beautiful curves that are impossible to express neatly in Cartesian form.

5.1 The Polar Coordinate System

In the polar coordinate system, a point $P$ is described by $(r, \theta)$ where $r$ is the distance from the origin (the pole) and $\theta$ is the angle measured anticlockwise from the positive $x$-axis (the initial line).

$$P = (r, \theta) \quad r \geq 0,\quad \theta \in (-\pi, \pi]$$
Convention The principal value of $\theta$ is taken in $(-\pi, \pi]$. A polar curve is defined by an equation $r = f(\theta)$ — for each angle, it tells you how far from the pole the curve lies. When $r = 0$ the curve passes through the pole.

Unlike Cartesian coordinates, polar representations are not unique: $(r, \theta)$ and $(r, \theta + 2\pi)$ describe the same point. Some textbooks allow $r < 0$, meaning the point is reflected through the pole — but at A Level we generally keep $r \geq 0$.

5.2 Converting Coordinates

The conversion between polar and Cartesian coordinates follows directly from the definition of $\sin$ and $\cos$.

$$x = r\cos\theta \qquad y = r\sin\theta$$ $$r^2 = x^2 + y^2 \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}$$
⚠ Be careful with arctan Always sketch the point to determine which quadrant it lies in before computing $\theta = \arctan(y/x)$. The arctan function only returns values in $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$, so for points in the 2nd or 3rd quadrant you must add or subtract $\pi$.
Worked Example 5.2a
Convert (a) the Cartesian point $(-1, \sqrt{3})$ to polar form, and (b) the polar point $(4, 2\pi/3)$ to Cartesian form.
1
(a) $r = \sqrt{1+3} = 2$. Point is in 2nd quadrant. $\theta = \pi - \arctan(\sqrt{3}) = \pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3}$. Polar: $\left(2, \dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right)$.
2
(b) $x = 4\cos\dfrac{2\pi}{3} = 4\times(-\frac{1}{2}) = -2$. $y = 4\sin\dfrac{2\pi}{3} = 4\times\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 2\sqrt{3}$. Cartesian: $(-2, 2\sqrt{3})$.
Worked Example 5.2b — Converting Equations
Convert $r = 4\cos\theta$ to Cartesian form.
1
Multiply both sides by $r$: $r^2 = 4r\cos\theta$
2
Substitute $r^2 = x^2+y^2$ and $r\cos\theta = x$: $x^2+y^2 = 4x$
3
Complete the square: $(x-2)^2 + y^2 = 4$ — a circle, centre $(2,0)$, radius $2$.

5.3 Sketching Polar Curves

To sketch $r = f(\theta)$, build a table of values for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$ (or $[-\pi, \pi]$), plot the key points, and join them smoothly. Look for symmetry to halve the work.

Symmetry

Symmetry about the initial line (i.e. about $\theta = 0$): the curve has this if $f(-\theta) = f(\theta)$. This is the most common type.

Symmetry about $\theta = \pi/2$: if $f(\pi - \theta) = f(\theta)$.

Rotational symmetry: if $f(\theta + \alpha) = f(\theta)$ for some angle $\alpha$.

Exam Technique: Table of Values Always compute $r$ at $\theta = 0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2, 2\pi/3, \pi$ (and their negatives if needed). Mark where $r = 0$ — these are points where the curve passes through the pole. Mark where $r$ is maximum.
Worked Example 5.3a
Sketch the curve $r = 1 + \cos\theta$.
1
Since $\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$, the curve is symmetric about $\theta = 0$ (the initial line). Only need $\theta \in [0, \pi]$.
2
Key values:
$\theta=0$: $r=2$ (maximum, on initial line)
$\theta=\pi/3$: $r=\frac{3}{2}$
$\theta=\pi/2$: $r=1$
$\theta=2\pi/3$: $r=\frac{1}{2}$
$\theta=\pi$: $r=0$ (passes through pole)
3
The curve is a cardioid — heart-shaped. It starts at $(2,0)$, loops round to touch the pole at $\theta=\pi$, and reflects back symmetrically.
The name "cardioid" comes from the Greek for heart. It arises when a circle rolls around another circle of equal radius.

5.4 Common Polar Curves

You need to recognise and sketch these families instantly in the exam.

Circle: $r = a$

All points at distance $a$ from the pole — a circle centred at the origin, radius $a$.

Circle through pole: $r = 2a\cos\theta$ or $r = 2a\sin\theta$

A circle of radius $a$, centred at $(a, 0)$ or $(0, a)$ in Cartesian form. Passes through the pole at $\theta = \pi/2$ or $\theta = 0$ respectively.

Cardioid: $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$ or $r = a(1 - \cos\theta)$

Heart-shaped curve. Maximum $r = 2a$ on the initial line; passes through pole at $\theta = \pi$. The $1 - \cos\theta$ version is the mirror image.

Limaçon: $r = a + b\cos\theta$, $a \neq b$

When $a > b$: a dimpled or convex loop. When $a < b$: has an inner loop. The cardioid is the special case $a = b$.

Rose curves: $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ or $r = a\sin(n\theta)$

Has $n$ petals if $n$ is odd, $2n$ petals if $n$ is even. Maximum $r = a$.

Archimedean spiral: $r = a\theta$

$r$ increases linearly with $\theta$ — a spiral that winds outward uniformly.

$$r = a \quad\text{(circle)} \qquad r = a(1\pm\cos\theta) \quad\text{(cardioid)}$$ $$r = a + b\cos\theta \quad\text{(limaçon)} \qquad r = a\cos(n\theta) \quad\text{(rose, }n\text{ petals)}$$

5.5 Area Enclosed by a Polar Curve

The area swept out by the radius vector from $\theta = \alpha$ to $\theta = \beta$ is:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2\,d\theta$$

This comes from approximating the region as a sum of thin sectors of angle $d\theta$ and radius $r$, each with area $\frac{1}{2}r^2\,d\theta$.

⚠ Choose limits carefully Use symmetry to simplify. For a cardioid $r = a(1+\cos\theta)$, the full area is $2 \times \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi} r^2\,d\theta$. For a rose with $n$ petals, find the area of one petal (between the two $\theta$ values where $r=0$) and multiply by $n$.
Worked Example 5.5a
Find the total area enclosed by the cardioid $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$.
1
By symmetry about $\theta=0$: $A = 2\times\dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta = a^2\int_0^{\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta$
2
Expand: $(1+\cos\theta)^2 = 1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 + 2\cos\theta + \dfrac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2} = \dfrac{3}{2} + 2\cos\theta + \dfrac{\cos 2\theta}{2}$
3
$\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\left(\frac{3}{2}+2\cos\theta+\frac{\cos 2\theta}{2}\right)d\theta = \left[\frac{3\theta}{2}+2\sin\theta+\frac{\sin 2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{\pi} = \frac{3\pi}{2}$
4
$A = a^2 \times \dfrac{3\pi}{2} = \dfrac{3\pi a^2}{2}$
Worked Example 5.5b
Find the area of one petal of the rose curve $r = \cos 2\theta$.
1
$r = 0$ when $\cos 2\theta = 0$, i.e. $2\theta = \pm\pi/2$, so $\theta = \pm\pi/4$. One petal lies between $\theta = -\pi/4$ and $\theta = \pi/4$.
2
$A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2 2\theta\,d\theta = \displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}\cos^2 2\theta\,d\theta$ (by symmetry)
3
$= \displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}\dfrac{1+\cos 4\theta}{2}\,d\theta = \left[\dfrac{\theta}{2}+\dfrac{\sin 4\theta}{8}\right]_0^{\pi/4} = \dfrac{\pi}{8}+0 = \dfrac{\pi}{8}$

Area Between Two Curves

The area between $r = f(\theta)$ (outer) and $r = g(\theta)$ (inner) for $\alpha \leq \theta \leq \beta$:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta}\left[f(\theta)^2 - g(\theta)^2\right]d\theta$$

5.6 Tangents to Polar Curves

To find the gradient of a polar curve, convert to parametric form using $x = r\cos\theta$ and $y = r\sin\theta$, then use $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/d\theta}{dx/d\theta}$.

$$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta$$ $$\frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta}{\frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta}$$

Tangents parallel to the initial line occur when $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 0$.

Tangents perpendicular to the initial line occur when $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = 0$.

Worked Example 5.6a
Find the values of $\theta$ where $r = 1 + \cos\theta$ has tangents parallel to the initial line.
1
$\dfrac{dr}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta$. So $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta\cdot\sin\theta + (1+\cos\theta)\cos\theta$
2
$= -\sin^2\theta + \cos\theta + \cos^2\theta = \cos 2\theta + \cos\theta$
Used $\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \cos 2\theta$.
3
Set $= 0$: $\cos 2\theta + \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow 2\cos^2\theta - 1 + \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow (2\cos\theta - 1)(\cos\theta + 1) = 0$
4
$\cos\theta = \frac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \theta = \pm\frac{\pi}{3}$, or $\cos\theta = -1 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi$ (but $r=0$ here, so this is the pole — check separately).
Tangents parallel to initial line at $\theta = \pi/3$ and $\theta = -\pi/3$.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
Convert to polar coordinates: (a) $(\sqrt{3}, 1)$, (b) $(-2, 2)$, (c) $(0, -3)$. Convert to Cartesian: (d) $(5, \pi/6)$, (e) $(2, -3\pi/4)$.
(a) $r=2$, 1st quadrant, $\theta=\pi/6$. So $(2,\pi/6)$. (b) $r=2\sqrt{2}$, 2nd quadrant, $\theta=3\pi/4$. So $(2\sqrt{2},3\pi/4)$. (c) $r=3$, negative $y$-axis, $\theta=-\pi/2$. So $(3,-\pi/2)$. (d) $x=5\cos(\pi/6)=5\sqrt{3}/2$, $y=5/2$. (e) $x=2\cos(-3\pi/4)=-\sqrt{2}$, $y=2\sin(-3\pi/4)=-\sqrt{2}$.
[6 marks]
Q2
Convert to Cartesian form and identify the curve: (a) $r = 6\sin\theta$, (b) $r = \dfrac{3}{\cos\theta}$, (c) $r^2 = \cos 2\theta$ (just state what type of curve).
(a) Multiply by $r$: $r^2=6r\sin\theta \Rightarrow x^2+y^2=6y \Rightarrow x^2+(y-3)^2=9$. Circle centre $(0,3)$ radius $3$. (b) $r\cos\theta=3 \Rightarrow x=3$. Vertical line. (c) $r^2=\cos2\theta$ is a lemniscate of Bernoulli — a figure-of-eight curve.
[5 marks]
Q3
Sketch the curve $r = 2 + 2\sin\theta$, labelling the maximum $r$, where it crosses the initial line, and where it passes through the pole (if at all).
This is a cardioid $r=2(1+\sin\theta)$. Max $r=4$ at $\theta=\pi/2$ (top). On initial line ($\theta=0$ and $\pi$): $r=2$ both times. Passes through pole at $\theta=-\pi/2$ (or $3\pi/2$) where $\sin\theta=-1$. Symmetric about $\theta=\pi/2$ (the vertical axis). Shape is a heart pointing upward.
[4 marks]
Q4
Find the area enclosed by $r = 3\cos\theta$ for $-\pi/2 \leq \theta \leq \pi/2$. Verify your answer using the Cartesian form.
$A=\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}9\cos^2\theta\,d\theta=\frac{9}{2}\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\frac{1+\cos2\theta}{2}\,d\theta=\frac{9}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}=\frac{9\pi}{4}$. Cartesian form: $x^2+y^2=3x \Rightarrow (x-3/2)^2+y^2=9/4$. Circle radius $3/2$, area $=\pi(3/2)^2=9\pi/4$ ✓.
[5 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q5
Find the area of the region that lies inside $r = 3\cos\theta$ but outside $r = 1 + \cos\theta$.
Find intersection: $3\cos\theta=1+\cos\theta \Rightarrow 2\cos\theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=\pm\pi/3$. Area $=\frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/3}^{\pi/3}[(3\cos\theta)^2-(1+\cos\theta)^2]\,d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/3}[9\cos^2\theta-1-2\cos\theta-\cos^2\theta]\,d\theta = \int_0^{\pi/3}[8\cos^2\theta-2\cos\theta-1]\,d\theta$. $=\int_0^{\pi/3}[4(1+\cos2\theta)-2\cos\theta-1]\,d\theta=[3\theta+2\sin2\theta-2\sin\theta]_0^{\pi/3}=\pi+\sqrt{3}-\sqrt{3}=\pi$.
[8 marks]
Q6
For the limaçon $r = 1 + 2\cos\theta$: (a) find where $r = 0$, (b) sketch the curve showing the inner loop, (c) find the area of the inner loop only.
(a) $1+2\cos\theta=0 \Rightarrow \cos\theta=-1/2 \Rightarrow \theta=\pm2\pi/3$. (b) Inner loop traced for $2\pi/3\leq\theta\leq4\pi/3$ (where $r<0$ conventionally, but the loop is between $\theta=2\pi/3$ and $\theta=4\pi/3$). (c) $A_{inner}=\frac{1}{2}\int_{2\pi/3}^{4\pi/3}(1+2\cos\theta)^2\,d\theta=\frac{1}{2}\int_{2\pi/3}^{4\pi/3}(1+4\cos\theta+4\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta$. Expanding and integrating: $=\frac{1}{2}[\theta+4\sin\theta+\theta+\sin2\theta]_{2\pi/3}^{4\pi/3}=\pi-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}$. Inner loop area $=\pi-\frac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
[8 marks]
Q7
Find the equations of the tangents to $r = 2(1-\cos\theta)$ that are perpendicular to the initial line, and the Cartesian coordinates of the points of tangency.
$x=r\cos\theta=2(1-\cos\theta)\cos\theta=2\cos\theta-2\cos^2\theta$. $\frac{dx}{d\theta}=-2\sin\theta+4\sin\theta\cos\theta=2\sin\theta(2\cos\theta-1)=0$. So $\theta=0,\pi$ (pole) or $\cos\theta=1/2 \Rightarrow \theta=\pm\pi/3$. At $\theta=\pi/3$: $r=2(1-1/2)=1$. Point: $(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)=(1/2,\sqrt{3}/2)$. At $\theta=-\pi/3$: $(1/2,-\sqrt{3}/2)$. Tangents: $x=1/2$.
[7 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q8
The curve $C$ has polar equation $r^2 = a^2\cos 2\theta$ (a lemniscate of Bernoulli). (i) State the values of $\theta$ for which the curve is defined. (ii) Convert to Cartesian form. (iii) Show that the total area enclosed by $C$ is $a^2$. (iv) Find the angle $\theta$ at which the tangent to $C$ is parallel to the initial line, for $0 < \theta < \pi/4$.
(i) Need $\cos2\theta\geq0$, so $-\pi/4\leq\theta\leq\pi/4$ or $3\pi/4\leq|\theta|\leq\pi$ (giving two loops). (ii) $r^2=a^2(\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta)=a^2\frac{x^2-y^2}{r^2}$, so $r^4=a^2(x^2-y^2)$, i.e. $(x^2+y^2)^2=a^2(x^2-y^2)$. (iii) Total area $=4\times\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/4}a^2\cos2\theta\,d\theta=2a^2[\frac{\sin2\theta}{2}]_0^{\pi/4}=2a^2\cdot\frac{1}{2}=a^2$ ✓. (iv) $y=r\sin\theta$; $\frac{dy}{d\theta}=0$. With $r^2=a^2\cos2\theta$, differentiate $r$ and apply the tangent formula. Setting $dy/d\theta=0$ leads to $\tan^2\theta=1/2$, so $\theta=\arctan(1/\sqrt{2})$.
[10 marks]
Q9
Two curves have polar equations $r = 2\cos\theta$ and $r = 2\sin\theta$. (i) Show that they intersect at the pole and at the point with Cartesian coordinates $(1,1)$. (ii) Find the total area of the region enclosed by both curves (i.e. inside both simultaneously). (iii) Verify your answer by computing it a different way using Cartesian integration.
(i) Equal when $2\cos\theta=2\sin\theta \Rightarrow \theta=\pi/4$: $r=\sqrt{2}$, giving $(\sqrt{2}\cos(\pi/4),\sqrt{2}\sin(\pi/4))=(1,1)$ ✓. At pole: $r=2\cos\theta=0$ when $\theta=\pi/2$; $r=2\sin\theta=0$ when $\theta=0$, so both pass through $(0,0)$ ✓. (ii) By symmetry about $y=x$, area inside both $=2\times\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/4}(2\sin\theta)^2\,d\theta=\int_0^{\pi/4}4\sin^2\theta\,d\theta=[2\theta-\sin2\theta]_0^{\pi/4}=\pi/2-1$. (iii) Both curves in Cartesian: $(x-1)^2+y^2=1$ and $x^2+(y-1)^2=1$. Area of intersection of two equal circles of radius 1 with centres distance $\sqrt{2}$ apart $=2\cos^{-1}(\frac{d}{2r})\cdot r^2 - \frac{d}{2}\sqrt{4r^2-d^2}=2\cos^{-1}(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})-\sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{2}=\pi/2-1$ ✓ (where $d=\sqrt{2}$, $r=1$).
[10 marks]
