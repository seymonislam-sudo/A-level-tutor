14.1 Work Done by a Force

The work done by a constant force $\mathbf{F}$ as a particle moves through displacement $\mathbf{s}$ is:

$$W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{s} = Fs\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the force and the displacement. Work is a scalar measured in joules (J = N m).

When $\theta = 0°$ (force and motion in same direction): $W = Fs$ (maximum positive work). When $\theta = 90°$ (force perpendicular to motion): $W = 0$ (e.g. normal reaction on a horizontal surface). When $\theta = 180°$ (force opposing motion): $W = -Fs$ (negative work, e.g. friction).

Friction always does negative work Friction always opposes motion, so it always does negative work on the moving object, removing kinetic energy from the system. This is why friction is a non-conservative force — the work done depends on the path taken.

14.2 Work-Energy Theorem

The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on a particle equals its change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta\text{KE} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$$

This follows directly from Newton's second law: $F = ma = m\dfrac{dv}{dt}$, and $W = \displaystyle\int F\,ds = \int m\frac{dv}{dt}\,ds = \int mv\,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{1}{2}mu^2$.

Worked Example 14.2a
A 5 kg block is pulled 10 m along a rough horizontal surface by a force of 30 N at 20° to the horizontal. The coefficient of friction is 0.3. Find the final speed if it starts from rest.
1
Work done by applied force: $W_F = 30\cos20° \times 10 = 281.9$ J
2
Normal reaction: $R = mg - 30\sin20° = 5(9.8) - 10.26 = 38.74$ N. Friction: $F_r = 0.3 \times 38.74 = 11.62$ N. Work done by friction: $W_f = -11.62 \times 10 = -116.2$ J
3
Net work $= 281.9 - 116.2 = 165.7$ J $= \frac{1}{2}(5)v^2$. So $v = \sqrt{2 \times 165.7/5} = \sqrt{66.28} \approx 8.14$ m s⁻¹

14.3 Potential Energy & Conservation of Energy

$$\text{GPE} = mgh \qquad \text{(gravitational potential energy)}$$ $$\text{KE} + \text{PE} = \text{constant} \quad \text{(when no non-conservative forces act)}$$

The principle of conservation of mechanical energy states that in the absence of friction and other dissipative forces, the total mechanical energy (KE + PE) is constant.

When friction or other non-conservative forces act, the energy equation becomes:

$$\text{KE}_1 + \text{PE}_1 + W_{\text{driving}} = \text{KE}_2 + \text{PE}_2 + W_{\text{resistance}}$$

Or equivalently: Work done by all forces = change in KE (work-energy theorem applied to all forces).

Worked Example 14.3a
A skier of mass 70 kg descends a 200 m slope inclined at 30°. The resistance to motion is 80 N. Find the speed at the bottom if the skier starts from rest.
1
Height dropped: $h = 200\sin30° = 100$ m. Loss in GPE $= 70 \times 9.8 \times 100 = 68600$ J
2
Work done against resistance $= 80 \times 200 = 16000$ J
3
Energy equation: $68600 - 16000 = \frac{1}{2}(70)v^2$. $v^2 = \dfrac{52600}{35} = 1502.9$. $v \approx 38.8$ m s⁻¹

14.4 Power

Power is the rate of doing work, measured in watts (W = J s⁻¹).

$$P = \frac{dW}{dt} = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{v} = Fv\cos\theta$$

For a vehicle moving at velocity $v$ with driving force $F$ in the direction of motion: $P = Fv$.

Maximum speed (terminal velocity) At maximum speed, acceleration $= 0$, so driving force $=$ resistance. With constant power $P$: driving force $= P/v_{\max}$. Setting this equal to resistance $R$: $v_{\max} = P/R$.
Worked Example 14.4a
A car of mass 1200 kg has engine power 40 kW. Resistance to motion is $600 + 2v^2$ N where $v$ is speed in m s⁻¹. Find (a) the maximum speed on a level road, (b) the acceleration at 20 m s⁻¹.
1
(a) At max speed: driving force $=$ resistance. $\frac{40000}{v_{\max}} = 600 + 2v_{\max}^2$. So $40000 = 600v_{\max} + 2v_{\max}^3$. Try $v_{\max} = 20$: $40000 = 12000 + 16000 = 28000$ ✗. Try $v = 25$: $40000 = 15000 + 31250 = 46250$ ✗. Try $v = 22$: $40000 = 13200 + 21296 \approx 34496$ — bracket between 22 and 25. Numerically: $v_{\max} \approx 23.1$ m s⁻¹.
2
(b) At $v = 20$ m s⁻¹: driving force $= 40000/20 = 2000$ N. Resistance $= 600 + 800 = 1400$ N. Net force $= 600$ N. $a = 600/1200 = 0.5$ m s⁻².

14.5 Work Done by a Variable Force

When force varies with position, work done is found by integration:

$$W = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F(x)\,dx$$

This is the area under the force-displacement graph. For a spring obeying Hooke's Law ($F = kx$ where $x$ is extension/compression from natural length):

$$W_{\text{spring}} = \int_0^x kx'\,dx' = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \qquad \text{(Elastic PE stored } = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\text{)}$$
Worked Example 14.5a
A force $F = (3x^2 + 2x)$ N acts on a particle moving from $x = 1$ m to $x = 4$ m. Find the work done.
1
$W = \displaystyle\int_1^4 (3x^2 + 2x)\,dx = \left[x^3 + x^2\right]_1^4 = (64 + 16) - (1 + 1) = 80 - 2 = 78$ J
Worked Example 14.5b — Hooke's Law
A spring has natural length 0.5 m and stiffness $k = 200$ N m⁻¹. Find the work done in stretching it from its natural length to 0.8 m, and then from 0.8 m to 1.1 m.
1
Stretching to 0.8 m: extension $x = 0.3$ m. $W_1 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.3)^2 = 9$ J.
2
From 0.8 m to 1.1 m: extensions 0.3 m to 0.6 m. $W_2 = \int_{0.3}^{0.6} 200x\,dx = [100x^2]_{0.3}^{0.6} = 36 - 9 = 27$ J.
3
It takes more work to stretch from 0.6–0.9 than 0–0.3 m — the force increases with extension.
Alternatively: $W_2 = \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.6)^2 - \frac{1}{2}(200)(0.3)^2 = 36 - 9 = 27$ J ✓

14.6 Conservative Forces & Potential Energy Functions

A force is conservative if the work it does is independent of the path taken — it depends only on the start and end points. Gravity and spring forces are conservative; friction is not.

For a conservative force in 1D, there exists a potential energy function $V(x)$ such that:

$$F(x) = -\frac{dV}{dx} \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad V(x) = -\int F(x)\,dx$$

The total mechanical energy $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + V(x)$ is conserved when only conservative forces act.

Equilibrium positions

Equilibrium occurs where $F = 0$, i.e. where $\dfrac{dV}{dx} = 0$ (stationary points of $V$). The type of equilibrium is determined by the second derivative:

$$\frac{d^2V}{dx^2} > 0 \implies \text{stable equilibrium (potential energy minimum)}$$ $$\frac{d^2V}{dx^2} < 0 \implies \text{unstable equilibrium (potential energy maximum)}$$
Worked Example 14.6a
A particle moves along the $x$-axis under a force $F(x) = 6x - x^3$ N. Find the equilibrium positions and classify them.
1
Equilibrium: $F = 0 \Rightarrow 6x - x^3 = 0 \Rightarrow x(6 - x^2) = 0$. So $x = 0$, $x = \pm\sqrt{6}$.
2
$V(x) = -\int (6x - x^3)\,dx = -3x^2 + \frac{x^4}{4} + C$. $\frac{d^2V}{dx^2} = -6 + 3x^2$.
3
At $x=0$: $V'' = -6 < 0$ → unstable. At $x = \pm\sqrt{6}$: $V'' = -6 + 18 = 12 > 0$ → stable.

14.7 Equations of Motion from Energy

Energy conservation is often the most efficient route to finding velocity as a function of position — especially when forces are variable or when you don't need acceleration explicitly.

The key technique: write $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 + V(x) = E$ (constant), then differentiate with respect to time to get the equation of motion:

$$mv\frac{dv}{dx}\cdot\dot{x} + \frac{dV}{dx}\dot{x} = 0 \implies m\dot{x}\ddot{x} = -\frac{dV}{dx}\dot{x}$$ $$\implies m\ddot{x} = -\frac{dV}{dx} = F(x)$$

This is Newton's second law — confirming the energy approach is consistent.

Worked Example 14.7a — SHM from energy
A particle of mass $m$ is attached to a spring of stiffness $k$ (natural length $l$), lying on a smooth surface. The spring is compressed by $a$ and released. Use energy to find $v$ as a function of displacement $x$ from equilibrium, and hence show the motion is SHM.
1
At displacement $x$ from equilibrium, elastic PE $= \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. Initial energy (spring compressed by $a$, at rest): $E = \frac{1}{2}ka^2$.
2
Conservation: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}ka^2$. So $v^2 = \dfrac{k}{m}(a^2 - x^2)$.
3
Differentiate $v^2 = \frac{k}{m}(a^2-x^2)$ w.r.t. $t$: $2v\dot{v} = -\frac{2k}{m}x\dot{x}$. Since $v = \dot{x}$: $\ddot{x} = -\dfrac{k}{m}x$.
This is $\ddot{x} = -\omega^2 x$ with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ — simple harmonic motion with amplitude $a$. ✓
Worked Example 14.7b — Vehicle on incline
A car of mass 1000 kg and engine power 50 kW moves up an incline of $\sin\theta = \frac{1}{20}$. Resistance is 500 N. Write the equation of motion and find the terminal velocity on the slope.
1
Driving force at speed $v$: $F_D = P/v = 50000/v$ N. Equation of motion (up the slope positive): $1000\ddot{x} = \dfrac{50000}{v} - 500 - 1000g\cdot\frac{1}{20} = \dfrac{50000}{v} - 990$.
2
Terminal velocity: $\ddot{x} = 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{50000}{v_T} = 990 \Rightarrow v_T = \dfrac{50000}{990} \approx 50.5$ m s⁻¹.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
A particle of mass 3 kg slides down a rough incline of length 8 m inclined at 25° to the horizontal. The coefficient of friction is 0.2. Using the work-energy theorem, find the speed at the bottom if it starts from rest.
Height $=8\sin25°=3.381$ m. GPE lost $=3(9.8)(3.381)=99.40$ J. Normal reaction $=3(9.8)\cos25°=26.63$ N. Friction $=0.2\times26.63=5.326$ N. Work by friction $=-5.326\times8=-42.61$ J. Net work $=99.40-42.61=56.79$ J $=\frac{1}{2}(3)v^2$. $v=\sqrt{37.86}\approx6.15$ m s⁻¹.
[6 marks]
Q2
A car of mass 900 kg has engine power 36 kW. Resistance to motion is constant at 800 N. Find (a) the maximum speed on a level road, (b) the maximum speed up an incline where $\sin\theta = \frac{1}{15}$, (c) the acceleration at 20 m s⁻¹ on the level.
(a) At max speed: $\frac{36000}{v}=800\Rightarrow v=45$ m s⁻¹. (b) On incline: $\frac{36000}{v}=800+900(9.8)\frac{1}{15}=800+588=1388\Rightarrow v=\frac{36000}{1388}\approx25.9$ m s⁻¹. (c) Driving force $=36000/20=1800$ N. Net force $=1800-800=1000$ N. $a=1000/900=1.11$ m s⁻².
[7 marks]
Q3
A force $F(x) = \dfrac{4}{x^2}$ N acts on a particle moving from $x = 1$ m to $x = 4$ m. Find (a) the work done, (b) the speed at $x = 4$ m if the mass is 2 kg and the particle starts from rest at $x = 1$ m.
(a) $W=\int_1^4\frac{4}{x^2}dx=\left[-\frac{4}{x}\right]_1^4=-1+4=3$ J. (b) $W=\frac{1}{2}mv^2\Rightarrow3=\frac{1}{2}(2)v^2\Rightarrow v=\sqrt{3}\approx1.73$ m s⁻¹.
[5 marks]
Q4
A particle moves in a potential field $V(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x$. Find the equilibrium positions, classify them, and find the force on the particle at $x = 2$.
$F=-\frac{dV}{dx}=-(3x^2-12x+9)=-3(x^2-4x+3)=-3(x-1)(x-3)$. Equilibrium: $x=1$ and $x=3$. $V''=6x-12$. At $x=1$: $V''=-6<0\Rightarrow$ unstable. At $x=3$: $V''=6>0\Rightarrow$ stable. At $x=2$: $F=-3(4-8+3)=-3(-1)=3$ N (in positive $x$ direction).
[6 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q5
A particle of mass 2 kg moves along the $x$-axis under a force $F = 8 - 2x$ N (with $x$ in metres). (a) Find the potential energy function $V(x)$. (b) If the particle starts from rest at $x = 0$, find its speed at $x = 3$ m. (c) Find the range of $x$ for which the particle can reach, and the turning points.
(a) $V(x)=-\int(8-2x)dx=-8x+x^2+C$. Take $C=0$: $V(x)=x^2-8x$. (b) Total energy at $x=0$: $E=0+V(0)=0$. At $x=3$: $\frac{1}{2}(2)v^2+(9-24)=0\Rightarrow v^2=15\Rightarrow v=\sqrt{15}\approx3.87$ m s⁻¹. (c) Turning points where $v=0$: $V(x)=0\Rightarrow x^2-8x=0\Rightarrow x(x-8)=0$. So turning points at $x=0$ (start) and $x=8$ m. The particle oscillates between $x=0$ and $x=8$.
[8 marks]
Q6
A lorry of mass 5000 kg travels along a level road with resistance $R = 1000 + 50v$ N where $v$ is in m s⁻¹. The engine works at constant power $P$. (a) Write the equation of motion. (b) If the maximum speed is 30 m s⁻¹, find $P$. (c) Find the acceleration when $v = 10$ m s⁻¹.
(a) $5000\ddot{x}=\frac{P}{v}-(1000+50v)$. (b) At max speed $a=0$: $\frac{P}{30}=1000+1500=2500\Rightarrow P=75000$ W $=75$ kW. (c) At $v=10$: driving force $=75000/10=7500$ N. Resistance $=1000+500=1500$ N. Net $=6000$ N. $a=6000/5000=1.2$ m s⁻².
[7 marks]
Q7
A bead of mass $m$ slides on a smooth wire in the shape of a curve $y = f(x)$. Using energy conservation, show that its speed $v$ at height $y$ satisfies $v^2 = v_0^2 - 2gy$ where $v_0$ is the speed at $y=0$. A bead starts from rest at height $H$ on a smooth loop-the-loop of radius $r$. Find the minimum $H$ for the bead to maintain contact at the top of the loop.
Energy conservation (no friction, smooth wire): $\frac{1}{2}mv^2+mgy=\frac{1}{2}mv_0^2+0\Rightarrow v^2=v_0^2-2gy$. Starting from rest at height $H$: $v_0=0$, so $v^2=2g(H-y)$. At the top of the loop, height $y=2r$: $v_{\text{top}}^2=2g(H-2r)$. At the top, for circular motion (minimum contact means normal reaction $N=0$): centripetal force provided by gravity only: $\frac{mv_{\text{top}}^2}{r}=mg\Rightarrow v_{\text{top}}^2=gr$. So $2g(H-2r)\geq gr\Rightarrow H-2r\geq\frac{r}{2}\Rightarrow H\geq\frac{5r}{2}$. Minimum $H=\frac{5r}{2}$.
[8 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q8
A particle of mass $m$ moves in one dimension under a force $F(x) = -kx + \dfrac{c}{x^3}$ where $k, c > 0$. (i) Find the potential energy function $V(x)$ for $x > 0$. (ii) Find the equilibrium position $x_0$ and show it is stable. (iii) For small displacements $x = x_0 + \xi$ where $|\xi| \ll x_0$, show the motion is approximately SHM and find the angular frequency.
(i) $V(x)=-\int(-kx+cx^{-3})dx=\frac{kx^2}{2}+\frac{c}{2x^2}+C$. (ii) Equilibrium: $F=0\Rightarrow -kx_0+c/x_0^3=0\Rightarrow x_0^4=c/k\Rightarrow x_0=(c/k)^{1/4}$. $V''(x)=k+3c/x^4$. At $x_0$: $V''=k+3k=4k>0\Rightarrow$ stable ✓. (iii) $F(x_0+\xi)\approx F(x_0)+\xi F'(x_0)=0+\xi(-k-3c/x_0^4)=\xi(-k-3k)=-4k\xi$. Equation of motion: $m\ddot{\xi}=-4k\xi$, which is SHM with $\omega^2=4k/m$, so $\omega=2\sqrt{k/m}$.
[10 marks]
Q9
A car of mass $M$ moves along a level road with driving force $F$ and resistance $kv^2$ (where $v$ is speed). (i) Write the equation of motion and show that if $F$ is constant, the terminal velocity is $v_T = \sqrt{F/k}$. (ii) Show that the speed satisfies $v = v_T\tanh\!\left(\dfrac{v_T k t}{M}\right)$ when starting from rest. (iii) Find the distance travelled in time $t$ and the limiting distance as $F \to \infty$ with $v_T$ fixed.
(i) $M\dot{v}=F-kv^2=k(v_T^2-v^2)$. At terminal: $\dot{v}=0\Rightarrow v=v_T=\sqrt{F/k}$ ✓. (ii) Separate: $\frac{dv}{v_T^2-v^2}=\frac{k}{M}dt$. Integrate: $\frac{1}{v_T}\tanh^{-1}(v/v_T)=\frac{kt}{M}\Rightarrow v=v_T\tanh(kv_Tt/M)$ ✓. (iii) $x=\int_0^tv_T\tanh(kv_Ts/M)ds=\frac{M}{k}\ln\cosh(kv_Tt/M)$. As $F\to\infty$ with $v_T$ fixed means $k\to\infty$, $M/k\to0$... actually as $t\to\infty$: $x\approx v_Tt-\frac{M}{k}\ln2\to\infty$, so distance is unbounded. For fixed $t$ and $F\to\infty$: $kv_T=\sqrt{kF}\to\infty$, so $\tanh\to1$ quickly and $v\to v_T$ rapidly, distance $\to v_Tt$.
[10 marks]
