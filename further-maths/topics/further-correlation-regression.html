12.1 Recap: PMCC & Regression

The product moment correlation coefficient (PMCC) $r$ measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables. It always lies in $[-1, 1]$.

$$r = \frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx} S_{yy}}} \qquad \text{where}$$ $$S_{xx} = \sum x^2 - \frac{(\sum x)^2}{n}, \quad S_{yy} = \sum y^2 - \frac{(\sum y)^2}{n}, \quad S_{xy} = \sum xy - \frac{(\sum x)(\sum y)}{n}$$

The least-squares regression line of $y$ on $x$ is $\hat{y} = a + bx$ where:

$$b = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$$
Regression of y on x vs x on y The regression line of $y$ on $x$ minimises the sum of squared vertical residuals — use it to predict $y$ from $x$. The regression line of $x$ on $y$ minimises horizontal residuals — use it to predict $x$ from $y$. These two lines are different unless $r = \pm 1$.

12.2 Spearman's Rank Correlation

When data cannot be measured precisely (e.g. judges' rankings), or when the relationship may be monotone but not linear, use Spearman's rank correlation coefficient $r_s$.

$$r_s = 1 - \frac{6\sum d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)}$$

where $d_i$ = difference in ranks for the $i$th data point, and $n$ = number of pairs.

Tied ranks When two observations share a rank, assign each the mean of the positions they would occupy. For example, if two values both rank 3rd and 4th, assign each a rank of 3.5. If there are many ties, Spearman's formula becomes less reliable and PMCC on the ranked data is preferred.
Worked Example 12.2a
Eight athletes are ranked by two judges. Calculate Spearman's rank correlation coefficient.
AthleteABCDEFGH
Judge 131528467
Judge 242317586
1
Calculate $d_i$ = (Judge 1 rank) $-$ (Judge 2 rank) for each athlete: $-1, -1, 2, 1, 1, -1, -2, 1$
2
$d_i^2$: $1, 1, 4, 1, 1, 1, 4, 1$. So $\sum d_i^2 = 14$.
3
$r_s = 1 - \dfrac{6 \times 14}{8(64-1)} = 1 - \dfrac{84}{504} = 1 - \dfrac{1}{6} = \dfrac{5}{6} \approx 0.833$
4
Strong positive agreement between the two judges.
$r_s$ close to 1 indicates the judges are ranking athletes similarly.

12.3 Hypothesis Tests for Correlation

Both $r$ (PMCC) and $r_s$ (Spearman's) can be used to test whether a population correlation is zero.

$$H_0: \rho = 0 \text{ (no linear correlation)} \quad H_1: \rho \neq 0 \text{ (or } > 0 \text{ or } < 0 \text{)}$$

Under $H_0$ (bivariate normal population), the test statistic is:

$$t = \frac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}} \sim t(n-2) \text{ under } H_0$$

In practice at A Level, critical values for $r$ and $r_s$ are looked up directly from tables for a given $n$ and significance level. Compare the calculated $r$ (or $r_s$) to the critical value.

Using critical value tables The table gives the minimum $|r|$ needed to reject $H_0$ at a given level for $n$ data points. If $|r| >$ critical value, reject $H_0$. The same table format is used for both PMCC and Spearman's tests — always check which table you're reading.
Worked Example 12.3a
A sample of $n = 10$ pairs gives PMCC $r = 0.65$. Test at the 5% level whether there is evidence of positive linear correlation in the population.
1
$H_0$: $\rho = 0$ (no linear correlation). $H_1$: $\rho > 0$ (positive linear correlation). One-tailed test at 5%.
2
From tables: the critical value for PMCC with $n = 10$ at the 5% level (one-tailed) is $0.5494$.
3
Since $r = 0.65 > 0.5494$, reject $H_0$. There is significant evidence at the 5% level of positive linear correlation.
Worked Example 12.3b — Spearman's test
Using the data from Example 12.2a ($n=8$, $r_s = 5/6 \approx 0.833$), test at the 1% level (two-tailed) whether there is evidence of correlation in the population rankings.
1
$H_0$: $\rho_s = 0$. $H_1$: $\rho_s \neq 0$. Two-tailed at 1%.
2
Critical value for Spearman's with $n = 8$ at 1% (two-tailed): $0.8810$.
3
Since $|r_s| = 0.833 < 0.8810$, do not reject $H_0$. Insufficient evidence at the 1% level of correlation between judges' rankings.
Note: at the 5% level (critical value 0.7381), we would reject $H_0$. The conclusion depends critically on the chosen significance level.

12.4 The Linear Regression Model

The formal statistical model for simple linear regression is:

$$Y_i = \alpha + \beta x_i + \varepsilon_i \qquad \varepsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \text{ independently}$$

Here $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are the true (unknown) population parameters, and $\varepsilon_i$ are random errors. The least-squares estimators $a$ and $b$ are unbiased estimates of $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

Properties of the estimators

$$E(b) = \beta \qquad \text{Var}(b) = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}$$ $$E(a) = \alpha \qquad \text{Var}(a) = \sigma^2\left(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{\bar{x}^2}{S_{xx}}\right)$$

Since $\sigma^2$ is unknown, it is estimated by the residual mean square:

$$\hat{\sigma}^2 = s^2 = \frac{S_{yy} - b \cdot S_{xy}}{n - 2} = \frac{\text{RSS}}{n-2}$$

where RSS is the residual sum of squares and $n - 2$ is the degrees of freedom (we estimated 2 parameters: $\alpha$ and $\beta$).

Worked Example 12.4a
For a dataset with $n=8$, $S_{xx} = 40$, $S_{xy} = 60$, $S_{yy} = 110$, $\bar{x} = 3$, $\bar{y} = 7$. Find the regression line, estimate $\sigma^2$, and find $\text{Var}(b)$.
1
$b = \dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = \dfrac{60}{40} = 1.5$. $a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x} = 7 - 1.5(3) = 2.5$. Line: $\hat{y} = 2.5 + 1.5x$.
2
RSS $= S_{yy} - b \cdot S_{xy} = 110 - 1.5(60) = 110 - 90 = 20$.
3
$\hat{\sigma}^2 = \dfrac{20}{8-2} = \dfrac{20}{6} = 3.\overline{3}$.
4
$\widehat{\text{Var}}(b) = \dfrac{\hat{\sigma}^2}{S_{xx}} = \dfrac{10/3}{40} = \dfrac{1}{12}$.

12.5 Residuals & Goodness of Fit

The residual for observation $i$ is $e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i = y_i - (a + bx_i)$. Residuals tell us how well the model fits each data point.

$$\text{RSS} = \sum e_i^2 = S_{yy} - b \cdot S_{xy}$$ $$r^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{RSS}}{S_{yy}} = \frac{S_{xy}^2}{S_{xx} S_{yy}} \quad \text{(coefficient of determination)}$$

$r^2$ (or $R^2$) gives the proportion of variation in $y$ explained by the regression on $x$. It ranges from 0 (no fit) to 1 (perfect fit).

Residual plots

Plot residuals $e_i$ against fitted values $\hat{y}_i$ (or against $x_i$). A good model shows residuals scattered randomly around zero with no pattern. Patterns in residuals suggest:

A funnel shape (increasing spread) → non-constant variance (heteroscedasticity). A curved pattern → the linear model is inappropriate; try a transformation. Systematic runs above/below → a trend not captured by the model.

Properties of residuals Always: $\sum e_i = 0$ (residuals sum to zero) and $\sum x_i e_i = 0$ (residuals are uncorrelated with $x$). These follow directly from the least-squares normal equations and can be used to check calculations.

12.6 Transforming Non-linear Data

When a scatter plot suggests a non-linear relationship, a suitable transformation can linearise the data, allowing standard regression techniques to be applied.

ModelEquationTransformationLinearised form
Exponential$y = ae^{bx}$$Y = \ln y$$Y = \ln a + bx$
Power$y = ax^b$$X = \ln x$, $Y = \ln y$$Y = \ln a + bX$
Reciprocal$y = a + b/x$$X = 1/x$$y = a + bX$
Quadratic$y = a + bx^2$$X = x^2$$y = a + bX$
Worked Example 12.6a
A researcher believes a population $P$ grows exponentially with time $t$: $P = ae^{bt}$. Data gives $\ln P$ vs $t$ as a straight line with gradient $0.12$ and intercept $3.4$. Find $a$, $b$, and predict $P$ at $t = 20$.
1
The linearised model is $\ln P = \ln a + bt$. Comparing: $b = 0.12$ and $\ln a = 3.4$.
2
$a = e^{3.4} \approx 29.96$. So $P \approx 29.96\,e^{0.12t}$.
3
At $t = 20$: $P = 29.96 \times e^{2.4} \approx 29.96 \times 11.02 \approx 330$.
Always check whether prediction is interpolation (within data range) or extrapolation (outside) — extrapolation is unreliable.
Worked Example 12.6b
Data suggests $y = ax^b$. After taking logs, $\ln y = \ln a + b \ln x$. From the coded data: $\sum \ln x = 12$, $\sum \ln y = 18$, $\sum (\ln x)^2 = 32$, $\sum (\ln x)(\ln y) = 28$, $n = 6$. Find $a$ and $b$.
1
Let $X = \ln x$, $Y = \ln y$. $\bar{X} = 2$, $\bar{Y} = 3$.
2
$S_{XX} = 32 - \dfrac{144}{6} = 32 - 24 = 8$. $S_{XY} = 28 - \dfrac{12 \times 18}{6} = 28 - 36 = -8$.
3
$b = \dfrac{S_{XY}}{S_{XX}} = \dfrac{-8}{8} = -1$. $\ln a = \bar{Y} - b\bar{X} = 3 - (-1)(2) = 5$. So $a = e^5 \approx 148.4$.
4
Model: $y = 148.4\, x^{-1} = \dfrac{148.4}{x}$ — an inverse relationship.

12.7 Multiple Regression (Introduction)

When more than one explanatory variable affects $Y$, we use multiple linear regression:

$$Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_p x_p + \varepsilon$$

The least-squares estimates minimise $\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$. In matrix form: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}$, but at A Level you will only be asked to interpret output from a computer or given values.

Interpreting multiple regression output

Each coefficient $\hat{\beta}_j$ estimates the change in $Y$ per unit increase in $x_j$, holding all other variables constant. This is the key difference from simple regression — we can separate the effects of correlated predictors.

⚠ Multicollinearity If two predictor variables are highly correlated with each other, the regression coefficients become unreliable — small changes in data can cause large changes in estimated coefficients. This is called multicollinearity. Signs include large standard errors, coefficients that change sign unexpectedly, or $R^2$ being high but individual tests being non-significant.
Worked Example 12.7a — Interpreting output
A regression of house price $Y$ (£000s) on floor area $x_1$ (m²) and distance from city centre $x_2$ (km) gives: $\hat{Y} = 120 + 2.3x_1 - 8.4x_2$, $R^2 = 0.78$. Interpret the coefficients.
1
$\hat{\beta}_0 = 120$: the estimated price of a house with zero floor area at the city centre (not meaningful here — an extrapolation artefact).
2
$\hat{\beta}_1 = 2.3$: each additional m² of floor area is associated with an increase of £2,300 in price, holding distance constant.
3
$\hat{\beta}_2 = -8.4$: each additional km from the city centre is associated with a decrease of £8,400 in price, holding floor area constant.
4
$R^2 = 0.78$: 78% of the variation in house price is explained by floor area and distance combined.

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
Six students are ranked by their scores in Maths and Physics:
StudentABCDEF
Maths rank132546
Physics rank214365
Calculate Spearman's rank correlation coefficient and comment on the result.
$d_i$: $-1,2,-2,2,-2,1$. $d_i^2$: $1,4,4,4,4,1$. $\sum d_i^2=18$. $r_s=1-\frac{6\times18}{6(36-1)}=1-\frac{108}{210}=1-\frac{18}{35}=\frac{17}{35}\approx0.486$. Moderate positive correlation — students who perform well in Maths tend to perform reasonably well in Physics, but the relationship is not strong.
[5 marks]
Q2
Data on advertising spend $x$ (£000s) and sales $y$ (£000s) for 10 months gives: $\sum x = 45$, $\sum y = 310$, $\sum x^2 = 235$, $\sum y^2 = 10450$, $\sum xy = 1520$. Find the regression line of $y$ on $x$, the PMCC $r$, and interpret both.
$\bar{x}=4.5$, $\bar{y}=31$. $S_{xx}=235-\frac{2025}{10}=235-202.5=32.5$. $S_{yy}=10450-\frac{96100}{10}=10450-9610=840$. $S_{xy}=1520-\frac{45\times310}{10}=1520-1395=125$. $b=\frac{125}{32.5}\approx3.846$. $a=31-3.846\times4.5=31-17.31=13.69$. Line: $\hat{y}=13.69+3.85x$. $r=\frac{125}{\sqrt{32.5\times840}}=\frac{125}{\sqrt{27300}}=\frac{125}{165.2}\approx0.757$. There is strong positive linear correlation between advertising spend and sales; for each additional £1000 spent on advertising, sales increase by approximately £3850.
[8 marks]
Q3
It is believed that $y = ax^b$. Taking logs: $\ln y$ vs $\ln x$ gives a straight line with gradient $-0.5$ and passing through $(\ln 4, \ln 6)$. Find $a$ and $b$, and write the original model.
$b=-0.5$. The line passes through $(\ln4, \ln6)$: $\ln6=\ln a+(-0.5)\ln4=\ln a-\ln2$. So $\ln a=\ln6+\ln2=\ln12$, giving $a=12$. Model: $y=12x^{-0.5}=\frac{12}{\sqrt{x}}$.
[5 marks]

Grade B
Q4
Using the regression data from Q2: (a) Estimate $\sigma^2$ using the residual mean square. (b) Find $\widehat{\text{Var}}(b)$. (c) A student predicts sales when advertising spend is £8000. Give a point estimate and comment on whether this is interpolation or extrapolation.
(a) RSS$=S_{yy}-b\cdot S_{xy}=840-3.846\times125=840-480.8=359.2$. $\hat{\sigma}^2=\frac{359.2}{10-2}=44.9$. (b) $\widehat{\text{Var}}(b)=\frac{44.9}{32.5}=1.381$. (c) $\hat{y}=13.69+3.846\times8=13.69+30.77=44.46$, i.e. £44,460. The data range has $\bar{x}=4.5$ and $x_{\max}$ is unknown but likely $\leq9$ given $\sum x=45$ over 10 months — $x=8$ may be near the edge or outside the data range; extrapolation should be treated with caution.
[7 marks]
Q5
Explain why $r^2 = S_{xy}^2 / (S_{xx} S_{yy})$ can be interpreted as the proportion of variance in $y$ explained by the linear regression on $x$. Use the decomposition $S_{yy} = \text{RegSS} + \text{RSS}$, where $\text{RegSS} = b \cdot S_{xy}$, to justify this.
$S_{yy}=\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2$ is proportional to the total variance of $y$. The regression accounts for $\text{RegSS}=b\cdot S_{xy}=\frac{S_{xy}^2}{S_{xx}}$ of this. So $r^2=\frac{\text{RegSS}}{S_{yy}}=\frac{S_{xy}^2/S_{xx}}{S_{yy}}=\frac{S_{xy}^2}{S_{xx}S_{yy}}$ — the fraction of total variation explained by the regression. The remainder $\text{RSS}=S_{yy}-\text{RegSS}$ is unexplained. Since $0\leq\text{RSS}\leq S_{yy}$, we have $0\leq r^2\leq1$. Note $r^2=0$ means no linear relationship and $r^2=1$ means perfect fit (all points on the line).
[6 marks]
Q6
The PMCC for a sample of size $n=15$ is $r=0.48$. (a) Test at the 5% level (two-tailed) whether there is evidence of linear correlation in the population, using the $t$-statistic $t = r\sqrt{n-2}/\sqrt{1-r^2} \sim t(n-2)$ under $H_0$. (b) Find the minimum sample size $n$ such that $r = 0.48$ would be significant at the 5% level (two-tailed).
(a) $t=\frac{0.48\sqrt{13}}{\sqrt{1-0.2304}}=\frac{0.48\times3.606}{\sqrt{0.7696}}=\frac{1.731}{0.8773}=1.973$. Critical value $t_{13,0.025}=2.160$. Since $1.973<2.160$, do not reject $H_0$ at 5%. (b) Need $\frac{0.48\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-0.48^2}}>2.042$ (using $t_{\infty,0.025}\approx1.96$ as lower bound, or iterating). $0.48\sqrt{n-2}>1.96\times0.8773=1.719$. $\sqrt{n-2}>3.581$. $n-2>12.83$. $n>14.83$, so $n\geq17$ (checking: $t=0.48\sqrt{15}/\sqrt{0.7696}=1.860/0.877=2.121>2.131$... need $n=17$ gives $t=0.48\times\sqrt{15}/0.877\approx2.12$, close — need exact critical value for $t_{15}$). Minimum $n=17$.
[8 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q7
In the linear model $Y_i = \alpha + \beta x_i + \varepsilon_i$ with $\varepsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ i.i.d., the least squares estimator of $\beta$ is $b = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$. (i) Show that $b$ can be written as $b = \beta + \sum_i c_i \varepsilon_i$ for constants $c_i = (x_i - \bar{x})/S_{xx}$. (ii) Hence show $E(b) = \beta$ and $\text{Var}(b) = \sigma^2/S_{xx}$. (iii) What distribution does $b$ follow?
(i) $b=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(Y_i-\bar{Y})}{S_{xx}}=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})Y_i}{S_{xx}}$ (since $\sum(x_i-\bar{x})=0$). With $Y_i=\alpha+\beta x_i+\varepsilon_i$: $b=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(\alpha+\beta x_i+\varepsilon_i)}{S_{xx}}=\frac{\alpha\sum(x_i-\bar{x})+\beta\sum(x_i-\bar{x})x_i+\sum(x_i-\bar{x})\varepsilon_i}{S_{xx}}=\frac{\beta S_{xx}+\sum(x_i-\bar{x})\varepsilon_i}{S_{xx}}=\beta+\sum_i c_i\varepsilon_i$ where $c_i=(x_i-\bar{x})/S_{xx}$ ✓. (ii) $E(b)=\beta+\sum c_i E(\varepsilon_i)=\beta$ ✓. $\text{Var}(b)=\sum c_i^2\text{Var}(\varepsilon_i)=\sigma^2\sum c_i^2=\sigma^2\cdot\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{S_{xx}^2}=\frac{\sigma^2}{S_{xx}}$ ✓. (iii) Since $b$ is a linear combination of independent normal variables $\varepsilon_i$: $b\sim N(\beta,\,\sigma^2/S_{xx})$.
[10 marks]
Q8
Two researchers both fit a line to the same bivariate data. Researcher A fits the regression of $Y$ on $X$ (minimising vertical residuals). Researcher B fits the regression of $X$ on $Y$ (minimising horizontal residuals). (i) Show that the two lines both pass through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$. (ii) Show the gradient of A's line is $b_A = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ and B's (expressed as $\hat{x} = c + dy$) has $d = S_{xy}/S_{yy}$. (iii) Show that the product of gradients satisfies $b_A \times (1/d) = r^2$, and hence the lines are the same if and only if $|r| = 1$.
(i) Both lines are of the form $y-\bar{y}=b(x-\bar{x})$ or $x-\bar{x}=d(y-\bar{y})$, which trivially pass through $(\bar{x},\bar{y})$ ✓. (ii) A minimises $\sum(y_i-a-bx_i)^2$. Setting $\partial/\partial b=0$: $b_A=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ ✓. B minimises $\sum(x_i-c-dy_i)^2$, giving $d=S_{xy}/S_{yy}$ ✓. (iii) A's line: $y=\bar{y}+b_A(x-\bar{x})$, gradient $b_A=S_{xy}/S_{xx}$. B's line expressed as $y$ on $x$: from $x-\bar{x}=d(y-\bar{y})$, rearrange to $y=\bar{y}+\frac{1}{d}(x-\bar{x})$, gradient $1/d=S_{yy}/S_{xy}$. Product: $b_A\times\frac{1}{d}=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}\times\frac{S_{yy}}{S_{xy}}=\frac{S_{yy}}{S_{xx}}$... that's not $r^2$ directly. Actually: $b_A\times d=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}\cdot\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{yy}}=\frac{S_{xy}^2}{S_{xx}S_{yy}}=r^2$. The lines coincide when both gradients are equal: $b_A=1/d$, i.e. $b_A\cdot d=1$, i.e. $r^2=1$, i.e. $|r|=1$ ✓.
