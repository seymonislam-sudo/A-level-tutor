16.1 Kinematics of Circular Motion

A particle moving in a circle of radius $r$ with angular velocity $\omega$ has linear speed $v = r\omega$. Its acceleration is directed towards the centre (centripetal) with magnitude:

$$a = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2 = v\omega$$ $$\text{Angular velocity: } \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \quad \text{rad s}^{-1} \qquad \text{Period: } T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi r}{v}$$

In vector form, if $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ points outward from the centre and $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is tangential:

$$\mathbf{a} = -r\omega^2\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\omega}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = -\frac{v^2}{r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \dot{v}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$

For uniform circular motion ($\omega$ constant, $\dot{v}=0$): only the centripetal (inward radial) component exists. For non-uniform motion, there is also a tangential component $\dot{v}$.

No work done by centripetal force The centripetal force is always perpendicular to the velocity (which is tangential), so it does no work. This means kinetic energy ‚Äî and speed ‚Äî can only change due to other forces with a tangential component (gravity, tangential thrust, etc.).

16.2 Horizontal Circular Motion

For a particle moving in a horizontal circle, the net inward (centripetal) force provides the centripetal acceleration. Resolve forces horizontally and vertically separately.

$$F_{\text{centripetal}} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2 \quad \text{(directed inward)}$$
Worked Example 16.2a ‚Äî Car on a banked track
A car of mass 1200 kg travels around a banked circular track of radius 80 m. The track is banked at angle $\alpha$ to the horizontal. Find the angle required for the car to travel at 20 m s‚Åª¬π without any sideways friction force.
1
With no friction, the only forces are weight $mg$ (down) and normal reaction $N$ (perpendicular to track). Resolving vertically: $N\cos\alpha = mg$. Resolving horizontally (centripetal): $N\sin\alpha = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}$.
2
Dividing: $\tan\alpha = \dfrac{v^2}{rg} = \dfrac{400}{80 \times 9.8} = \dfrac{400}{784} \approx 0.510$. So $\alpha = \arctan(0.510) \approx 27.0¬∞$.
Worked Example 16.2b ‚Äî Particle on a string
A particle of mass 0.3 kg is attached to a fixed point by a string of length 0.5 m and moves in a horizontal circle. The string makes angle 30¬∞ with the vertical. Find the tension, angular velocity, and period.
1
Radius of circle: $r = 0.5\sin30¬∞ = 0.25$ m. Resolving vertically: $T\cos30¬∞ = 0.3g$. $T = \dfrac{0.3 \times 9.8}{\cos30¬∞} = \dfrac{2.94}{0.866} = 3.394$ N.
2
Resolving horizontally: $T\sin30¬∞ = mr\omega^2$. $3.394 \times 0.5 = 0.3 \times 0.25 \times \omega^2$. $1.697 = 0.075\omega^2$. $\omega^2 = 22.63$. $\omega \approx 4.76$ rad s‚Åª¬π.
3
Period $T = \dfrac{2\pi}{\omega} = \dfrac{2\pi}{4.76} \approx 1.32$ s.

16.3 The Conical Pendulum

A conical pendulum consists of a particle on a string of length $l$ attached to a fixed point, rotating so the string makes a constant angle $\alpha$ with the vertical. The particle traces a horizontal circle of radius $r = l\sin\alpha$.

$$\tan\alpha = \frac{\omega^2 r}{g} = \frac{\omega^2 l\sin\alpha}{g} \implies \cos\alpha = \frac{g}{\omega^2 l}$$ $$T_{\text{period}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l\cos\alpha}{g}}$$
Increasing $\omega$ increases $\alpha$ As the angular velocity increases, the particle rises (angle $\alpha$ increases, $\cos\alpha$ decreases). There is a minimum $\omega$ to maintain any given angle: $\omega_{\min} = \sqrt{g/(l\cos\alpha)}$. As $\omega \to \infty$, $\alpha \to 90¬∞$ (particle approaches the horizontal), but this requires infinite tension.
Worked Example 16.3a
A conical pendulum has string length 0.8 m. Find (a) the period when $\alpha = 60¬∞$, (b) the angular velocity needed to make $\alpha = 45¬∞$, (c) whether $\alpha = 90¬∞$ is achievable.
1
(a) $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.8\cos60¬∞}{9.8}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.4}{9.8}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0408} = 2\pi(0.2020) \approx 1.27$ s.
2
(b) $\cos45¬∞ = \dfrac{g}{\omega^2 l}$. $\omega^2 = \dfrac{9.8}{0.8\cos45¬∞} = \dfrac{9.8}{0.566} = 17.31$. $\omega \approx 4.16$ rad s‚Åª¬π.
3
(c) $\cos90¬∞ = 0$, requiring $\omega^2 l = g/0 \to \infty$ ‚Äî impossible. $\alpha = 90¬∞$ cannot be achieved with finite $\omega$.

16.4 Vertical Circular Motion

For a particle moving in a vertical circle of radius $r$, the speed varies with position (gravity does work). At angle $\theta$ from the bottom, measured from centre:

$$v^2 = v_{\text{bottom}}^2 - 2gr(1 - \cos\theta) \quad \text{(by energy conservation, measuring height from bottom)}$$

At any position, resolve forces along the radial direction (towards centre = positive centripetal direction):

$$T - mg\cos\phi = \frac{mv^2}{r} \quad \text{(string/rod, at angle }\phi\text{ from vertical above centre)}$$

where $\phi$ is the angle from the vertically-upward position (so $\phi = 0$ at top, $\phi = \pi$ at bottom). The component of gravity toward the centre is $mg\cos\phi$ (positive when above centre, negative below).

Standard angle convention It's clearest to measure $\theta$ from the lowest point. At angle $\theta$ from the bottom, the height above the bottom is $r(1-\cos\theta)$. At the top, $\theta = \pi$, height $= 2r$. The centripetal equation at general $\theta$: the component of gravity toward centre is $-g\cos\theta$ (gravity pulls downward; toward centre from $\theta = 0$ to $\theta = \pi/2$ has a centripetal component of $+g\cos(\pi-\theta) = -g\cos\theta$ when $\theta > \pi/2$)... it's easiest to draw a diagram and resolve case by case.
Worked Example 16.4a ‚Äî Speed and tension at top of circle
A particle of mass 0.5 kg moves on the inside of a smooth circular track of radius 1.5 m in a vertical plane. At the bottom its speed is 7 m s‚Åª¬π. Find the speed and normal reaction at the top.
1
Height from bottom to top $= 2r = 3$ m. Energy: $\frac{1}{2}mv_T^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_B^2 - mg(2r)$. $v_T^2 = 49 - 2(9.8)(3) = 49 - 58.8 = -9.8$ ‚Äî negative!
2
Since $v_T^2 < 0$ is impossible, the particle does not reach the top with this speed. It leaves the track before reaching the top.
The minimum speed at the bottom to complete the circle on the inside of a track is found by requiring $v_T^2 \geq 0$: $v_B^2 \geq 4gr = 58.8$, so $v_B \geq 7.67$ m s‚Åª¬π.

16.5 Completing a Vertical Circle

The conditions for completing a full vertical circle depend on whether the particle is on a string, a rod, or the inside/outside of a surface.

String: tension ‚â• 0 at top A string can only pull. At the top, centripetal force = $T + mg = mv_T^2/r$. For the string to remain taut: $T \geq 0$, so $mv_T^2/r \geq mg$, giving $v_T^2 \geq gr$. Using energy from the bottom: $v_B^2 \geq 5gr$. Minimum speed at bottom: $v_B = \sqrt{5gr}$.
Rod: no minimum speed at top needed A rod can push or pull, so $T$ can be negative (thrust). No condition on $v_T$ ‚Äî the particle can have zero speed at the top and still be in circular motion (the rod provides an upward thrust). Minimum speed at bottom: $v_B = \sqrt{4gr}$ (for $v_T = 0$).
Outside a sphere: normal reaction ‚â• 0 The surface can only push (normal reaction $N \geq 0$). At the top outside: $mg - N = mv^2/r$, so $N = m(g - v^2/r) \geq 0$ requires $v^2 \leq gr$. But for the particle to reach the top without leaving the sphere earlier, we need to find where $N = 0$ ‚Äî this is the point of leaving (see 16.6).
$$\text{String: } v_B \geq \sqrt{5gr} \qquad \text{Rod: } v_B \geq \sqrt{4gr} \qquad \text{Inside surface: } v_B \geq \sqrt{5gr}$$
Worked Example 16.5a ‚Äî Minimum speed on a string
A particle on a string of length 0.6 m moves in a vertical circle. Find (a) the minimum speed at the bottom to complete the circle, (b) the tension at the bottom when moving at this minimum speed.
1
(a) Minimum speed at bottom: $v_B = \sqrt{5gr} = \sqrt{5 \times 9.8 \times 0.6} = \sqrt{29.4} \approx 5.42$ m s‚Åª¬π.
2
(b) At the bottom, centripetal force is upward. $T - mg = mv_B^2/r$. $T = m(g + v_B^2/r) = m(9.8 + 29.4/0.6) = m(9.8 + 49) = 58.8m$. For $m = 1$ kg: $T = 58.8$ N. In general $T = 6mg$ at the bottom when $v_B = \sqrt{5gr}$.
This is a standard result: at minimum speed, $T_{\text{bottom}} = 6mg$ and $T_{\text{top}} = 0$.

16.6 Leaving a Circular Path

A particle leaves a circular surface when the normal reaction becomes zero ($N = 0$). Set $N = 0$ in the radial equation to find the position and speed at which the particle leaves.

Worked Example 16.6a ‚Äî Particle sliding off a sphere
A smooth sphere of radius $R$ sits on a horizontal surface. A particle is placed at the top with a tiny nudge and slides off. Find the angle $\theta$ from the vertical at which it leaves the sphere.
1
At angle $\theta$ from vertical, height below top $= R - R\cos\theta = R(1-\cos\theta)$. Energy: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgR(1-\cos\theta)$, so $v^2 = 2gR(1-\cos\theta)$.
2
Radial equation (inward positive, along radius toward centre): $mg\cos\theta - N = \dfrac{mv^2}{R}$.
3
At the point of leaving: $N = 0$. $mg\cos\theta = \dfrac{mv^2}{R} = \dfrac{m \cdot 2gR(1-\cos\theta)}{R} = 2mg(1-\cos\theta)$.
4
$\cos\theta = 2 - 2\cos\theta \Rightarrow 3\cos\theta = 2 \Rightarrow \cos\theta = \dfrac{2}{3} \Rightarrow \theta \approx 48.2¬∞$.
This is a classic result ‚Äî independent of $R$ or $m$. The particle always leaves at $\cos\theta = 2/3$ above the equator.
Worked Example 16.6b ‚Äî Particle on inside of circular loop
A particle moves on the inside of a smooth circular loop (radius 2 m) in a vertical plane. It starts from rest at the same height as the centre. Find where it leaves the loop (if it does) and what happens next.
1
Let $\theta$ = angle from bottom. Starting height = centre height = $r$ above bottom, so $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mgr(1-\cos\theta) - mg \cdot r \cdot (1) + mg \cdot r = mg \cdot r(1-\cos\theta)$... Let's use energy from the start. At angle $\theta$, height above start $= r\cos\theta - 0$... Let me set up clearly: start at height 0 (centre level), angle from bottom: start position is $\theta_0 = 90¬∞$ (at the side). At angle $\theta$ from bottom: height = $-r\cos\theta$ from centre = $r - r\cos\theta$ from bottom. Start height from bottom = $r$. $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = mg(r - r(1-\cos\theta+...))$. Energy from start: $v^2 = 2g(r - r + r\cos\theta - r\cos90¬∞) = 2gr\cos\theta$. So $v^2 = 2gr\cos\theta$.
2
Radial equation (inside of loop, centripetal = inward): $N - mg\cos(\pi - \theta)_{\text{component}} $... At angle $\theta$ from bottom, component of gravity toward centre = $-g\cos\theta$ for $\theta < 90¬∞$ (gravity away from centre for upper half). Let's use $\phi$ = angle from top: $N + mg\cos\phi = mv^2/r$ where $v^2 = 2gr\sin\phi$ (measured from top). At leaving: $N=0$: $mg\cos\phi = mv^2/r = 2mg\sin\phi \Rightarrow \cos\phi = 2\sin\phi \Rightarrow \tan\phi = 1/2$. So $\phi = \arctan(1/2) \approx 26.6¬∞$ from top on the lower half ‚Äî which means the particle leaves in the upper part of the loop, having not completed the circle.

16.7 General Motion in a Circle

For non-uniform circular motion, there are two components of acceleration: centripetal (inward, $v^2/r$) and tangential ($\dot{v} = dv/dt$). Both must be included when the speed is changing.

$$\text{Centripetal: } a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} \quad \text{(inward)} \qquad \text{Tangential: } a_t = \frac{dv}{dt} \quad \text{(along motion)}$$

Using $v = r\omega$ and $\omega = \dot{\theta}$, the equations in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ for a particle constrained to a circle give:

$$\text{Radial (inward):} \quad F_r = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\dot{\theta}^2$$ $$\text{Tangential:} \quad F_t = m\dot{v} = mr\ddot{\theta}$$
Worked Example 16.7a ‚Äî Non-uniform circular motion
A particle of mass 0.2 kg moves on a smooth circular wire of radius 0.5 m in a vertical plane. At angle $\theta$ from the bottom, $v = 3\cos\theta$ m s‚Åª¬π. Find the normal force from the wire and the tangential force at $\theta = 0$ (bottom) and $\theta = \pi/3$.
1
At $\theta = 0$ (bottom): $v = 3$ m s‚Åª¬π. Centripetal: $N - mg = mv^2/r$. $N = m(g + v^2/r) = 0.2(9.8 + 18) = 0.2(27.8) = 5.56$ N.
2
Tangential acceleration: $\dot{v} = \frac{dv}{dt} = -3\sin\theta \cdot \dot{\theta} = -3\sin\theta \cdot v/r = -3\sin\theta \cdot 3\cos\theta/0.5 = -18\sin\theta\cos\theta = -9\sin(2\theta)$. At $\theta = 0$: $\dot{v} = 0$. Tangential force $= 0$.
3
At $\theta = \pi/3$: $v = 3\cos60¬∞ = 1.5$ m s‚Åª¬π. Component of gravity toward centre $= -mg\cos\theta = -0.2(9.8)(0.5) = -0.98$ N (away from centre since $\theta < \pi/2$ means we're in lower half). $N + mg\cos(\pi - \theta)\cdot\text{sign}$... Resolve carefully: gravity downward, centripetal direction is toward centre (upward at bottom, inward at side). At $\theta = 60¬∞$ from bottom, the radius points at $30¬∞$ above horizontal. Centripetal direction: inward. Component of gravity centripetally $= -mg\cos\theta = -mg\cos60¬∞$. $N - mg\cos60¬∞ = mv^2/r$. $N = m(v^2/r + g\cos60¬∞) = 0.2(4.5 + 4.9) = 0.2(9.4) = 1.88$ N. Tangential force: $mg\sin60¬∞ - F_t = m\dot{v}$ at this $\theta$. Hmm ‚Äî tangential direction is perpendicular to radius. The component of gravity tangentially $= mg\sin\theta$ (opposing motion going up). $m\dot{v} = -mg\sin\theta + F_t$. $\dot{v} = -9\sin(2\times60¬∞) = -9\sin120¬∞ = -9(\sqrt3/2) = -7.79$ m s‚Åª¬≤. $F_t = m(\dot{v} + g\sin\theta) = 0.2(-7.79 + 8.49) = 0.2(0.70) = 0.14$ N.

A particle of mass $m$ is on the inside of a smooth sphere of radius $R$ and moves in a horizontal circle at height $h$ below the centre. (a) Find the normal reaction $N$ and speed $v$ in terms of $m$, $R$, $h$, $g$. (b) Show that $N > mg$ always. (c) Find the period of revolution.
Let $\alpha$ = angle the radius to the particle makes with the downward vertical. Then $h = R\cos\alpha$ (below centre), radius of circle $= R\sin\alpha$. Resolving vertically: $N\cos\alpha=mg\Rightarrow N=mg/\cos\alpha=mgR/h$. Since $h < R$, $N = mgR/h > mg$ ‚úì. Horizontally: $N\sin\alpha=mv^2/(R\sin\alpha)\Rightarrow v^2=N R\sin^2\alpha/m=gR^2\sin^2\alpha/h=g(R^2-h^2)/h$ (using $R^2\sin^2\alpha=R^2-R^2\cos^2\alpha=R^2-h^2$). Period $=2\pi R\sin\alpha/v=2\pi\sqrt{R\sin^2\alpha\cdot h/(g(R^2-h^2))}$... more cleanly: $T=2\pi(R\sin\alpha)/v=2\pi\sqrt{h/g}$ (remarkably, same as a pendulum of length $h$).

Q8
A particle of mass $m$ slides from rest at the top of a smooth hemisphere of radius $R$ fixed on a horizontal surface. (i) Find the speed and normal reaction at angle $\theta$ from the vertical. (ii) Find the angle at which it leaves the hemisphere. (iii) After leaving the hemisphere, show it follows projectile motion and find where it lands on the horizontal surface. (iv) At what angle does it hit the ground?
(i) Height descended $= R - R\cos\theta = R(1-\cos\theta)$. Energy: $v^2 = 2gR(1-\cos\theta)$. Radial: $mg\cos\theta - N = mv^2/R$. $N = mg\cos\theta - 2mg(1-\cos\theta) = mg(3\cos\theta - 2)$. (ii) Leaves when $N=0$: $3\cos\theta = 2\Rightarrow\cos\theta_0 = 2/3$. Height above ground at leaving: $R\cos\theta_0 = 2R/3$. Speed at leaving: $v_0^2 = 2gR(1-2/3) = 2gR/3$. Direction: tangential to sphere, i.e. at angle $\theta_0$ below horizontal (since it's sliding down the side). Velocity components: $v_x = v_0\sin\theta_0$, $v_y = -v_0\cos\theta_0$ (taking up as positive). $\sin\theta_0 = \sqrt{1-4/9} = \sqrt{5}/3$. $v_x = v_0\sqrt{5}/3$, $v_y = -v_0(2/3) = -2v_0/3$. (iii) After leaving at height $h_0 = 2R/3$, horizontal position from centre $= R\sin\theta_0 = R\sqrt5/3$. Projectile: $y = h_0 + v_y t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$. Lands when $y=0$: $\frac{1}{2}gt^2 - (-2v_0/3)t - 2R/3 = 0$. With $v_0 = \sqrt{2gR/3}$: $\frac{1}{2}gt^2 + \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{\frac{2gR}{3}}\cdot t - \frac{2R}{3} = 0$. Solving gives $t$ and then horizontal distance. (iv) At landing, $v_x = v_0\sqrt{5}/3$ (unchanged), $v_y = v_y^{\text{initial}} - gt = $ (computed from $t$). Angle with horizontal = $\arctan(|v_y|/v_x)$ at that time.
