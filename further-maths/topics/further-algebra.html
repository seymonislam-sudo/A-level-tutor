3.1 Roots of Polynomials

Every polynomial of degree $n$ has exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$ (counting multiplicity) — this is the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. For polynomials with real coefficients, complex roots always appear in conjugate pairs.

$$f(x) = a_n(x - \alpha_1)(x - \alpha_2)\cdots(x - \alpha_n)$$
Key Fact If a polynomial with rational coefficients has an irrational root $a + b\sqrt{c}$, then $a - b\sqrt{c}$ is also a root. Combined with the conjugate root theorem, polynomials with rational coefficients always have roots that come in pairs — or are rational themselves.

3.2 Vieta's Formulae

Vieta's formulae relate the roots of a polynomial directly to its coefficients — without needing to solve it.

Quadratic: $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, roots $\alpha, \beta$

$$\alpha + \beta = -\frac{b}{a} \qquad \alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a}$$

Cubic: $ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d = 0$, roots $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$

$$\alpha+\beta+\gamma = -\frac{b}{a} \qquad \alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha = \frac{c}{a} \qquad \alpha\beta\gamma = -\frac{d}{a}$$

Quartic: $ax^4+bx^3+cx^2+dx+e=0$, roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta$

$$\sum\alpha = -\frac{b}{a} \qquad \sum\alpha\beta = \frac{c}{a} \qquad \sum\alpha\beta\gamma = -\frac{d}{a} \qquad \alpha\beta\gamma\delta = \frac{e}{a}$$
Memory Pattern Signs alternate: $-b/a,\ +c/a,\ -d/a,\ +e/a$. The symmetric functions go: sum of roots, pairs, triples, all four.
Worked Example 3.2a
The cubic $2x^3 - 5x^2 + x + 3 = 0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Without solving, find (a) $\alpha+\beta+\gamma$, (b) $\alpha\beta\gamma$, (c) $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2$.
1
(a) $\alpha+\beta+\gamma = \dfrac{5}{2}$    (b) $\alpha\beta\gamma = -\dfrac{3}{2}$
2
For (c), use the identity $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2 = (\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha)$
3
$= \left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)^2 - 2\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right) = \dfrac{25}{4} - 1 = \dfrac{21}{4}$

Useful Identities

$$\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2 = (\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2 - 2(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha)$$ $$\alpha^3+\beta^3+\gamma^3 = (\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^3 - 3(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha) + 3\alpha\beta\gamma$$

3.3 New Equations from Old Roots

Given a polynomial with roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$, find a new polynomial whose roots are some function of the originals — for example $\alpha+2$ or $1/\alpha$. The method is substitution: if the new roots are $w = f(\alpha)$, express $\alpha = f^{-1}(w)$ and substitute into the original equation.

Worked Example 3.3a
The equation $x^3 - 6x^2 + 3x + 1 = 0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Find the equation with roots $\alpha+2, \beta+2, \gamma+2$.
1
Let $w = x+2$, so $x = w-2$. Substitute into the original equation.
2
$(w-2)^3 - 6(w-2)^2 + 3(w-2) + 1 = 0$
3
Expand: $w^3-6w^2+12w-8\ -\ 6w^2+24w-24\ +\ 3w-6+1 = 0$
4
$w^3 - 12w^2 + 39w - 37 = 0$
Worked Example 3.3b
The equation $x^3 + 3x - 2 = 0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Find the equation with roots $1/\alpha, 1/\beta, 1/\gamma$.
1
Let $w = 1/x$, so $x = 1/w$. Substitute: $\dfrac{1}{w^3} + \dfrac{3}{w} - 2 = 0$
2
Multiply by $w^3$: $1 + 3w^2 - 2w^3 = 0$, i.e. $2x^3 - 3x^2 - 1 = 0$
The coefficients reverse — this always happens for reciprocal roots.

3.4 Rational Inequalities

⚠ Never multiply by a variable expression You don't know the sign of $g(x)$, so multiplying could flip the inequality. Instead, rearrange to get 0 on one side and use a sign diagram.
Worked Example 3.4a
Solve $\dfrac{x+3}{x-1} > 2$.
1
Rearrange: $\dfrac{x+3}{x-1} - 2 > 0 \implies \dfrac{-x+5}{x-1} > 0$
2
Critical values: $x=1$ (denominator) and $x=5$ (numerator).
3
Sign diagram: fraction $> 0$ only when $1 < x < 5$.
Test $x=3$: $\frac{-3+5}{3-1} = 1 > 0$ ✓
Worked Example 3.4b
Solve $\dfrac{x^2-1}{x+3} \leq 0$.
1
Factorise: $\dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x+3} \leq 0$. Critical values: $x = -3, -1, 1$.
2
Sign diagram (testing each interval): negative for $x < -3$ and $-1 \leq x \leq 1$.
3
Solution: $x < -3$ or $-1 \leq x \leq 1$
Include $x=\pm1$ (expression $=0$), exclude $x=-3$ (undefined).

3.5 Modulus Inequalities

$$|f(x)| < a \iff -a < f(x) < a$$ $$|f(x)| > a \iff f(x) < -a \text{ or } f(x) > a$$ $$|f(x)| < |g(x)| \iff f(x)^2 < g(x)^2$$
Worked Example 3.5a
Solve $|2x-3| < |x+1|$.
1
Square both sides: $(2x-3)^2 < (x+1)^2$
2
$(2x-3)^2-(x+1)^2 < 0 \implies [(2x-3)-(x+1)][(2x-3)+(x+1)] < 0 \implies (x-4)(3x-2) < 0$
3
Solution: $\dfrac{2}{3} < x < 4$

3.6 Summation of Series

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} r = \frac{1}{2}n(n+1) \qquad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^2 = \frac{1}{6}n(n+1)(2n+1) \qquad \sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \frac{1}{4}n^2(n+1)^2$$
Beautiful Identity $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} r^3 = \left(\sum_{r=1}^{n} r\right)^2$ — the sum of cubes equals the square of the sum.
Worked Example 3.6a
Find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}(3r^2-2r+1)$ in fully factorised form.
1
$3\cdot\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} - 2\cdot\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} + n$
2
$= \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{2} - n(n+1) + n$
3
Factor out $n$: $= \dfrac{n(2n^2+n-1)}{2} = \dfrac{n(2n-1)(n+1)}{2}$

To find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=k}^{n}$, compute $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} - \sum_{r=1}^{k-1}$.

3.7 Method of Differences

If each term can be written as $f(r) - f(r+1)$, summing gives a telescoping series where almost everything cancels.

$$\sum_{r=1}^{n} [f(r) - f(r+1)] = f(1) - f(n+1)$$
Worked Example 3.7a
Show that $\dfrac{2}{r(r+2)} = \dfrac{1}{r} - \dfrac{1}{r+2}$ and hence find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} \dfrac{2}{r(r+2)}$.
1
Partial fractions: $A=1$, $B=-1$ gives $\frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r+2}$ ✓
2
Write out terms: $\left(\frac{1}{1}-\frac{1}{3}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{4}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{5}\right)+\cdots$
3
Surviving terms: $S_n = 1 + \dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{n+1} - \dfrac{1}{n+2}$
The first two positive and last two negative terms survive.
4
$S_n = \dfrac{3}{2} - \dfrac{2n+3}{(n+1)(n+2)}$
Exam Tip Always write out the first few and last few terms explicitly and show the cancellation clearly. Examiners want to see the telescoping.

Practice Questions

Section A — Foundation
Grade A
Q1
The cubic $x^3-4x^2+x+6=0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Find (a) $\alpha+\beta+\gamma$, (b) $\alpha\beta\gamma$, (c) $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2$, (d) $\frac{1}{\alpha}+\frac{1}{\beta}+\frac{1}{\gamma}$.
(a) $4$. (b) $-6$. (c) $(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2-2(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha)=16-2(1)=14$. (d) $\frac{\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha}{\alpha\beta\gamma}=\frac{1}{-6}=-\frac{1}{6}$.
[6 marks]
Q2
The equation $x^3-3x+2=0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. Find the cubic equation with roots $2\alpha,2\beta,2\gamma$.
Let $w=2x$, $x=w/2$: $(w/2)^3-3(w/2)+2=0 \Rightarrow w^3/8-3w/2+2=0$. Multiply by 8: $x^3-12x+16=0$.
[4 marks]
Q3
Solve the inequality $\dfrac{2x+1}{x-3} \leq 1$.
$\frac{2x+1}{x-3}-1\leq0 \Rightarrow \frac{x+4}{x-3}\leq0$. Critical values $x=-4$, $x=3$. Solution: $-4\leq x<3$.
[4 marks]
Q4
Find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} r(r+3)$ in fully factorised form.
$\sum r^2+3\sum r=\frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}+\frac{3n(n+1)}{2}=\frac{n(n+1)}{6}(2n+1+9)=\frac{n(n+1)(2n+10)}{6}=\frac{n(n+1)(n+5)}{3}$.
[4 marks]
Q5
Show that $\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}=\dfrac{1}{r}-\dfrac{1}{r+1}$ and hence find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}\dfrac{1}{r(r+1)}$. What does this tend to as $n\to\infty$?
Telescoping gives $S_n=1-\frac{1}{n+1}=\frac{n}{n+1}$. As $n\to\infty$, $S_n\to 1$.
[5 marks]
Section B — Challenging
Grade B
Q6
The equation $x^3+px^2+qx+r=0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ where $\alpha+\beta+\gamma=6$, $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2=14$, and $\alpha^3+\beta^3+\gamma^3=36$. Find $p$, $q$, and $r$.
$p=-(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)=-6$. $\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha=\frac{(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^2-(\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2)}{2}=\frac{36-14}{2}=11$, so $q=11$. For $r$: use $\alpha^3+\beta^3+\gamma^3=(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)^3-3(\alpha+\beta+\gamma)(\alpha\beta+\beta\gamma+\gamma\alpha)+3\alpha\beta\gamma$: $36=216-3(6)(11)+3\alpha\beta\gamma \Rightarrow 36=216-198+3\alpha\beta\gamma \Rightarrow \alpha\beta\gamma=6$, so $r=-6$.
[7 marks]
Q7
Solve $\dfrac{x^2+2x}{x^2-4}>1$, giving your answer in set notation.
$\frac{x^2+2x-(x^2-4)}{x^2-4}>0 \Rightarrow \frac{2x+4}{(x-2)(x+2)}>0 \Rightarrow \frac{2(x+2)}{(x-2)(x+2)}>0 \Rightarrow \frac{2}{x-2}>0$ (for $x\neq-2$). So $x>2$. Answer: $\{x\in\mathbb{R}:x>2\}$.
[6 marks]
Q8
Use the method of differences to find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}\dfrac{4r+2}{r(r+1)(r+2)}$. (Hint: use partial fractions first.)
Partial fractions: $\frac{1}{r}-\frac{2}{r+1}+\frac{1}{r+2}=\left(\frac{1}{r}-\frac{1}{r+1}\right)-\left(\frac{1}{r+1}-\frac{1}{r+2}\right)$. Two telescoping sums give $S_n=\left(1-\frac{1}{n+1}\right)-\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{n+2}\right)=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{(n+1)(n+2)}$.
[7 marks]
Section C — STEP-style
Hard
Q9
The depressed cubic $x^3+px+q=0$ has roots $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$. (i) Show $\alpha^2+\beta^2+\gamma^2=-2p$. (ii) Show $\alpha^3+\beta^3+\gamma^3=-3q$. (iii) Hence find $\alpha^4+\beta^4+\gamma^4$ in terms of $p$ and $q$.
Vieta's: $e_1=0$, $e_2=p$, $e_3=-q$. (i) $\sum\alpha^2=e_1^2-2e_2=-2p$ ✓. (ii) $\sum\alpha^3=e_1^3-3e_1e_2+3e_3=-3q$ ✓. (iii) $\sum\alpha^4=(\sum\alpha^2)^2-2\sum\alpha^2\beta^2=4p^2-2(e_2^2-2e_1e_3)=4p^2-2p^2=2p^2$.
[9 marks]
Q10
Prove by induction that $\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n}r\cdot r!=(n+1)!-1$. Hence find $\displaystyle\sum_{r=2}^{n}r\cdot(r-1)!$.
Base: $n=1$: LHS$=1$, RHS$=2!-1=1$ ✓. Step: assume true for $k$. Add $(k+1)(k+1)!$: sum $=(k+1)!-1+(k+1)(k+1)!=(k+1)!(1+k+1)-1=(k+2)!-1$ ✓. For second sum: $\sum_{r=2}^{n}r(r-1)!=\sum_{r=2}^{n}r!=(n+1)!-1-1!=(n+1)!-2$.
[8 marks]
